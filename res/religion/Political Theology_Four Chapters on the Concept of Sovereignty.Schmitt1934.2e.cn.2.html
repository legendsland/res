1. Definition of Sovereignty

Sovereign is he who decides on the exception.

626

This definition alone can do justice to the concept of sovereignty as a limiting concept. Limiting concept does not mean a vague concept, as in the inexact terminology of popular literature, but rather a concept of the outermost sphere. Accordingly, this definition cannot be associated with the general case, but rather with a borderline case. It will become clear from the following that the state of exception must be understood as a general concept in the theory of state, not just a result of any emergency decree or state of siege. That the state of exception is eminently suitable for the juristic definition of sovereignty has a systematic, legally logical rationale. A decision regarding the exception is a decision in the eminent sense of the term, for a general norm, as represented by a normal legal proposition, can never encompass a total exception, and therefore also cannot completely justify the decision that a true state of exception exists. When Robert von Mohl (Monographien, p. 626) says that the test of whether an emergency exists cannot be a juristic one, he assumes the premise that a decision in the legal sense must derive completely from the content of a norm. But that is the question. In the general sense in which Mohl articulates the proposition, it is only an expression of constitutional liberalism, and underestimates the independent significance of the decision itself.

The abstract scheme established as the definition of sovereignty (that it is the highest power, not a derived power) can be accepted or not, without making a great practical or theoretical difference. About a concept in itself there will generally not be much argument, least of all in the history of sovereignty. One argues about the concrete application, and that means who decides what constitutes the public or state interests, public safety, and order, le salut public, et cetera in case of a conflict. The state of exception, which is not circumscribed in the existing legal order, can at best be designated as a case of the most extreme emergency, a threat to the existence of the state or the like, but can not be circumscribed matter-of-factly. This is precisely what makes relevant the question of the subject of sovereignty, that is the question of sovereignty overall. It can neither be stated with subsumable clarity when an emergency will exist, nor can it be substantially enumerated what might happen in such a case when it truly comes to an extreme emergency and its elimination. Precondition, as well as content of competence, are here necessarily unlimited. In the sense of the constitutional state, there is no competence at all. At best, the constitution can state who can act in such a case. If this action is not subject to any controls, distributed in some way in a system of checks and balances, as in the case of a liberal constitution, then it will quickly become clear who the sovereign is. He will decide immediately whether an extreme emergency exists, as well as what should be done to eliminate it. He will stand outside of the normally valid legal system and yet belong to it, for he is responsible for the decision as to whether the constitution can be suspended entirely. All tendencies in the development of the modern constitutional state are towards eliminating the sovereign in this sense. Therein lie the consequences of the ideas of Krabbe and Kelsen, which will be dealt with in the following chapter. But the question of whether the extreme state of exception can be eliminated of is not a juristic one. Whether one holds the belief and the hope that it can be eliminated depends on philosophical, especially historic-philosophical or metaphysical convictions.

There are historical depictions of the development of the concept of sovereignty, but they settle for the assembly of abstract formulae in which, as in a textbook, the definitions of sovereignty are contained. None seem to have made the effort to more closely examine the endlessly repeated, completely empty clichs used to refer to the highest power among the famous authors of the concept of sovereignty. That this concept is orientated toward the critical case, that is, the exception, was already apparent in Bodin. More than with his oft-cited definition (la souverainet est la puissance absolute et perptuelle d'une Rpublique sovereignty is the absolute and perpetual power of a republic), he marked the beginning of the modern theory of state with his work Of the True Marks of Sovereignty (Chapter 10 of the first book of Republic). He discussed his concept using many practical examples and always returned to the same question: to what extent is the sovereign bound to laws, and responsible to the estates? To this last, especially important question, Bodin replies that commitments are binding because the obligatory strength of a commitment rests on natural law; in the case of emergent the bond to general natural principles ceases. Generally, he says, the ruler is responsible to the estates or the people only so long as the fulfillment of his commitment is in the interest of the people, but that he is not so bound si la ncessit est urgente (if the necessity is urgent). The deciding factor in Bodin's proposition is that he brings the discussion of the relationship between ruler and estates to a simple either-or, and indeed this is because he refers to the emergency. This was the truly impressive aspect of his definition, which considered sovereignty to be an indivisible unit, and finally settled the question of power in the state. His scholarly achievement and the reason for his success lie in his to incorporation of the decision into the concept of sovereignty.

selon l'exigence des cas, des temps, et des personnes

Today there is hardly any discussion of the concept of sovereignty in which the usual quotation from Bodin does not feature, but nowhere does one find the core quote from the core of that chapter cited. Bodin asks whether the promises that the ruler gives to the estates or the people override his sovereignty. He answers by referring to the case when it becomes necessary to counteract such promises, to amend or completely override the laws, selon l'exigence des cas, des temps, et des personnes according to the demands of the case, the laws, and the people). If in such a case the ruler must first ask permission of a senate or the people, he must be prepared to let his subjects dispense with him. This seems to Bodin an absurdity, for he holds that since the estates are also not masters over the law, they would in turn have to allow their ruler to dispense with them, and so sovereignty would become joue deux parties (a play between two parties): sometimes the people would be master, sometimes the ruler, and that is contrary to all reason and justice. That is why the authority to suspend normally valid law be this in general or in a particular case is so much the true mark of sovereignty that Bodin derives all other features from it: declaration of war or peace agreement, appointment of officials, last resort, right to grant pardon, and so on.

192117 bellum omnium contra omnes

In my book on dictatorship (Die Diktatur, Munich and Leipzig, 1921) I showed, in contrast to the traditional schema of historical representations, that even among the authors of natural law in the 17th century, the question of sovereignty was understood as the question as to the decision on the state of exception. This goes especially for Samuel von Pufendorf. Everyone agrees that when antagonisms appear within a state, each party natural only wants the general good therein consists indeed the bellum omnium contra omnes (the war of all against all) but that sovereignty, and thereby the state itself, consists in deciding this controversy, that is, in determining definitively was public order and security are, when they are disturbed, and so on. In concrete reality public order and security present themselves very differently, depending on whether some militaristic bureaucracy, a self-governing body controlled by a commercial spirit, or a radical party organization determines when there is order and security and when they are threatened or disturbed. After all, every state of order rests on a decision, and also the concept of legal order, which is thoughtlessly applied as something self-evident, contains within itself the contrast of the two different elements of the juristic. Even legal order, like every order, rests on a decision and not a norm.

16 extremus necessitatis casus1871

Whether only God is sovereign that is, the one who acts in the earthly reality as his uncontested representative, or the emperor, or the prince or the people are sovereign, that is, those who can uncontestably identify themselves with the people, the question is always aimed at the subject of sovereignty, that is, the application of the concept to a concrete situation. Since the 16th century, jurists who discuss the question of sovereignty take as a starting point a catalogue of sovereign capacities that assembles a range of decisive features of sovereignty, and in essence traces back to Bodin's oft-cited discussion. To be sovereign means to have these capacities. In the vague legal relationships of the old German Reich, the constitutional argumentation went that since one of the various features of sovereignty was undoubtedly present, the other, dubious features must also be present. The controversy always revolved around to whom authority should fall in a situation for which there were no positive provisions, perhaps a capitulation for example; in other words, who should be responsible for the case for which no responsibility has been anticipated? Put more familiarly, it was asked, who is supposed to have unlimited power for himself? Hence the discussion on the state of exception, the extremus necessitatis casus (case of extreme necessity). In the discussion on the so-called monarchical principle, this is repeated with the same legally logical structure. Here too it is always asked who decides on the capacities not outlined in the constitution; that is, who is responsible to act when the legal system gives no answer regarding jurisdiction. During the controversy as to whether the individual German states were sovereign after the constitution of 1871, the debate concerned an issue of far more minor political significance. Even so, the same pattern of argumentation can be recognized. The crucial point of Max Seydel's attempt to prove that the individual states were sovereign is not so much the concept of the derivability or non-derivability of the rights remaining to the individual states, but rather the claim that the jurisdiction of the Reich was circumscribed by the constitution, that is, fundamentally limited, while the jurisdiction of the individual states was fundamentally unlimited.

19194848481815144848

According to Article 48 of the German constitution of 1919, a state of exception is declared by the president of the Reich but is under control of the Reichstag, which can at any time demand its suspension. This regulation corresponds to the development and practice of the constitutional state, which, through a division of powers and mutual control, attempts to postpone the question of sovereignty as much as possible. Yet only the regulation of conditions for emergency powers corresponds to the liberal constitutional tendency, not the content of Article 48, which rather lends unlimited powers, and therefor if applied without controls it would lend sovereignty in the same way as the emergency powers Article 14 of the French Charter of 1815, which granted the Monarch complete sovereignty. If the individual states have no more independent jurisdiction to declare a state of exception according to the prevailing interpretation of Article 48, then they are not states at all. In Article 48 lies the true crux of the question as to whether the German states remain independent states or not.

If the emergency powers granted in a state of exception could be successfully circumscribed be it through mutual control, through term limits, or, as in the constitutional state's regulation of what constitutes a state of siege, through enumeration of extraordinary powers the question of sovereignty would be put aside to a large degree, but of course not eliminated. A jurisprudence oriented towards questions of day-to-day life and ordinary business has practically no interest in the concept of sovereignty. For such a jurisprudence, only the normal is recognizable, and everything else is a disturbance. Such a jurisprudence is bewildered when facing an extreme case, for not all extraordinary actions, not every police emergency measure or emergency decree is a true state of exception. Fundamentally unlimited authority appertains to this much more, meaning the suspension of the entire existing order. If this situation occurs, it becomes clear that the state persists while the law recedes. Because the state of exception is something entirely different from anarchy and chaos, order still exists in the juristic sense, even if the normal legal order does not. The existence of the state here proves that it supersedes the validity of the legal norm. The decision frees itself from any normative confines and becomes in the true sense absolute. In the state of exception, the state suspends law by virtue of its right to self-preservation, so to speak. The two elements of the concept of legal order come face to face and prove their conceptual independence. Whereas in the normal case the individual moment of decision can be kept to a minimum, in the state of exception the norm will be negated. Despite this, the state of exception remains accessible to jurisprudence because both elements, the norm and the decision, remain within the framework of the juristic.

It would be a gross distortion of the schematic disjunction between sociology and legal theory if one were to say the exception had no juristic significance and was thus sociology. The exception is the unsubsumable; it eludes general codification, yet simultaneously manifests a specifically juristic element the decision in absolute purity. The case of exception appears in its absolute form when a situation in which legal propositions can be valid must first be brought about. Every general norm requires a normal organization of living conditions to which it can be applied according to the facts of the situation, and which is subject to its regulations. The norm requires a homogenous medium. This factual normalcy is not merely a superficial presupposition that a jurist can ignore; rather, it pertains to its immanent validity. There is no norm that would be applicable to chaos. Order must be established so that a legal order makes sense. A normal situation must be achieved, and he is sovereign who decides whether this situation actually exists.

All law is situational law. The sovereign manages and guarantees the situation as a whole in its totality. He has the monopoly of this final decision. Therein lies the essence of state sovereignty, which is to be juristically defined not as a coercive or command monopoly, but rather as a monopoly on decision, where the word decision is employed in the broader, general sense. The case of exception most clearly reveals the essence of state authority. Here the decision departs from the legal norm, and (to formulate it paradoxically) the authority proves that in it does not need the law in order to create the law.

181718

The state of exception was incommensurable to the liberal constitutional doctrine of John Locke and the rationalist 18th century. The vivid consciousness of the significance of the exceptional case that prevailed in the natural law of the 17th century was soon lost again in the 18th century as a relatively long-lasting order was established. For Kant, emergency law was no longer law at all. The contemporary theory of state shows the interesting spectacle of both tendencies confronting each other: the rationalist tendency of ignoring the emergency, and interest in the case of emergency stemming from fundamentally opposing ideas. That a neo-Kantian like Kelsen systematically has no idea how to deal with the state of exception is self-evident. But it would have to be of interest even to the rationalist that a legal order itself could foresee the state of emergency and suspend itself. That a norm or an order or a point of imputation can establish itself seems to this type of juristic rationalist to be a particularly easily enforceable proposition. But how a systematic unity and order can suspend itself in a concrete case is difficult to comprehend, and yet is a juristic problem as long as the state of exception is differentiated from juristic chaos and from arbitrary anarchy. The liberal constitutional tendency to regulate the state of exception as thoroughly as possible simply indicates an attempt to precisely circumscribe the case in which the law suspends itself. From where does the law draw this power, and how is it logically possible that a norm is valid with the exception of one concrete case, which, which it cannot completely define?

90619

It would be a consequence of rationalism to say that the exception proves nothing and only the normal can be a subject of scholarly interest. The exception confounds the unity and order of the rationalist scheme. In the positive theory of state, one frequently encounters a similar argument. To the question of how to proceed without an available budget law, Gerhard Anschtz responds that this is not a question of law at all: There is here not so much a gap in the law, meaning in the text of the constitution, as a gap in law as a whole, which cannot be filled by any juristic conceptual operations. Constitutional law ends here, (Lehrbuch des Deutschen Staatsrechts, p.906). It is precisely a philosophy of concrete life that cannot retreat from the exception and the extreme case, but must rather be interested in it to the highest degree. The exception can be more important than the rule, not only from a romantic irony for the paradox, but rather with the full earnestness of an insight that goes deeper than the clear generalization drawn from that which ordinarily repeats itself. The exception is more interesting than the normal case. The normal proves nothing, the exception proves everything; it not only confirms the rule the rule only exists because of the exception. In the exception, the power of real life breaks through the crust of a mechanism grown sclerotic through repetition. A protestant theologian, who has proven what vital intensity theological reflection is capable of even in the 19th century, said: The exception explains the general and itself. And if one wishes to properly study the general, one need only look around for a true exception. It reveals everything more clearly than the general itself. In the long run, one becomes weary of eternal chatter about the general; there are exceptions. If one cannot explain it, then one can also not explain the general. Usually one does not notice the difficulty, because one does not think about the general with passion, but with a comfortable superficiality. The exception, on the other hand, thinks of the general with energetic passion. Sren Kierkegaard

2. The Problem of Sovereignty as a Problem of the Legal Form and the Decision

When theories and concepts of constitutional law are reshaped under the influence of political events and changes, the discussion comes under the control of the practical viewpoints of the day and modifies the traditional conceptions according to some obvious purpose. New actualities can elicit a new sociological interest and a reaction against the formalistic approach to problems of constitutional law. It is, however, possible for an endeavor to emerge that makes juristic treatment independent from the change in political relations, and consequently achieves scholarly objectivity by a formal method of treatment. Thus, various scholarly tendencies and currents can emerge from the same political situation.

1616181871

Of all juristic concepts, the concept of sovereignty is most governed by contemporary interests. One customarily allows that its history begins with Bodin, but one can not say that it has experienced a logical development or elucidation since the 16th century. The stages of its dogmatic history are marked by various political power struggles, not by a dialectical heightening from its conception. Bodin's concept of sovereignty appeared in the 16th century from the final dissolution of Europe into nation-states, and from the struggle of the absolute rule with the estates. In the 18th century the self-consciousness of the newly established states was reflected in Emer de Vattel's concept of sovereignty in international law. In the newly founded German Reich it became necessary after 1871 to advance a principle for the demarcation of the authority of the member states from that of the federal state, and from this concern arose a distinction in the German theory of state between the concept of sovereignty and the concept of state, with the help of which the individual states can retain their status as states, without their sovereignty being adjudicated. The old definition, in all kinds of variations, is always repeated: sovereignty is the highest, legally independent, underived power.

-

Such a definition can be applied to all kinds of political-sociological complexes and employed in the service of all kinds of political interests, It is not the adequate expression of a reality, but rather a formula, a sign, a signal. It is endlessly ambiguous and thus in practice extraordinarily useful or entirely worthless depending on the situation It employs the superlative highest power as a designation of an actual quantity, although in reality governed by the law of causality, no single factor can be singled out and regarded with such a superlative. An irresistible highest or greatest power functioning according to the certainty of natural law does not exist in political reality; power proves nothing for the law for the banal reason that Jean-Jacques Rousseau formulated in agreement with his time thusly: La force est une puissance physique; le pistolet que le brigand tient est aussi une puissance Force is a physical power; the pistol carried by the brigand is also a form of power, (The Social Contract, book 1, Chapter 3). The connection of factual and legal highest power is the fundamental problem of the concept of sovereignty. Herein lie all the difficulties, and the issue is finding a definition that captures this fundamental concept of jurisprudence not through general tautological predicates, but through specification of its juristic essence.

19201922

The most thorough treatment of the concept of sovereignty in recent years attempts a simple solution nevertheless: sociological jurisprudence, and with a simple either-or obtaining something purely sociological or something purely juristic. Kelsen followed this path in his works Das Problem der Souvernitt und die Theorie des Vlkerrechts (The Problem of Sovereignty and the Theory of International Law Tbingen 1920) and Der soziologische und der juristische Staatsbegriff (The Sociological and the Legal Concept of State Tbingen 1922). All sociological elements are left out of the juristic concept so that a system of attribution to norms and to a final uniform base norm can be achieved in unadulterated purity. The old contrast between is and ought, between causal and normative considerations, is transferred to the contrast of sociology and jurisprudence, with greater insistence and rigor than had already been done by Georg Jellinek and Kistiakowski, but with the same unproven implicitness. It seems to be the fate of juristic scholarship that such disjunctions are applied to it from some other scholarly discipline or from epistemology. Kelsen, using this procedure, arrived at the unsurprising conclusion that for juristic consideration, the state must be something purely juristic, something normatively valid, not just some reality or concept beside alongside and outside of the legal order, but rather nothing other than this legal order itself, conceived of as a unity. (That this is precisely where the problem lies does not seem to create any difficulties). Thus, the state is neither the originator nor the source of the legal order; all such perceptions, according to Kelsen, are personifications and hypostatization, duplications of the uniform and identical legal order in different subjects. The state, that is, the legal order, is a system of attributions to a final point of attribution and a final base norm. The hierarchical order that is valid in the state rests on the premise that authorizations and competences emanate from the uniform central point to the lowest point. The highest competence comes not from a person or a socio-psychological power complex, but from the sovereign order itself in the unity of the system of norms. For juristic consideration there are neither real nor fictitious persons, but rather only points of attribution. The state is the endpoint of attributions, to which attributions, which are the essence of juristic consideration, can come to a stop. This point is simultaneously an order that cannot be further derived. A continuous system of orders emanating from the original, final, highest, to a lower, meaning a delegated norm, can be thought of in such a manner. The decisive argument, constantly repeated and brought against every scholarly opponent, remains the same: the basis for the validity of a norm can only be a norm; for juristic considerations, the state is therefore identical with its constitution, meaning with the uniform base norm.

The watchword of this deduction is unity. The unity of the epistemological viewpoint imperiously demands a monistic view. The dualism of the methods of sociology and jurisprudence ends in a monistic metaphysics. The unity of the legal order (meaning the state), however, remains pure and free of everything sociological within the framework of the juristic. Is this juristic unity of the same kind as the worldwide unity of the entire system? How is it that a host of positive provisions can be traced to a unity with the same point of attribution, when what is meant is not the unity of a system of natural law, nor a general theoretical legal theory, but rather the unity of a positively valid order? Words like order, system, and unity are indeed only circumscriptions of the same postulation, which must demonstrate how it can be fulfilled in its purity. It must be shown how a system can arise on the basis of a constitution (which means either a further tautological circumscription of the unity or a brutal socio-political fact). According to Kelsen, systematic unity is an independent act of juristic perception. Let us disregard the interesting mathematical mythology according to which a point should be an order, and a system, and identical with a norm, and let us ask on what the intellectual necessity and objectivity of the various attributions to the various points of attribution, if not on a positive proposition, that is, a command. The continuous unity and order is constantly discussed as though it were the most self-evident matter in the world; as though there existed a pre-established harmony between the result of a free juristic knowledge and the complex that only constitutes a unity in political reality, a stepladder of higher and lower orders is spoken of, supposedly found in every positive ordinance trotted out before jurisprudence. The normative scholarship to which Kelsen wishes to raise jurisprudence in total purity cannot be normative in the sense that the jurist judges from his own free will; he can only draw from given (positively given) values. Objectivity thus appears to be possible but has no necessary connection to positivity. The values the jurist draws are indeed given to him, but he acts with relativistic superiority in the face of them. For he can construct a unity from everything in which he is juristically interested, provided that he remains pure. Unity and purity are, however, easily achieved when one ignores actual difficulties with great insistence and, for formal reasons, everything which contradicts the system is excluded as impure. He who takes no chances and stays resolutely methodological, without a single concrete example to point to in which his jurisprudence differs from that which was previously taken as jurisprudence, finds it easy to be critical. The methodological invocation, conceptual sharpening, and astute criticism are only valuable as preparation. If they do not get to the point, with the rationale that jurisprudence is something formal, then they remain, despite all effort, in the antechamber of jurisprudence.

Kelsen solves the problem of the concept of sovereignty by negating it. The result of his deduction is that the concept of sovereignty must be radically repressed (Das Problem der Souvernitt, p. 320). This is the old liberal negation of the state as opposed to the law, and ignoring the independent problem of implementing the law. This perception is presented in Hugo Krabbe, whose theory of legal sovereignty, under the title Die Moderne Staatsidee (The Modern Idea of the State, 1906, expanded edition 1919), rests on the thesis that it is not the state but rather the law that is sovereign.. Kelsen seems to see here a mere precursor to his own theory of the identity of state and legal order. In reality, Krabbes' theory has a common ideological root with Kelsen's result, but precisely where Kelsen was original in his methodology, there is no connection between the Dutch jurist and the epistemological and methodological distinctions of the German Neo-Kantian. As Krabbes says, however one wants to take it, the theory of legal sovereignty is either the description of an actually extant state of affairs, or a postulation whose implementation should be striven for (p. 39). The modern idea of state, according to Krabbe, replaces personal force (of the king, of the authorities) with spiritual power. We no longer live under the authority of persons, be they natural or artificial (legal) persons, but under the authority of norms, of spiritual forces. There the essence of the modern idea of the state is revealed. He continues, These forces rule in the strictest sense of the word, for, because they originate from the spiritual nature of man, these forces can achieve voluntary obedience. The basis, the source of the legal order, is to be found only in the members of the nation's feeling and consciousness of justice. For the sociology of the concept of sovereignty it is necessary to be clear about the sociology of legal concepts in general. That systematic analogy between theological and juristic concepts is emphasized here because a sociology of juristic concepts presupposes a consistent and developed ideology. It would be a serious misunderstanding to believe that this is a spiritual philosophy of history as opposed to a materialist one. The political theology of the Restoration period offers an excellent illustration of the point that Max Weber made in his critique of Rudolf Stammeler's philosophy of law, namely that a radically materialist philosophy of history can be irrefutably contrasted with an equally radically spiritual philosophy of history. The counter-revolutionary writers explained the political changes from a change in worldview, and attributed the French Revolution to the philosophy of the Enlightenment. It was simply a clear antithesis when radical revolutionaries, conversely, attributed the change in thought to the change in political and social conditions. Already in the 1820s it was a common dogma in Western Europe, especially in France, that religious, philosophical, artistic, and literary changes were closely related to political and social conditions. In the Marxist philosophy of history this connection is extended to the economic and systematically taken seriously, in that a point of attribution for political and social changes is also sought and found in the economic. This materialistic explanation makes an isolated consideration of ideological consequence impossible, because it sees everywhere only "reflexes", "reflections", "disguises" of economic relations, thus consequently working with psychological explanations, interpretations and, at least in its vulgar form, with suspicions. Precisely because of its grave rationalism, however, it can easily turn into an irrationalist view of history, because it conceives of all thought as a function and emanation of vital processes. The anarcho-syndicalist socialism of Georges Sorel thus knew to combine Henri Bergson's philosophy of life with Marx's economic view of history.

The spiritualist explanation of material processes and the materialist explanation of spiritual phenomena both seek to establish causal connections. First, they establish an opposition between two spheres and then, by reducing one to the other, they dissolve this opposition again into nothingness. This procedure turns into a caricature out of methodical necessity. Just as Engels regards the Calvinist dogma of predestination as a reflection of the senselessness and unpredictability of capitalist competition, modern relativity and its success can just as easily be reduced to the exchange rates of today's world market, and would then have found its economic foundation. There is a parlance that would call this the sociology of a concept or theory. That won't be taken into consideration here. The situation is different with the sociological method, which, for certain ideas and intellectual constructions, looks for the typical group of people who come to certain ideological results from their sociological situations. In this sense, it is a sociology of juristic concepts when Max Weber attributes the differentiation of substantive legal fields to the development of trained legal experts, civil servants in the administration of justice, or legal dignitaries (Rechtssoziologie, II, I). The sociological "peculiarity of the group of persons who professionally deal with the formation of law" necessitates certain methods and evidence of juristic argumentation. But even that is still not sociology of a juristic concept. To attribute a conceptual result to a sociological carrier is psychology, and determination of a certain type of motivation of human action. For him, therefore, there is nothing negative and evil but the theological doctrine of God and sin, which stamps man as a villain in order to have a pretext for its imperiousness and lust for power. All moral evaluations lead to theology and to an authority that artificially imposes an alien, extrinsic ought on the natural and immanent truth and beauty of human life, whose source is greed and lust for power and whose result is a general corruption of those who exercise power as well as those over whom it is exercised. When today anarchists see the patriarchy and monogamy-based family as the actual state of sin and preach a return to matriarchy, the alleged paradisal primordial state, a stronger awareness of the deepest connections is expressed in this than in that laughter of Proudhon. Such final consequences, such as the dissolution of the patriarchy-based family, are always on Donoso's mind, because he sees that the moral disappears with the theological, the political idea with the moral, and every moral and political decision is paralyzed in a paradisal mortal world of immediate, natural life and problem-free "corporeality".

Today there is nothing more modern than the struggle against the political. American financiers, industrial technicians, Marxist socialists, and anarcho-syndicalist revolutionaries unite in the demand that the nonobjective rule of politics over the objectivity of economic life must be eliminated. They insist there ought to be only organizational-technical and economic-sociological tasks, but no more political problems. The kind of economic and technical thinking that prevails today is no longer able to perceive a political idea. The modern state really seems to have become what Max Weber sees in it: a large business. A political idea is generally only understood when it has been possible to prove that a group of people has a plausible economic interest in using it to its advantage. If the political disappears into economic or technical-organizational, it melts away on the other side in the eternal conversation of cultural and historical-philosophical generalities, which, by aesthetic characterizations, accept an epoch as classical, romantic or baroque. In both, the core of the political idea, the demanding moral decision, is circumvented. But the current significance of those counter-revolutionary philosophers of state lies in the consistency with which they decide. They heighten the moment of decision so much that it finally overrides the idea of legitimacy from which they started. As soon as Donoso Corts realized that the time of the monarchy was over because there were no more kings and no one would have the courage to be king other than by the will of the people, he brought his decisionism to a conclusion; that is, he demanded a political dictatorship. Already in the quoted statements of de Maistre there was a reduction of the state to the moment of decision, and consequently to a pure, non-reasoning and non-discussing, unapologetic, i.e. created from nothing, absolute decision.

But that is essentially dictatorship, not legitimacy. Donoso was convinced that the moment of the final battle had come; in the face of radical evil there is only one dictatorship, and the legitimist idea of succession becomes at such a moment empty self-righteousness. Thus the opposites, authority and anarchy, could confront each other with absolute decisiveness and form the clear antithesis mentioned above: when de Maistre says that every government is necessarily absolute, an anarchist literally says the same thing; only he draws the opposite practical conclusion with the help of his axiom of the good man and corrupt government, that every government must be fought precisely because every government is dictatorship. Every pretension of a decision must be evil for the anarchist, because the right thing comes automatically if the immanence of life is not disturbed by such pretensions. Of course, this radical antithesis compels him to decide decisively against decision; and in the case of the greatest anarchist of the 19th century, Bakunin, the strange paradox arises that he had to become theoretically the theologian of anti-theology and in practice the dictator of anti-dictatorship.
