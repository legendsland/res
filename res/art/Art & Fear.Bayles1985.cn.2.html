INTRODUCTION

这是一本关于艺术创作的书。普通艺术。普通艺术的意思是：所有不是莫扎特创作的艺术。毕竟，艺术很少是由像莫扎特这样的人创作的——基本上（从统计上来说）没有这样的人。但是，虽然天才可能一个世纪左右就会出现一次，但好的艺术却一直在诞生。创作艺术是一项常见且与人类密切相关的活动，任何有价值的努力都伴随着所有的危险（和回报）。艺术创作者面临的困难并不是遥远的、英雄的，而是普遍的、熟悉的。

THIS IS A BOOK ABOUT MAKING ART. Ordinary art. Ordinary art means something like: all art not made by Mozart. After all, art is rarely made by Mozart-like people — essentially (statistically speaking) there aren’t any people like that. But while geniuses may get made once-a-century or so, good art gets made all the time. Making art is a common and intimately human activity, filled with all the perils (and rewards) that accompany any worthwhile effort. The difficulties artmakers face are not remote and heroic, but universal and familiar.

那么，这是一本写给我们其他人的书。两位作者都是艺术家，每天都在努力解决现实世界中的艺术创作问题。我们在这里所做的观察来自个人经验，与艺术家的需求更密切相关，而不是与观众的兴趣相关。这本书讲述的是坐在工作室或教室里，坐在方向盘或键盘、画架或相机上，尝试做你需要做的工作的感觉。它将你的未来交托给你自己，将自由意志置于命运之上，将选择置于机会之上。这是关于找到自己的工作。

This, then, is a book for the rest of us. Both authors are working artists, grappling daily with the problems of making art in the real world. The observations we make here are drawn from personal experience, and relate more closely to the needs of artists than to the interests of viewers. This book is about what it feels like to sit in your studio or classroom, at your wheel or keyboard, easel or camera, trying to do the work you need to do. It is about committing your future to your own hands, placing Free Will above predestination, choice above chance. It is about finding your own work.

创作艺术是困难的。我们留下未完成的图画和未写的故事。我们所做的工作感觉不像我们自己的。我们重复自己。我们在掌握材料之前就停下来，或者在它们的潜力耗尽之后继续前进。通常，我们未完成的工作在我们看来比我们已完成的工作更真实。于是问题就出现了：艺术是如何完成的？为什么常常做不完？阻碍如此多创业者的困难的本质是什么？

MAKING ART IS DIFFICULT. We leave drawings unfinished and stories unwritten. We do work that does not feel like our own. We repeat ourselves. We stop before we have mastered our materials, or continue on long after their potential is exhausted. Often the work we have not done seems more real in our minds than the pieces we have completed. And so questions arise: How does art get done? Why, often, does it not get done? And what is the nature of the difficulties that stop so many who start?

这些看似永恒的问题实际上可能是我们这个时代所特有的。很久以前，在洞穴墙壁上画野牛可能比今天写这句话（或任何其他）更容易。其他人，在其他时间和地点，有一些强有力的机构来支撑他们：见证教会、氏族、仪式、传统。很容易想象，艺术家在为上帝服务时比在为自我服务时更少怀疑自己的使命。

These questions, which seem so timeless, may actually be particular to our age. It may have been easier to paint bison on the cave walls long ago than to write this (or any other) sentence today. Other people, in other times and places, had some robust institutions to shore them up: witness the Church, the clan, ritual, tradition. It’s easy to imagine that artists doubted their calling less when working in the service of God than when working in the service of self.

今天不是这样。如今，几乎没有人感到受到支撑。如今，艺术品并不是从一个安全的共同点中诞生的：墙上的野牛是别人的魔法。现在创作艺术意味着要面对不确定性。它意味着生活在怀疑和矛盾中，做一些没人关心你是否做的事情，而且可能既没有观众也没有回报。做好自己想做的事，就意味着抛开这些疑虑，这样你才能清楚地看到自己做了什么，从而知道下一步该往哪里走。创作你想做的作品意味着在作品本身中寻找营养。这不是信仰、真理和确定性的时代。

Not so today. Today almost no one feels shored up. Today artwork does not emerge from a secure common ground: the bison on the wall is someone else’s magic. Making art now means working in the face of uncertainty ; it means living with doubt and contradiction, doing something no one much cares whether you do, and for which there may be neither audience nor reward. Making the work you want to make means setting aside these doubts so that you may see clearly what you have done, and thereby see where to go next. Making the work you want to make means finding nourishment within the work itself. This is not the Age of Faith, Truth and Certainty.

然而，即使是“你在这个过程中拥有发言权”的观念也与当今艺术创作的流行观点相冲突——即，艺术从根本上取决于天赋，而天赋是随机赋予某些人而不是其他人的天赋。用通俗的话说，要么你有，要么你没有——伟大的艺术是天才的产物，好的艺术是近乎天才的产物（纳博科夫将其比作近乎啤酒），依此类推，通俗浪漫主义和按数字绘制。这种观点本质上是宿命论的——即使它是真的，也是宿命论的——并且对那些想要创作艺术的人没有提供任何有用的鼓励。就我个人而言，我们会支持康拉德的宿命论观点：即，这是一种恐惧——恐惧你的命运掌握在你自己的手中，但你的手是软弱的。

Yet even the notion that you have a say in this process conflicts with the prevailing view of artmaking today — namely, that art rests fundamentally upon talent, and that talent is a gift randomly built into some people and not into others. In common parlance, either you have it or you don’t — great art is a product of genius, good art a product of near-genius (which Nabokov likened to Near-Beer), and so on down the line to pulp romances and paint-by-the-numbers. This view is inherently fatalistic — even if it’s true, it’s fatalistic — and offers no useful encouragement to those who would make art. Personally, we’ll side with Conrad’s view of fatalism: namely, that it is a species of fear — the fear that your fate is in your own hands, but that your hands are weak.

但是，尽管天赋——更不用说命运、运气和悲剧——都在人类命运中发挥着作用，但它们很难被视为在日常基础上推进自己的艺术的可靠工具。在日常生活的世界中（毕竟，这是我们生活的唯一世界），继续工作的工作取决于对人性做出一些基本假设，这些假设将权力（并因此责任），你的行为掌握在你自己手中。其中一些可以直接说明：

But while talent — not to mention fate, luck and tragedy — all play their role in human destiny, they hardly rank as dependable tools for advancing your own art on a day-to-day basis. Here in the day-to-day world (which is, after all, the only one we live in), the job of getting on with your work turns upon making some basic assumptions about human nature, assumptions that place the power (and hence the responsibility) for your actions in your own hands. Some of these can be stated directly:

A FEW ASSUMPTIONS

艺术创作涉及可以学习的技能。这里的传统观点是，虽然“手艺”可以传授，但“艺术”仍然是一种只有神灵赐予的神奇礼物。并非如此。在很大程度上，成为一名艺术家需要学会接受自己，这使你的作品变得个性化，并跟随自己的声音，这使你的作品与众不同。显然，这些品质可以由其他人培养。从长远来看，即使是天赋也很难与毅力和大量的努力区分开来。确实，每隔几年，作者就会遇到一些摄影初学者，他们第一学期的照片看起来就像安塞尔·亚当斯 (Ansel Adams) 制作的那样精美。确实，这样的天赋（尤其是在脆弱的早期学习阶段）会给它的创造者带来无价的鼓励。但这一切都与艺术内容无关。相反，它只是指出了这样一个事实：我们大多数人（包括亚当斯本人！）必须工作数年才能完善我们的艺术。

ARTMAKING INVOLVES SKILLS THAT CAN BE LEARNED. The conventional wisdom here is that while “craft” can be taught, “art” remains a magical gift bestowed only by the gods. Not so. In large measure becoming an artist consists of learning to accept yourself, which makes your work personal, and in following your own voice, which makes your work distinctive. Clearly, these qualities can be nurtured by others. Even talent is rarely distinguishable, over the long run, from perseverance and lots of hard work. It’s true that every few years the authors encounter some beginning photography student whose first-semester prints appear as finely crafted as any Ansel Adams might have made. And it’s true that a natural gift like that (especially coming at the fragile early learning stage) returns priceless encouragement to its maker. But all that has nothing to do with artistic content. Rather, it simply points up the fact that most of us (including Adams himself!) had to work years to perfect our art.

艺术是由普通人创造的。很难想象只有美德的生物才能创作艺术。很难想象圣母玛利亚画的风景。或者蝙蝠侠扔罐子。完美的生物不需要创作艺术。因此，具有讽刺意味的是，理想的艺术家根本不是一个理论人物。如果艺术是由普通人创作的，那么你就必须承认理想的艺术家也是一个普通人，具有真实人类所拥有的所有常见的混合特征。这是关于艺术的一个巨大暗示，因为它表明我们的缺陷和弱点虽然常常成为我们完成工作的障碍，但也是力量的源泉。艺术创作与克服困难有关，它为我们提供了一个明确的机会，以我们一直知道应该做的方式做事。

ART IS MADE BY ORDINARY PEOPLE. Creatures having only virtues can hardly be imagined making art. It’s difficult to picture the Virgin Mary painting landscapes. Or Batman throwing pots. The flawless creature wouldn’t need to make art. And so, ironically, the ideal artist is scarcely a theoretical figure at all. If art is made by ordinary people, then you’d have to allow that the ideal artist would be an ordinary person too, with the whole usual mixed bag of traits that real human beings possess. This is a giant hint about art, because it suggests that our flaws and weaknesses, while often obstacles to our getting work done, are a source of strength as well. Something about making art has to do with overcoming things, giving us a clear opportunity for doing things in ways we have always known we should do them.

创作艺术和观看艺术的核心是不同的。理智的人会感到满意，他/她在任何特定时刻能做的最好的事情就是他/她在任何特定时刻可以做的最好的事情。这种信念如果被广泛接受，将使这本书变得不必要、错误或两者兼而有之。不幸的是，这样的理智是罕见的。艺术创作提供了关于你想做的事和你所做的事之间不可避免地存在的差距的令人不安的准确反馈。事实上，如果艺术创作没有告诉你（创作者）如此多的关于你自己的信息，那么创作对你来说很重要的艺术是不可能的。对于除了你自己之外的所有观众来说，重要的是产品：成品艺术品。对于你，而且只有你来说，重要的是过程：塑造艺术品的体验。观众的担忧不是你的担忧（尽管很容易采纳他们的态度，这是危险的。）他们的工作是什么：被艺术感动，被艺术娱乐，靠艺术致富，等等。你的工作就是学会做好你的工作。

MAKING ART AND VIEWING ART ARE DIFFERENT AT THEIR CORE. The sane human being is satisfied that the best he / she can do at any given moment is the best he/she can do at any given moment. That belief, if widely embraced, would make this book unnecessary, false, or both. Such sanity is, unfortunately, rare. Making art provides uncomfortably accurate feedback about the gap that inevitably exists between what you intended to do, and what you did. In fact, if artmaking did not tell you (the maker) so enormously much about yourself, then making art that matters to you would be impossible. To all viewers but yourself, what matters is the product: the finished artwork. To you, and you alone, what matters is the process: the experience of shaping that artwork. The viewers’ concerns are not your concerns (although it’s dangerously easy to adopt their attitudes.) Their job is whatever it is: to be moved by art, to be entertained by it, to make a killing off it, whatever. Your job is to learn to work on your work.

对于艺术家来说，这个事实凸显了一个熟悉且可预见的推论：艺术创作可能是一件相当孤独、吃力不讨好的事情。几乎所有艺术家都会花费一些时间（有些艺术家几乎花费所有时间）创作其他人不太关心的作品。它似乎只是随着领土而来。但出于某种原因——也许是自卫——艺术家们发现将这种缺乏反应浪漫化是很诱人的，通常是通过（英勇地）想象自己在其他人有目光可循之前就深入洞察事物的根本本质。

For the artist, that truth highlights a familiar and predictable corollary: artmaking can be a rather lonely, thankless affair. Virtually all artists spend some of their time (and some artists spend virtually all of their time) producing work that no one else much cares about. It just seems to come with the territory. But for some reason — self-defense, perhaps — artists find it tempting to romanticize this lack of response, often by (heroically) picturing themselves peering deeply into the underlying nature of things long before anyone else has eyes to follow.

浪漫，但错误。发人深省的事实是，对他人的漠不关心绝不会反映出愿景的鸿沟。事实上，一般来说没有充分的理由让其他人应该关心任何一位艺术家的大部分作品。你的绝大多数艺术作品的作用只是教你如何让你的艺术作品中的一小部分飞升。每个艺术家必须学习的基本和困难的教训之一是，即使是失败的作品也是必不可少的。著名画作的 X 射线显示，即使是大师级艺术家有时也会通过在仍然湿润的画布上涂漆来进行基本的中途修正（或删除非常愚蠢的错误）。重点是，你通过创作自己的作品来学习如何创作你的作品，而你一路上制作的很多作品永远不会成为成品艺术品。你能做的最好的事情就是创作你关心的艺术——而且是很多！

Romantic, but wrong. The sobering truth is that the disinterest of others hardly ever reflects a gulf in vision. In fact there’s generally no good reason why others should care about most of any one artist’s work. The function of the overwhelming majority of your artwork is simply to teach you how to make the small fraction of your artwork that soars. One of the basic and difficult lessons every artist must learn is that even the failed pieces are essential. X-rays of famous paintings reveal that even master artists sometimes made basic mid-course corrections (or deleted really dumb mistakes) by overpainting the still-wet canvas. The point is that you learn how to make your work by making your work, and a great many of the pieces you make along the way will never stand out as finished art. The best you can do is make art you care about — and lots of it!

剩下的很大程度上就是毅力的问题。当然，一旦你成名，收藏家和学者就会成群结队地回来声称在每件早期作品中发现了天才的证据。但在你的船进来之前，唯一真正关心你工作的人是那些关心你个人的人。你身边的人都知道，工作对你的健康至关重要。他们将永远关心你的工作，如果不是因为它很棒，那就是因为它是你的——这是值得真正感激的事情。然而，无论他们多么爱你，对他们来说和对世界其他人来说仍然一样：学习创作你的作品不是他们的问题。

The rest is largely a matter of perseverance. Of course once you’re famous, collectors and academics will circle back in droves to claim credit for spotting evidence of genius in every early piece. But until your ship comes in, the only people who will really care about your work are those who care about you personally. Those close to you know that making the work is essential to your well being. They will always care about your work, if not because it is great, then because it is yours — and this is something to be genuinely thankful for. Yet however much they love you, it still remains as true for them as for the rest of the world: learning to make your work is not their problem.

艺术创作的历史比艺术机构的历史还要悠久。在大部分历史中，创作艺术的人从来不认为自己在创作艺术。事实上，很可能早在意识兴起之前，早在代词“我”被使用之前，艺术就已经被创造出来了。洞穴画家除了不认为自己是艺术家之外，很可能根本就没有想过自己。

ARTMAKING HAS BEEN AROUND LONGER THAN THE ART ESTABLISHMENT. Through most of history, the people who made art never thought of themselves as making art. In fact it’s quite presumable that art was being made long before the rise of consciousness, long before the pronoun “I” was ever employed. The painters of caves, quite apart from not thinking of themselves as artists, probably never thought of themselves at all.

除其他外，这表明当前将艺术等同于“自我表达”的观点更多地揭示了我们思维中的当代偏见，而不是媒介的潜在特征。甚至艺术与工艺的分离在很大程度上也是后文艺复兴时期的概念，而最近的观念仍然是艺术超越你所做的事情，并代表你是谁。在过去的几个世纪里，西方艺术已经从无署名的正统宗教场景画面转向个人宇宙观的个人展示。 “艺术家”逐渐成为一种身份形式（正如每个艺术家都知道的那样），它往往带来的弊端和好处一样多。考虑一下，如果艺术家等于自我，那么当（不可避免地）你创作有缺陷的艺术时，你就是一个有缺陷的人，而当（更糟糕）你不创作艺术时，你根本就不是人！通过接受多种成功艺术创作的途径——从隐居到华丽、从直觉到理智、从民间艺术到美术——来避开这种恶性循环似乎要健康得多。其中一条路就是你的。

What this suggests, among other things, is that the current view equating art with “self-expression” reveals more a contemporary bias in our thinking than an underlying trait of the medium. Even the separation of art from craft is largely a post-Renaissance concept, and more recent still is the notion that art transcends what you do, and represents what you are. In the past few centuries Western art has moved from unsigned tableaus of orthodox religious scenes to one-person displays of personal cosmologies. “Artist” has gradually become a form of identity which (as every artist knows) often carries with it as many drawbacks as benefits. Consider that if artist equals self, then when (inevitably) you make flawed art, you are a flawed person, and when (worse yet) you make no art, you are no person at all! It seems far healthier to sidestep that vicious spiral by accepting many paths to successful artmaking — from reclusive to flamboyant, intuitive to intellectual, folk art to fine art. One of those paths is yours.

那些想要创造艺术的人很可能会从反思前人的命运开始：大多数开始创作的人都退出了。这是一场真正的悲剧。更糟糕的是，这是一场不必要的悲剧。毕竟，继续创作的艺术家和放弃创作的艺术家有着巨大的共同情感基础。 （事实上​​，从外面看，它们是无法区分的。）我们都面临着人类常见的、普遍存在的麻烦——我们通常会幸存的麻烦，但（奇怪的是）这些麻烦通常对艺术创作过程来说是致命的。作为一名艺术家要想生存就必须面对这些麻烦。基本上，那些继续创作艺术的人是那些学会了如何继续——或者更准确地说，学会了如何不放弃的人。

THOSE WHO WOULD MAKE ART might well begin by reflecting on the fate of those who preceded them: most who began, quit. It’s a genuine tragedy. Worse yet, it’s an unnecessary tragedy. After all, artists who continue and artists who quit share an immense field of common emotional ground. (Viewed from the outside, in fact, they’re indistinguishable.) We’re all subject to a familiar and universal progression of human troubles — troubles we routinely survive, but which are (oddly enough) routinely fatal to the artmaking process. To survive as an artist requires confronting these troubles. Basically, those who continue to make art are those who have learned how to continue — or more precisely, have learned how to not quit.

但奇怪的是，虽然艺术家总是有无数的理由退出，但他们总是等待少数特定的时刻才能退出。当艺术家说服自己他们的下一次努力已经注定要失败时，他们就会放弃。当艺术家失去了作品的目的地——失去了作品所属的地方时，他们就会退出。

But curiously, while artists always have a myriad of reasons to quit, they consistently wait for a handful of specific moments to quit. Artists quit when they convince themselves that their next effort is already doomed to fail. And artists quit when they lose the destination for their work — for the place their work belongs.

几乎所有艺术家都会遇到这样的时刻。担心你的下一个作品会失败是艺术创作周期中正常的、反复出现的、总体上健康的一部分。这种情况经常发生：你在工作中专注于一些新想法，你尝试它，运行一段时间，达到收益递减的点，并最终决定它不值得进一步追求。作家甚至有一个短语来形容它——“笔已干”——但所有媒体都有相应的短语。在正常的艺术周期中，这只是告诉你，你已经兜了一圈，回到了你需要开始培养下一个新想法的那个点。但在艺术死亡中，它标志着最后发生的事情：你提出了一个想法，它停止工作，你放下画笔……三十年后，你在喝咖啡时向某人吐露，嗯，是的，你曾想当你年轻得多的时候画画。戒烟与停止有着根本的不同。后者经常发生。退出只会发生一次。放弃意味着不再重新开始——而艺术就是重新开始。

Virtually all artists encounter such moments. Fear that your next work will fail is a normal, recurring and generally healthy part of the artmaking cycle. It happens all the time: you focus on some new idea in your work, you try it out, run with it for awhile, reach a point of diminishing returns, and eventually decide it’s not worth pursuing further. Writers even have a phrase for it — “the pen has run dry” — but all media have their equivalents. In the normal artistic cycle this just tells you that you’ve come full circle, back to that point where you need to begin cultivating the next new idea. But in artistic death it marks the last thing that happens : you play out an idea, it stops working, you put the brush down...and thirty years later you confide to someone over coffee that, well, yes, you had wanted to paint when you were much younger. Quitting is fundamentally different from stopping. The latter happens all the time. Quitting happens once. Quitting means not starting again — and art is all about starting again.

当作品的目的地突然撤回时，艺术家的第二个普遍真理时刻就会出现。对于经验丰富的艺术家来说，这个时刻通常与到达目的地同时发生——我们觉得这很反常。作者回忆起一位共同的朋友，二十年来他一心一意地追求在他所在城市的主要艺术博物馆举办一场个人展览。他终于明白了。并且再也没有创作出任何严肃的艺术作品。对于这样的故事来说，发现成功是多么频繁且容易地转化为抑郁症，这是一个痛苦的讽刺。避免这种命运与不让当前的目标成为唯一的目标有关。对于个人艺术作品来说，这意味着留下一些松散的线索、一些未解决的问题，以便在下一件作品中继续推进和探索。有了更大的目标（比如专着或大型展览），这意味着你要始终携带着下一个目的地的晶种。对于一些对身体有风险的艺术形式（如舞蹈），这甚至可能意味着在身边保留一种替代媒介，以防年龄或受伤使你无法选择工作。

A second universal moment of truth for artists appears when the destination for the work is suddenly withdrawn. For veteran artists this moment usually coincides — rather perversely, we feel- with reaching that destination. The authors recall a mutual friend whose single-minded quest, for twenty years, was to land a one-man show at his city’s major art museum. He finally got it. And never produced a serious piece of art again. There’s a painful irony to stories like that, to discovering how frequently and easily success transmutes into depression. Avoiding this fate has something to do with not letting your current goal become your only goal. With individual artworks it means leaving some loose thread, some unresolved issue, to carry forward and explore in the next piece. With larger goals (like monographs or major shows) it means always carrying within you the seed crystal for your next destination. And for a few physically risky artforms (like dance), it may even mean keeping an alternative medium close by in case age or injury take you from your chosen work.

对于艺术系的学生来说，失去工作的归宿还有一个名字：毕业。问任何学生：在他们之前有多少人参加过毕业生展和终端展？当“批判”是艺术家前五年创作的唯一有效目的地时，难怪当这条路停止时，损耗率会呈螺旋式上升。如果百分之九十八的医学生在毕业五年后不再行医，参议院就会进行调查，但艺术专业的学生通常会因职业过早而死亡。当他们的作品突然不再被人看到、不再展出、不再评论、不再受到鼓励时，没有多少人会继续创作。您可以...吗？

For art students, losing the destination for the work goes by another name: Graduation. Ask any student: For how many before them was the Graduate Show the Terminal Show? When “The Critique” is the only validated destination for work made during the first half-decade of an artist’s productive life, small wonder that attrition rates spiral when that path stops. If ninety-eight percent of our medical students were no longer practicing medicine five years after graduation, there would be a Senate investigation, yet that proportion of art majors are routinely consigned to an early professional death. Not many people continue making art when — abruptly — their work is no longer seen, no longer exhibited, no longer commented upon, no longer encouraged. Could you?

令人惊讶的是，学校期间的辍学率并没有那么高——真正的杀手是毕业后缺乏任何持续的支持系统。或许，如果外界对提供这种支持兴趣不大，那么艺术家自己就仍然需要这样做。从这个角度来看，一个策略不言而喻：

Surprisingly, the dropout rate during school is not all that high — the real killer is the lack of any continuing support system afterwards. Perhaps then, if the outside world shows little interest in providing that support, it remains for artists themselves to do so. Viewed that way, a strategy suggests itself:

b. Learn to think of [A], rather than the Museum of Modern Art, as the destination of your work. (Look at it this way: If all goes well, MOMA will eventually come to you.)

创作艺术的愿望很早就开始了。在非常年轻的孩子中，这种做法受到鼓励（或者至少被认为是无害的），但推动“严肃”教育很快就会对梦想和幻想造成沉重打击。 （是的，作者确实认识一些学生，他们的父母要求他们停止在艺术上浪费时间，否则他们可以自己支付学费。） 然而，对于一些人来说，这种愿望仍然存在，迟早必须得到解决。有充分的理由：你对艺术的渴望——美丽的、有意义的或情感的艺术——是你对自己的认识不可或缺的一部分。生活与艺术一旦交织在一起，很快就会密不可分；九十岁时，弗兰克·劳埃德·赖特仍在设计，伊莫金·坎宁安仍在摄影，斯特拉文斯基仍在作曲，毕加索仍在绘画。

The desire to make art begins early. Among the very young this is encouraged (or at least indulged as harmless) but the push toward a “serious” education soon exacts a heavy toll on dreams and fantasies. (Yes, the authors really have known students whose parents demanded they stop wasting their time on art or they could damn well pay their own tuition.) Yet for some the desire persists, and sooner or later must be addressed. And with good reason: your desire to make art — beautiful or meaningful or emotive art — is integral to your sense of who you are. Life and Art, once entwined, can quickly become inseparable; at age ninety Frank Lloyd Wright was still designing, Imogen Cunning-ham still photographing, Stravinsky still composing, Picasso still painting.

但如果艺术创作能够充实你的自我意识，相应的恐惧就是你无法胜任这项任务——你做不到，或者做不好，或者不能再做；或者你不是一个真正的艺术家，或者不是一个好的艺术家，或者没有天赋，或者无话可说。艺术家和他/她的作品之间的界限充其量只是一条很好的界限，而对于艺术家来说（很自然地）感觉没有这样的界限。创作艺术可能会让人感觉危险且具有启发性。创作艺术是危险且具有启发性的。创作艺术会引发自我怀疑，搅动你知道自己应该成为什么样子和你害怕自己可能成为什么样子之间的深渊。对于许多人来说，仅此一点就足以阻止他们开始行动——而对于那些开始行动的人来说，麻烦很快就会到来。事实上，质疑很快就蜂拥而至：

But if making art gives substance to your sense of self, the corresponding fear is that you’re not up to the task — that you can’t do it, or can’t do it well, or can’t do it again; or that you’re not a real artist, or not a good artist, or have no talent, or have nothing to say. The line between the artist and his / her work is a fine one at best, and for the artist it feels (quite naturally) like there is no such line. Making art can feel dangerous and revealing. Making art is dangerous and revealing. Making art precipitates self-doubt, stirring deep waters that lay between what you know you should be, and what you fear you might be. For many people, that alone is enough to prevent their ever getting started at all — and for those who do, trouble isn’t long in coming. Doubts, in fact, soon rise in swarms:

然而客观地看，这些恐惧显然与艺术无关，而与艺术家有关。与个人艺术品的关系就更小了。毕竟，在艺术创作中，您将最高的技能运用到您最关心的材料和想法上。艺术是崇高的使命——恐惧只是巧合。我们可能会补充说，巧合、偷偷摸摸和破坏性的，以各种方式伪装成懒惰、抗拒最后期限、对材料或环境的恼怒、对他人成就的干扰——实际上是任何阻碍你在工作中发挥最大作用的事情。艺术家与前艺术家的区别在于，那些挑战恐惧的人会继续前进；那些不这样做的人，退出。艺术创作过程中的每一步都在考验这个问题。

Yet viewed objectively, these fears obviously have less to do with art than they do with the artist. And even less to do with individual artworks. After all, in making art you bring your highest skills to bear upon the materials and ideas you most care about. Art is a high calling — fears are coincidental. Coincidental, sneaky and disruptive, we might add, disguising themselves variously as laziness, resistance to deadlines, irritation with materials or surroundings, distraction over the achievements of others — indeed as anything that keeps you from giving your work your best shot. What separates artists from ex-artists is that those who challenge their fears, continue; those who don’t, quit. Each step in the artmaking process puts that issue to the test.

愿景与执行

VISION & EXECUTION

当你回顾过去时，恐惧就会出现，当你展望未来时，恐惧就会出现。如果你容易陷入灾难性的幻想，你甚至可能会发现自己陷入了困境，盯着半成品的画布，担心自己没有能力完成它，而且如果你完成了，没有人会理解它。

Fears arise when you look back, and they arise when you look ahead. If you’re prone to disaster fantasies you may even find yourself caught in the middle, staring at your half-finished canvas and fearing both that you lack the ability to finish it, and that no one will understand it if you do.

然而，更常见的是，当愿景领先于执行时，恐惧会在那些完全适当（并且经常重复出现）的时刻出现。想想一位年轻学生的故事——确切地说，是大卫·贝勒斯——开始跟随一位大师学习钢琴。经过几个月的练习，大卫向他的老师感叹道：“但我在脑海中听到的音乐比我从手指上听到的要好得多。”

More often, though, fears rise in those entirely appropriate (and frequently recurring) moments when vision races ahead of execution. Consider the story of the young student — well, David Bayles, to be exact — who began piano studies with a Master. After a few months’ practice, David lamented to his teacher, “But I can hear the music so much better in my head than I can get out of my fingers.”

对此，大师回答说：“是什么让你认为事物会不断变化呢？”

To which the Master replied, “What makes you think that ever changes?”

这就是为什么他们被称为大师。当他将大卫的发现从自我怀疑的表达提升为对现实的简单观察时，不确定性就变成了一种资产。今天的教训是：愿景永远领先于执行——而且也应该如此。愿景、不确定性和材料知识是所有艺术家都必须承认和学习的必然性：愿景总是领先于执行，材料知识是你与现实的接触，不确定性是一种美德。

That’s why they’re called Masters. When he raised David’s discovery from an expression of self-doubt to a simple observation of reality, uncertainty became an asset. Lesson for the day: vision is always ahead of execution — and it should be. Vision, Uncertainty, and Knowledge of Materials are inevitabilities that all artists must acknowledge and learn from: vision is always ahead of execution, knowledge of materials is your contact with reality, and uncertainty is a virtue.

想像力

IMAGINATION

当你开始制作一个物体时，想象力就在掌控之中。当第一笔触动、第一个和弦响起时，艺术品的潜力从未如此高过。但随着作品的不断发展，技术和工艺逐渐占据主导地位，想象力变得不再那么有用。一件作品通过变得具体而成长。当赫尔曼·梅尔维尔写下开场白“叫我以实玛利”时，一个真实的故事——《白鲸记》——开始与众多可以想象到的其他故事区分开来。依此类推，在接下来的五百多页中，每一个连续的句子都以某种方式承认并与前面的所有内容相关。琼·迪迪恩（Joan Didion）直截了当地（带着标志性的悲观主义）指出了这个问题，她说：“第一句话的难处在于你被困住了。其他一切都会从这句话中流露出来。当你写下前两句话时，你的选择就消失了。”

Imagination is in control when you begin making an object. The artwork’s potential is never higher than in that magic moment when the first brushstroke is applied, the first chord struck. But as the piece grows, technique and craft take over, and imagination becomes a less useful tool. A piece grows by becoming specific. The moment Herman Melville penned the opening line, “Call me Ishmael”, one actual story — Moby Dick — began to separate itself from a multitude of imaginable others. And so on through the following five hundred-odd pages, each successive sentence in some way had to acknowledge and relate to all that preceded. Joan Didion nailed this issue squarely (and with trademark pessimism) when she said, “What’s so hard about that first sentence is that you’re stuck with it. Everything else is going to flow out of that sentence. And by the time you’ve laid down the first two sentences, your options are all gone.”

对于所有媒介来说都是一样的：空白画布上的前几笔可以满足许多可能的绘画的要求，而最后几笔只适合那幅画——它们无处可去。将想象的作品发展为实际的作品是可能性递减的过程，因为执行中的每一步都通过将一种（而且只有一种）可能性转化为现实来减少未来的选择。最后，在某个时刻，这件作品不可能是别的样子，它就完成了。

It’s the same for all media: the first few brushstrokes to the blank canvas satisfy the requirements of many possible paintings, while the last few fit only that painting — they could go nowhere else. The development of an imagined piece into an actual piece is a progression of decreasing possibilities, as each step in execution reduces future options by converting one — and only one — possibility into a reality. Finally, at some point or another, the piece could not be other than it is, and it is done.

完成的那一刻不可避免地也是一个失落的时刻——失去了想象中的作品可能采取的所有其他形式。这里具有讽刺意味的是，你制作的作品总是与你的想象、或者你能想象的其他东西、或者你正处于能够想象的边缘的东西相距一步。设计师查尔斯·伊姆斯（Charles Eames）可以说是二十世纪文艺复兴时期的典型人物，他曾经善意地抱怨说，他只投入了大约百分之一的精力来构思设计，而剩下的百分之九十九则在项目运行时坚持下去它的课程。小惊喜。毕竟，你的想象力可以自由地与未来的一百件作品赛跑，构思你可以、也许应该、也许有一天会执行的作品——但不是今天，不是在手头的作品中。今天你能做的所有工作就在你面前。你的工作是培养对可能性的想象力。

That moment of completion is also, inevitably, a moment of loss — the loss of all the other forms the imagined piece might have taken. The irony here is that the piece you make is always one step removed from what you imagined, or what else you can imagine, or what you’re right on the edge of being able to imagine. Designer Charles Eames, arguably the quintessential Renaissance Man of the twentieth century, used to complain good-naturedly that he devoted only about one percent of his energy to conceiving a design — and the remaining ninety-nine percent to holding onto it as a project ran its course. Small surprise. After all, your imagination is free to race a hundred works ahead, conceiving pieces you could and perhaps should and maybe one day will execute — but not today, not in the piece at hand. All you can work on today is directly in front of you. Your job is to develop an imagination of the possible.

事实上，一件完成的作品是对想象力和执行力之间对应关系的考验。也许令人惊讶的是，实现这种一致性的更常见的障碍不是无纪律的执行，而是无纪律的想象力。相信你的材料比实际情况更具可塑性，你的想法更引人注目，你的执行更精致，这太诱人了。正如斯坦利·库尼茨（Stanley Kunitz）曾经评论的那样：“头脑中的诗总是完美的。当你试图将其转化为语言时，阻力就开始了。”确实，大多数艺术家并没有梦想创造伟大的艺术——他们梦想的是创造伟大的艺术。哪个艺术家没有经历过创作完美的缩略图、初稿、负片或旋律时的狂热——只是一头扎进石墙，试图将诱人的暗示转化为完成的壁画、小说、照片、奏鸣曲。艺术家的生活之所以令人沮丧，并不是因为过程缓慢，而是因为他想象得很快。

A finished piece is, in effect, a test of correspondence between imagination and execution. And perhaps surprisingly, the more common obstacle to achieving that correspondence is not undisciplined execution, but undisciplined imagination. It’s altogether too seductive to approach your proposed work believing your materials to be more malleable than they really are, your ideas more compelling, your execution more refined. As Stanley Kunitz once commented, “The poem in the head is always perfect. Resistance begins when you try to convert it into language.” And it’s true, most artists don’t daydream about making great art — they daydream about having made great art. What artist has not experienced the feverish euphoria of composing the perfect thumbnail sketch, first draft, negative or melody — only to run headlong into a stone wall trying to convert that tantalizing hint into the finished mural, novel, photograph, sonata. The artist’s life is frustrating not because the passage is slow, but because he imagines it to be fast.

材料

MATERIALS

艺术材料，就像缩略图一样，以其潜力吸引着我们。纸张的质地、油漆的气味、石头的重量——都在暗示和暗示，唤起我们的幻想。有了好的材料，希望就会增长，可能性就会倍增。这是有充分理由的：有些材料非常容易充电且反应灵敏，艺术家们已经使用它们数千年了，而且可能还会继续使用它们。对于许多艺术家来说，对特定材料的反应是非常个人化的，就好像材料直接与他们说话一样。据说，小时候，巴勃罗·卡萨尔斯从听到大提琴声音的那一刻起就知道那是他的乐器。

The materials of art, like the thumbnail sketch, seduce us with their potential. The texture of the paper, the smell of the paint, the weight of the stone — all cast hints and innuendoes, beckoning our fantasies. In the presence of good materials, hopes grow and possibilities multiply. And with good reason: some materials are so readily charged and responsive that artists have turned to them for thousands of years, and probably will for thousands more. For many artists the response to a particular material has been intensely personal, as if the material spoke directly to them. It’s been said that as a child, Pablo Casals knew from the first moment he heard the sound of a cello, that that was his instrument.

但当材料具有潜力时，它们也有局限性。墨水想要流动，但不只是流过任何表面；粘土想要保持某种形状，但不仅仅是任何形状。无论如何，如果没有你的积极参与，他们的潜力就只是潜力。材料就像基本粒子：带电，但无关紧要。他们不会倾听你的幻想，也不会响应你的无聊愿望而起身行动。直截了当的事实是，它们完全按照您的双手所做的事情进行。油漆准确地放置在您放置的位置；你写下的单词——而不是你需要写或考虑写作的单词——是唯一出现在纸上的单词。用本·沙恩的话来说，“站在空画布前的画家必须从绘画的角度思考。”

But where materials have potential, they also have limits. Ink wants to flow, but not across just any surface; clay wants to hold a shape, but not just any shape. And in any case, without your active participation their potential remains just that — potential. Materials are like elementary particles: charged, but indifferent. They do not listen in on your fantasies, do not get up and move in response to your idle wishes. The blunt truth is, they do precisely what your hands make them do. The paint lays exactly where you put it; the words you wrote — not the ones you needed to write or thought about writing — are the only ones that appear on the paper. In the words of Ben Shahn, “The painter who stands before an empty canvas must think in terms of paint.”

在艺术创作中，重要的是你的头脑内容和材料质量之间的实际契合。实现这种契合所需的知识来自于注意工作时实际发生的情况——材料的反应方式，以及反应（和阻力）向您提出新想法的方式。真正重要的是那些真实而普通的变化。艺术是把东西进行出来，而材料则是可以进行的东西。因为它们是真实的，所以是可靠的。

What counts, in making art, is the actual fit between the contents of your head and the qualities of your materials. The knowledge you need to make that fit comes from noticing what really happens as you work — the way the materials respond, and the way that response (and resistance) suggest new ideas to you. It’s those real and ordinary changes that matter. Art is about carrying things out, and materials are what can be carried out. Because they are real, they are reliable.

不确定

UNCERTAINTY

事实上，你的材料是你可以合理希望控制的少数几个艺术创作元素之一。至于其他一切——嗯，条件永远不会完美，手头很少有足够的知识，关键证据总是缺失，而且支持是出了名的善变。你所做的一切都不可避免地带有不确定性——不确定你要说什么，不确定材料是否正确，不确定作品应该长还是短，甚至不确定你是否会对你所做的任何事情感到满意。摄影师杰里·尤尔斯曼 (Jerry Uelsmann) 有一次在幻灯片讲座中展示了他在一年内创作的每一张照片：大约有一百多幅作品——除了大约十幅作品外，他认为其中的所有作品都不够充分，因此在没有展出的情况下就将其销毁。在打字机出现之前的时代，托尔斯泰将《战争与和平》重写了八次，并且在它最终付诸印刷时仍在修改校样。威廉·肯尼迪勇敢地承认，他将自己的小说《腿》重写了八次，但“七次都没有好结果”。六次尤其不好。第七次的表现相当不错，尽管时间太长了。那时我儿子六岁了，我的小说也六岁了，他们身高差不多。”

Your materials are, in fact, one of the few elements of artmaking you can reasonably hope to control. As for everything else — well, conditions are never perfect, sufficient knowledge rarely at hand, key evidence always missing, and support notoriously fickle. All that you do will inevitably be flavored with uncertainty — uncertainty about what you have to say, about whether the materials are right, about whether the piece should be long or short, indeed about whether you’ll ever be satisfied with anything you make. Photographer Jerry Uelsmann once gave a slide lecture in which he showed every single image he had created in the span of one year: some hundred-odd pieces — all but about ten of which he judged insufficient and destroyed without ever exhibiting. Tolstoy, in the Age Before Typewriters, re-wrote War & Peace eight times and was still revising galley proofs as it finally rolled onto the press. William Kennedy gamely admitted that he re-wrote his own novel Legs eight times, and that “seven times it came out no good. Six times it was especially no good. The seventh time out it was pretty good, though it was way too long. My son was six years old by then and so was my novel and they were both about the same height.”

简而言之，这是正常情况。事实是，这件在完成状态下看起来如此完美的艺术品可能早些时候距离彻底崩溃只有几英寸或几秒钟的距离。林肯怀疑自己是否有能力在葛底斯堡表达需要说的话，但无论如何还是继续前进，因为他知道自己正在尽最大努力提出他需要分享的想法。总是这样。艺术就像在你知道它的结尾之前开始一个句子。风险是显而易见的：你可能永远不会说完这句话，或者说完之后，你可能什么也没说。在公开演讲中这可能不是一个好主意，但在艺术创作中却是一个极好的主意。

It is, in short, the normal state of affairs. The truth is that the piece of art which seems so profoundly right in its finished state may earlier have been only inches or seconds away from total collapse. Lincoln doubted his capacity to express what needed to be said at Gettysburg, yet pushed ahead anyway, knowing he was doing the best he could to present the ideas he needed to share. It’s always like that. Art is like beginning a sentence before you know its ending. The risks are obvious: you may never get to the end of the sentence at all-or having gotten there, you may not have said anything. This is probably not a good idea in public speaking, but it’s an excellent idea in making art.

在创作艺术时，你需要给自己空间来真实地回应你的主题和材料。艺术发生在你和某物之间——一个主题、一个想法、一项技术——而你和那件事都需要自由移动。例如，许多小说作家很早就发现，制定详细的情节轮廓是徒劳的。随着实际写作的进展，角色越来越多地呈现出自己的生活，有时甚至到了作者和最终读者一样对他们的创作所说和所做的感到惊讶的程度。劳伦斯·达雷尔 (Lawrence Durrell) 将这一过程比作在地上打建筑木桩：你种下一根木桩，向前跑 50 码，再种植另一根木桩，很快你就知道道路将朝哪个方向延伸。 EM 福斯特回忆说，当他开始写《印度之行》时，他知道马拉巴尔洞穴将在小说中扮演核心角色，那里肯定会发生一些重要的事情——只是他不确定会发生什么。

In making art you need to give yourself room to respond authentically, both to your subject matter and to your materials. Art happens between you and something — a subject, an idea, a technique — and both you and that something need to be free to move. Many fiction writers, for instance, discover early on that making detailed plot outlines is an exercise in futility; as actual writing progresses, characters increasingly take on a life of their own, sometimes to the point that the writer is as surprised as the eventual reader by what their creations say and do. Lawrence Durrell likened the process to driving construction stakes in the ground: you plant a stake, run fifty yards ahead a plant another, and pretty soon you know which way the road will run. E.M. Forster recalled that when he began writing A Passage To India he knew that the Malabar Caves would play a central role in the novel, that something important would surely happen there — it’s just that he wasn’t sure what it would be.

显然，控制并不是答案。生活中需要确定性的人不太可能创作冒险的、颠覆性的、复杂的、不确定的、暗示性的或自发的艺术。真正需要的无非是对你正在寻找的东西有一个广泛的认识，一些如何找到它的策略，以及在这个过程中接受错误和惊喜的压倒性的意愿。简而言之，艺术创作充满了偶然性——它与可预测性不能很好地结合起来。不确定性是你艺术创作欲望中必不可少的、不可避免的、无处不在的伴侣。而对不确定性的容忍是成功的先决条件。

Control, apparently, is not the answer. People who need certainty in their lives are less likely to make art that is risky, subversive, complicated, iffy, suggestive or spontaneous. What’s really needed is nothing more than a broad sense of what you are looking for, some strategy for how to find it, and an overriding willingness to embrace mistakes and surprises along the way. Simply put, making art is chancy — it doesn’t mix well with predictability. Uncertainty is the essential, inevitable and all-pervasive companion to your desire to make art. And tolerance for uncertainty is the prerequisite to succeeding.

头顶是宽阔的河流，水流湍急。桨手刚刚学习了自己的技能，他紧张地操纵着，以避免下游唯一一块打破水面的岩石，位于正中心，两边都是平稳的水流。你从岸边观看。桨手就离开了。齐格对。然后直接撞到岩石上。当你出于恐惧而行动时，你的恐惧就会成真。

AHEAD LIES A BROAD EXPANSE of river, flowing rapidly. The oarsman, only recently learning his skill, nervously maneuvers to avoid the one and only rock breaking the surface downstream, dead center, smooth current to either side. You watch from shore. The oarsman zigs left. Zigs right. And then crashes directly into the rock. When you act out of fear, your fears come true.

对艺术创作的恐惧分为两类：对自己的恐惧，以及对他人接受你的恐惧。一般来说，对自己的恐惧会阻止你尽最大努力工作，而对别人的接待的恐惧会阻止你做好自己的工作。这两个家族都有多种形式，其中一些您可能会觉得非常熟悉。试试这个采样器...

Fears about artmaking fall into two families: fears about yourself, and fears about your reception by others. In a general way, fears about yourself prevent you from doing your best work, while fears about your reception by others prevent you from doing your own work. Both families surface in many forms, some of which you may find all too familiar. Try this sampler...

假装

PRETENDING

担心自己只是假装在做艺术，这是怀疑自己的艺术资历的（容易预测的）后果。毕竟，你比任何人都更了解你的艺术中出现的许多偶然性，更不用说你所知道的所有那些源自他人的元素（甚至还有一些你从未有意但观众已将其读入你的作品中的元素）。从那里开始，你就会感觉自己只是在走走过场，成为一名艺术家。很容易想象真正的艺术家知道他们在做什么，而且他们——与你不同——有权对自己和他们的艺术感觉良好。担心自己不是真正的艺术家会导致您低估自己的作品。

The fear that you’re only pretending to do art is the (readily predictable) consequence of doubting your own artistic credentials. After all, you know better than anyone else the accidental nature of much that appears in your art, not to mention all those elements you know originated with others (and even some you never even intended but which the audience has read into your work). From there it’s only a short hop to feeling like you’re just going through the motions of being an artist. It’s easy to imagine that real artists know what they’re doing, and that they — unlike you — are entitled to feel good about themselves and their art. Fear that you are not a real artist causes you to undervalue your work.

当你的工作进展不顺利、幸福的意外没有发生或者预感没有得到回报时，鸿沟就会进一步扩大。如果你相信艺术只能由非凡的人创作，那么这样的低潮期只会证明你不是。

The chasm widens even further when your work isn’t going well, when happy accidents aren’t happening or hunches aren’t paying off. If you buy into the premise that art can be made only by people who are extra-ordinary, such down periods only serve to confirm that you aren’t.

然而，在把这一切抛到日常工作之前，请考虑一下这里的工作动态。创作艺术和观看艺术都需要持续的精力投入——大量的精力。在软弱的时刻，非凡的神话为艺术家提供了放弃尝试创作艺术的借口，也为观众提供了放弃尝试理解艺术的借口。

Before chucking it all for a day job, however, consider the dynamics at work here. Both making art and viewing art require an ongoing investment of energy — lots of energy. In moments of weakness, the myth of the extraordinary provides the excuse for an artist to quit trying to make art, and the excuse for a viewer to quit trying to understand it.

与此同时，继续创作的艺术家常常会对自己的艺术创作产生危险的自我意识。如果您怀疑这可能是一个问题，请尝试凭直觉（或自发地）工作，同时有意识地权衡您每一个行动的效果。反身艺术——向内看、以自身为主题的艺术——的日益流行，在某种程度上可能只是说明了艺术家试图将这一障碍转化为自己的优势。 “关于艺术的艺术”反过来催生了一整套艺术批评流派，其基础是艺术家不断通过他们的作品“重新定义”艺术这一明显真实（但有限）的前提。这种方法将“艺术是什么”视为一个合法的、严肃的甚至棘手的话题，却很少花精力去探讨“艺术创作是什么”的问题。

Meanwhile artists who do continue often become perilously self-conscious about their artmaking. If you doubt this could be a problem, just try working intuitively (or spontaneously) while self-consciously weighing the effect of your every action. The increasing prevalence of reflexive art — art that looks inward, taking itself as its subject — may to some degree simply illustrate attempts by artists to turn this obstacle to their advantage. Art-that’s-about-art has in turn spawned a whole school of art criticism built around the demonstrably true (but limited) premise that artists continually “re-define” art through their work. This approach treats “what art is” as a legitimate, serious and even thorny topic, but expends little energy on the question of “what art making is”.

显然这里有些东西不平衡了。毕竟，如果“什么是国际象棋”正在被重新定义，你可能会在尝试下国际象棋时感到有点不安。当然，您可以通过将自己限制在一些您认为对其他人有效的简单动作来始终坚持游戏。然后你可能会再次得出这样的结论：既然你不确定自己什么是国际象棋，那么你就不是真正的国际象棋棋手，只是在移动棋子时才假装自己是棋手。你可能会暗自相信你应该失去。事实上，您甚至可能完全退出游戏。如果说前面的场景对于国际象棋来说听起来有些牵强，那么对于艺术来说，它仍然是令人沮丧的普遍现象。

Clearly something’s come unbalanced here. After all, if there were some ongoing redefinition of “what chess is”, you’d probably feel a little uneasy trying to play chess. Of course you could always stick with the game by limiting yourself to a few easy moves you’ve seen work for others. Then again you might conclude that since you weren’t sure yourself what chess was, you weren’t a real chess player and were only faking it when you moved the pieces around. You might secretly come to believe that you deserve to lose. In fact, you might even quit playing entirely. If the preceding scenario sounds farfetched vis-a-vis chess, it remains discouragingly common vis-a-vis art.

但是，虽然您可能觉得自己只是假装自己是一名艺术家，但没有办法假装自己在创作艺术。来吧，试着一边写故事，一边假装你正在写故事。不可能。你的作品可能不是策展人想要展示的或者出版商想要出版的，但这些完全是不同的问题。你通过（除其他外）做很多不太好的工作，并逐渐淘汰那些不好的部分，那些不属于你的部分，来做出好的工作。这就是所谓的反馈，它是了解你自己的愿景的最直接途径。这也称为做你的工作。毕竟，总得有人做你的工作，而你是最亲近的人。

But while you may feel you’re just pretending that you’re an artist, there’s no way to pretend you’re making art. Go ahead, try writing a story while pretending you’re writing a story. Not possible. Your work may not be what curators want to exhibit or publishers want to publish, but those are different issues entirely. You make good work by (among other things) making lots of work that isn’t very good, and gradually weeding out the parts that aren’t good, the parts that aren’t yours. It’s called feedback, and it’s the most direct route to learning about your own vision. It’s also called doing your work. After all, someone has to do your work, and you’re the closest person around.

天赋

TALENT

用通俗的话说，人才就是“容易得到的东西”。所以迟早，不可避免地，你会到达工作并不容易的地步，而且——啊哈！，正如你所担心的那样！

Talent, in common parlance, is “what comes easily”. So sooner or later, inevitably, you reach a point where the work doesn’t come easily, and — Aha!, it’s just as you feared!

错误的。根据定义，无论您拥有什么，都正是您创作出最佳作品所需要的。担心自己有多少天赋可能是最明显的精神能量浪费——而且可能没有比这种担心更常见的了。即使对于具有相当成就的艺术家来说也是如此。

Wrong. By definition, whatever you have is exactly what you need to produce your best work. There is probably no clearer waste of psychic energy than worrying about how much talent you have — and probably no worry more common. This is true even among artists of considerable accomplishment.

天赋，如果有的话，也是一种天赋，而不是艺术家自己创造的。这个想法并不新鲜：柏拉图认为，所有艺术都是来自神的礼物，是通过在创作艺术时“失去理智”的艺术家（在柏拉图看来，字面上的意思）传达的。然而，柏拉图并不是唯一持反对意见的哲学家。虽然他的描述与德尔菲神谕者、白痴学者和某些电视布道者的运作很好地相关，但很难与大多数现实世界的事件相一致。

Talent, if it is anything, is a gift, and nothing of the artist’s own making. This idea is hardly new: Plato maintained that all art is a gift from the gods, channeled through artists who are “out of their mind” — quite literally, in Plato’s view — when making art. Plato, however, is not the only philosopher on the block; while his description correlates well with the functioning of the Oracle at Delphi, idiot savants, and certain TV evangelists, it’s difficult to reconcile with most real world events.

如果天赋是先决条件，那么艺术品越好，制作起来就越容易。但可惜的是，命运很少如此慷慨。对于每一位以优雅和速度发展出成熟视野的艺术家来说，无数其他艺术家都通过丰产期和干旱期，通过错误的开始和突破爆发，通过方向、媒介和主题的连续和重大变化，辛勤地培育他们的艺术。天赋可能会让一个人更快地起步，但如果没有方向感或奋斗目标，它就没有多大意义。世界上有很多人被赋予了伟大的天赋，有时甚至是非常华丽的天赋，但他们却从未创造出任何东西。当这种情况发生时，世界很快就会不再关心他们是否有才华。

Were talent a prerequisite, then the better the artwork, the easier it would have been to make. But alas, the fates are rarely so generous. For every artist who has developed a mature vision with grace and speed, countless others have laboriously nurtured their art through fertile periods and dry spells, through false starts and breakaway bursts, through successive and significant changes of direction, medium, and subject matter. Talent may get someone off the starting blocks faster, but without a sense of direction or a goal to strive for, it won’t count for much. The world is filled with people who were given great natural gifts, sometimes conspicuously flashy gifts, yet never produce anything. And when that happens, the world soon ceases to care whether they are talented.

即使在最好的情况下，天赋仍然是恒定的，而那些仅仅依赖天赋而没有进一步发展的人，很快就会达到顶峰，很快就会变得默默无闻。天才的例子只会强调这个真理。报纸喜欢刊登五岁音乐神童举办独奏音乐会的故事，但你很少读到有关一个人后来成为莫扎特的故事。这里的要点是，无论莫扎特最初的天赋是什么，他也是一位学会了如何创作自己的作品并因此而进步的艺术家。在这方面，他与我们其他人有着共同点。艺术家通过提高技能或获得新技能来变得更好；他们通过学习工作并从工作中学习而变得更好。他们致力于自己内心的工作，并根据这一承诺采取行动。所以当你问“那为什么这对我来说不容易呢？”时，答案可能是“因为创作艺术很难！”你最终关心的是你做了什么，而不是这件事是困难还是容易。

Even at best talent remains a constant, and those who rely upon that gift alone, without developing further, peak quickly and soon fade to obscurity. Examples of genius only accentuate that truth. Newspapers love to print stories about five-year-old musical prodigies giving solo recitals, but you rarely read about one going on to become a Mozart. The point here is that whatever his initial gift, Mozart was also an artist who learned to work on his work, and thereby improved. In that respect he shares common ground with the rest of us. Artists get better by sharpening their skills or by acquiring new ones; they get better by learning to work, and by learning from their work. They commit themselves to the work of their heart, and act upon that commitment. So when you ask, “Then why doesn’t it come easily for me?”, the answer is probably, “Because making art is hard!” What you end up caring about is what you do, not whether the doing came hard or easy.

作者试图回答（或转移）反对意见的简短题外话：
A BRIEF DIGRESSION IN WHICH THE AUTHORS ATTEMPT TO ANSWER (OR DEFLECT) AN OBJECTION:
问：你是否忽视了人们的能力存在根本差异这一事实？
Q: Aren’t you ignoring the fact that people differ radically in their abilities?
答：没有。
A: No.
问：但是，如果人与人之间存在差异，并且每个人都要做最好的工作，那么不是更有天赋的人工作得更好，而天赋较差的人工作得更少吗？
Q: But if people differ, and each of them were to make their best work, would not the more gifted make better work, and the less gifted, less?
答：是的。那不是一个适合居住的星球吗？
A: Yes. And wouldn’t that be a nice planet to live on?

才华是一个网罗，也是一个错觉。最后，关于人才的实际问题归结为：谁在乎？谁会知道？和这会有什么不同？实际的答案是：没有人，没有人，没有人。

Talent is a snare and a delusion. In the end, the practical questions about talent come down to these: Who cares? Who would know? and What difference would it make? And the practical answers are: Nobody, Nobody, and None.

完美

PERFECTION

陶艺老师在开课当天宣布，他将全班分成两组。他说，工作室左侧的所有人员将仅根据他们制作的作品数量进行评分，而右侧的所有人员将仅根据其质量进行评分。他的程序很简单：在课程的最后一天，他会带上浴室秤，称量“数量”组的工作：五十磅的罐子评级为“A”，四十磅的罐子评级为“B”，依此类推。然而，那些根据“质量”进行评分的人只需要生产一个罐子——尽管是完美的罐子——就能获得“A”。好吧，到了评分时间，一个奇怪的事实出现了：最高质量的作品都是由按数量评分的小组制作的。似乎当“数量”组忙着做大量工作并从错误中吸取教训时，“质量”组却坐着思考完美的理论，最终除了宏大的理论和成果之外，他们的努力几乎没有什么可展示的。一堆死粘土

The ceramics teacher announced on opening day that he was dividing the class into two groups. All those on the left side of the studio, he said, would be graded solely on the quantity of work they produced, all those on the right solely on its quality. His procedure was simple: on the final day of class he would bring in his bathroom scales and weigh the work of the “quantity” group: fifty pounds of pots rated an “A”, forty pounds a “B”, and so on. Those being graded on “quality”, however, needed to produce only one pot — albeit a perfect one — to get an “A”. Well, came grading time and a curious fact emerged: the works of highest quality were all produced by the group being graded for quantity. It seems that while the “quantity” group was busily churning out piles of work-and learning from their mistakes — the “quality” group had sat theorizing about perfection, and in the end had little more to show for their efforts than grandiose theories and a pile of dead clay.

如果你认为好的工作在某种程度上就是完美的工作，那么你就会遇到大麻烦。艺术是人性的；错误是人为的；因此，艺术就是错误。不可避免地，你的工作（比如，呃，前面的三段论......）将会有缺陷。为什么？因为你是一个人，只有人类，无论有什么缺点，才能创造艺术。如果没有疣，我们不清楚你会是什么样，但显然你不会成为我们中的一员。

If you think good work is somehow synonymous with perfect work, you are headed for big trouble. Art is human; error is human; ergo, art is error. Inevitably, your work (like, uh, the preceding syllogism...) will be flawed. Why? Because you’re a human being, and only human beings, warts and all, make art. Without warts it is not clear what you would be, but clearly you wouldn’t be one of us.

尽管如此，一些艺术家（以及许多前艺术家）仍然坚信，从事艺术意味着完美地做事——忽视了这一先决条件将使大多数现有艺术作品失去资格的事实。事实上，提出反原则似乎更合理，即不完美不仅是艺术中的常见成分，而且很可能是一种基本成分。安塞尔·亚当斯（Ansel Adams）从来不会将精确误认为完美，他经常想起一句古老的格言：“完美是优秀的敌人”，他的观点是，如果他等待场景中的一切都完全正确，他可能永远不会拍一张照片。

Nonetheless, the belief persists among some artists (and lots of ex-artists) that doing art means doing things flawlessly — ignoring the fact that this prerequisite would disqualify most existing works of art. Indeed, it seems vastly more plausible to advance the counter-principle, namely that imperfection is not only a common ingredient in art, but very likely an essential ingredient. Ansel Adams, never one to mistake precision for perfection, often recalled the old adage that “the perfect is the enemy of the good”, his point being that if he waited for everything in the scene to be exactly right, he’d probably never make a photograph.

亚当斯是对的：要求完美就会导致瘫痪。这种模式是可以预测的：当你看到自己所做的事情中存在错误时，你就会将工作转向你认为可以完美完成的事情。你会更加坚定地坚持自己已经知道自己能做的事情——远离风险和探索，甚至可能远离你内心的工作。你会找到拖延的理由，因为不工作就是不犯错误。相信艺术作品应该是完美的，你逐渐确信自己无法做出这样的作品。 （你是对的。） 迟早，因为你无法做你想做的事，你就会放弃。在生活中那些反常的小讽刺之一中，只有模式本身才能达到完美——一个完美的死亡螺旋：你误导了你的工作；你停滞不前；你退出了。

Adams was right: to require perfection is to invite paralysis. The pattern is predictable: as you see error in what you have done, you steer your work toward what you imagine you can do perfectly. You cling ever more tightly to what you already know you can do — away from risk and exploration, and possibly further from the work of your heart. You find reasons to procrastinate, since to not work is to not make mistakes. Believing that artwork should be perfect, you gradually become convinced that you cannot make such work. (You are correct.) Sooner or later, since you cannot do what you are trying to do, you quit. And in one of those perverse little ironies of life, only the pattern itself achieves perfection — a perfect death spiral: you misdirect your work; you stall; you quit.

要求完美就是否认你的普通（和普遍）人性，好像没有它你会过得更好。然而，这种人性是你作品的最终源泉；你的完美主义剥夺了你完成工作所需的一切。继续工作需要认识到完美本身（矛盾的是）是一个有缺陷的概念。对于阿尔伯特·爱因斯坦来说，即使是看似完美的数学结构也屈服于他的观察：“就数学定律而言，它们是不确定的；就其确定而言，它们并不涉及现实。”对于查尔斯·达尔文来说，当一代人的完美生存策略在不断变化的世界中成为其后代的负担时，进化论就显现出来了。对你来说，你下一件艺术作品的种子就埋藏在你当前作品的缺陷之中。这些不完美（或错误，如果你今天对它们感到特别沮丧的话）是你的指南——有价值的、可靠的、客观的、非评判性的指南——指导你需要重新考虑或进一步发展的事情。正是这种理想与现实之间的相互作用将你的艺术锁定在现实世界中，并赋予两者意义。

To demand perfection is to deny your ordinary (and universal) humanity, as though you would be better off without it. Yet this humanity is the ultimate source of your work; your perfectionism denies you the very thing you need to get your work done. Getting on with your work requires a recognition that perfection itself is (paradoxically) a flawed concept. For Albert Einstein, even the seemingly perfect construct of mathematics yielded to his observation that “As far as the laws of mathematics refer to reality, they are not certain; and as far as they are certain, they do not refer to reality.” For Charles Darwin, evolution lay revealed when a perfect survival strategy for one generation became, in a changing world, a liability for its offspring. For you, the seed for your next art work lies embedded in the imperfections of your current piece. Such imperfections (or mistakes, if you’re feeling particularly depressed about them today) are your guides — valuable, reliable, objective, non-judgmental guides — to matters you need to reconsider or develop further. It is precisely this interaction between the ideal and the real that locks your art into the real world, and gives meaning to both.

歼灭

ANNIHILATION

对于大多数艺术家来说，艺术创作的停滞期将是一个严重的打击。对于一些人来说，这相当于毁灭。一些艺术家对自己的作品如此认同，以至于如果他们停止创作，他们就会担心自己将一无所有——他们将不再存在。用约翰·巴斯的话来说，“这是山鲁佐德的恐惧：这种恐惧来自于将讲故事与生活、生活本身等同起来。我对这个比喻的理解深入骨髓。”

For most artists, hitting a dry spell in their artmaking would be a serious blow; for a few it would amount to annihilation. Some artists identify so closely with their own work that were they to cease producing, they fear they would be nothing — that they would cease existing. In the words of John Barth, “It’s Scheherazade’s terror: the terror that comes from the literal or metaphorical equating of telling stories with living, with life itself. I understand that metaphor to the marrow of my bones.”

有些人通过变得惊人的生产力来避免这种自我强加的深渊，他们制作的工作量甚至让亲密的朋友都感到惊讶（并且让嫉妒的同龄人感到不安！）。他们热情地工作，就好像他们着了魔一样——如果这就是阻止收割者的唯一原因，你也不会吗？

Some avoid this self-imposed abyss by becoming stupendously productive, churning out work in quantities that surprise even close friends (and positively unnerve envious peers!). They work passionately, as if they were possessed — and wouldn’t you too, if that were all that kept the Reaper at bay?

其他人也同样有动力，表现出某种严肃的专业精神：精确、不懈、狭隘地致力于艺术创作——事实上，他们可能非常擅长艺术创作。历史记录显示，安东尼·特罗洛普每周有条不紊地起草四十九页手稿——每天七页——并且非常着迷于遵守这个时间表，以至于如果他早上完成一本小说，他就会为下一本书写下书名。在一张新纸上，坚持不懈地前进，直到完成当天的配额。根据作者的个人经验，作者可以证实布雷特·韦斯顿（Brett Weston）是一个关于毁灭的虚拟案例研究，几十年来，他的家中一直在展出他的十几张照片，其中没有一张照片的拍摄时间超过六个月。

Others, no less driven, project instead a certain no-nonsense professionalism: precise, relentless, and narrowly aimed at making art — which, indeed, they may be very good at. History records that Anthony Trollope methodically drafted exactly forty-nine pages of manuscript a week — seven pages a day — and was so obsessed with keeping to that schedule that if he finished a novel in the morning he’d pen the title for his next book on a new sheet and plod relentlessly ahead until he’d completed his quota for the day. And from personal experience the authors can verify that Brett Weston, a virtual case study in annihilation, for decades maintained in his home an ongoing exhibition of a dozen or more of his photographs, none of which was ever more than six months old.

尽管如此，肯定还有很多比无法停止艺术创作更糟糕的命运。害怕毁灭的艺术家可能会把做事和有点太紧联系起来，但这实际上只是拥有太多好东西的情况。湮灭是一种存在主义的恐惧：一种常见但严重透支的恐惧，即当你停止创作艺术时，你的某些部分就会死去。这是真的。非艺术家可能不明白这一点，但艺术家自己（尤其是那些陷入困境的艺术家）却非常明白这一点。你对制造事物的需求的深度决定了不制造事物的风险程度。

Still, there must be many fates worse than the inability to stop producing art. The artist who fears annihilation may draw the connection between doing and being a little too tight, but this is really just a case of having too much of a good thing. Annihilation is an existential fear: the common — but sharply overdrawn — fear that some part of you dies when you stop making art. And it’s true. Non-artists may not understand that, but artists themselves (especially those who are stuck) understand it all too well. The depth of your need to make things establishes the level of risk in not making them.

魔法

MAGIC

“在业余爱好者、乐观主义者和傻瓜中流传着一个神话，认为在取得一定成就之后，著名艺术家就会退休到某种极乐世界，那里的批评不再会带来伤害，作品无需他们的努力就能实现。”
“There’s a myth among amateurs, optimists and fools that beyond a certain level of achievement, famous artists retire to some kind of Elysium where criticism no longer wounds and work materializes without their effort.”

— 马克·马图塞克

— Mark Matousek

 

 

在黑暗的剧院里，穿燕尾服的男人挥了挥手，一只鸽子出现了。我们称之为魔法。在阳光照射的工作室里，画家挥挥手，整个世界就形成了。我们称之为艺术。有时差异并不那么明显。想象一下，您刚刚参加了一个展览，看到了有力且连贯的作品，作品具有范围和目的。门附近的艺术家声明很清楚：这些作品完全按照艺术家的构思具体化。这项工作是不可避免的。但是等一下 - 你的工作并不感觉是不可避免的（你认为），所以你开始怀疑：也许创作艺术需要一些特殊的甚至神奇的成分，而你没有。

In a darkened theater the man in the tuxedo waves his hand and a pigeon appears. We call it magic. In a sunlit studio a painter waves her hand and a whole world takes form. We call it art. Sometimes the difference isn’t all that clear. Imagine you’ve just attended an exhibition and seen work that’s powerful and coherent, work that has range and purpose. The Artist’s Statement framed near the door is clear: these works materialized exactly as the artist conceived them. The work is inevitable. But wait a minute -your work doesn’t feel inevitable (you think), and so you begin to wonder: maybe making art requires some special or even magic ingredient that you don’t have.

“真正的”艺术拥有某种无法定义的魔力成分的信念给你带来了压力，要求你证明你的作品也包含同样的成分。错了，非常错了。要求你的工作来证明任何事情只会招致厄运。此外，如果艺术家对魔法有任何共同的看法，那很可能是一种宿命论的怀疑，即当他们自己的艺术表现良好时，这是一种侥幸——但当结果不佳时，这是一个预兆。每当其他艺术家的品质受到赞扬时，对魔法的迷恋会让你感觉自己能力下降。因此，如果批评家赞扬纳博科夫对双关语的痴迷，你就会开始担心自己甚至无法拼写“obsession”。如果克里斯托对过程的热爱得到了拥护，你就会因为自己一直讨厌清洁刷子而感到内疚。如果一些艺术史学家评论说伟大的艺术是特别肥沃的时代和地方的产物，你开始认为也许你需要搬到纽约。

The belief that “real” art possesses some indefinable magic ingredient puts pressure on you to prove your work contains the same. Wrong, very wrong. Asking your work to prove anything only invites doom. Besides, if artists share any common view of magic, it is probably the fatalistic suspicion that when their own art turns out well, it’s a fluke-but when it turns out poorly, it’s an omen. Buying into magic leaves you feeling less capable each time another artist’s qualities are praised. So if a critic praises Nabokov’s obsession with wordplay, you begin to worry that you can’t even spell “obsession”. If Christo’s love of process is championed, you feel guilty that you’ve always hated cleaning your brushes. If some art historian comments that great art is the product of especially fertile times and places, you begin to think maybe you need to move to New York.

诚然，艺术创作可能确实需要一些特殊的东西，但这种东西可能是什么仍然非常难以捉摸——难以捉摸到足以表明它可能是每个艺术家特有的东西，而不是对他们所有人来说都是通用的。 （或者甚至，也许，这只不过是艺术界对皇帝新装的变体。）但这里重要的一点不是你拥有——或没有——其他艺术家拥有的东西，而是没关系。无论他们拥有什么，都是完成他们的工作所需的东西——即使你拥有它，它也不会帮助你的工作。他们的魔法是他们的。你并不缺乏它。你不需要它。这与你无关。时期。

Admittedly, artmaking probably does require something special, but just what that something might be has remained remarkably elusive — elusive enough to suggest that it may be something particular to each artist, rather than universal to them all. (Or even, perhaps, that it’s all nothing more than the art world’s variation on The Emperor’s New Suit of Clothes.) But the important point here is not that you have — or don’t have — what other artists have, but rather that it doesn’t matter. Whatever they have is something needed to do their work — it wouldn’t help you in your work even if you had it. Their magic is theirs. You don’t lack it. You don’t need it. It has nothing to do with you. Period.

期望

EXPECTATIONS

期望徘徊在因果之间、对自我的恐惧和对他人的恐惧之间。作为高级大脑功能之一（我们的新皮质谦虚地称呼自己），期望提供了一种将想象力与计算结合起来的方法。但这是一种微妙的平衡——向某一方向倾斜太远，你的头脑中就会充满不切实际的幻想，而向另一方向倾斜太远，你就会花一生的时间来制定“待办事项”清单。

Hovering out there somewhere between cause and effect, between fears about self and fears about others, lie expectations. Being one of the higher brain functions (as our neocortex modestly calls itself), expectations provide a means to merge imagination with calculation. But it’s a delicate balance — lean too far one way and your head fills with unworkable fantasies, too far the other and you spend your life generating “To Do” lists.

更糟糕的是，期望很容易变成幻想。在最近的一次作家研讨会上，讲师英勇地努力让讨论集中在工艺问题（尚未学习）上，而作家（尚未出版）同样努力通过有关版税、电影版权和续集的问题来转移焦点。

Worse yet, expectations drift into fantasies all too easily. At a recent writers’ workshop, the instructor labored heroically to keep the discussion centered upon issues of craft (as yet unlearned), while the writers (as yet unpublished) labored equally to divert the focus with questions about royalties, movie rights and sequels.

考虑到现实的一小部分和任何程度的乐观情绪，模糊的期望都会向你低声说，工作将会飙升，它会变得容易，它会自然而然地实现。确实，有时天空会打开，工作也会自动完成。不真实的期望很容易产生，既来自情感需求，也来自对奇迹时期的希望或记忆。不幸的是，基于幻想的期望几乎总是导致幻灭。

Given a small kernel of reality and any measure of optimism, nebulous expectations whisper to you that the work will soar, that it will become easy, that it will make itself. And verily, now and then the sky opens and the work does make itself. Unreal expectations are easy to come by, both from emotional needs and from the hope or memory of periods of wonder. Unfortunately, expectations based on illusion lead almost always to disillusionment.

相反，基于作品本身的期望是艺术家拥有的最有用的工具。关于下一篇文章您需要了解的内容包含在上一篇文章中。了解材料的地方是材料的最后一次使用。了解你的执行力的地方就在你的执行力中。关于你所爱的事物的最好信息是在你最后一次接触你所爱的事物时。简而言之，你的作品就是你的指南：一本关于你的作品的完整、全面、无限的参考书。没有其他这样的书，只有你的书。它以这种方式发挥作用，不适合其他人。你的指纹遍布你的作品，只有你知道它们是如何到达那里的。你的工作告诉你你的工作方法、你的纪律、你的优点和缺点、你习惯的手势、你拥抱的意愿。

Conversely, expectations based on the work itself are the most useful tool the artist possesses. What you need to know about the next piece is contained in the last piece. The place to learn about your materials is in the last use of your materials. The place to learn about your execution is in your execution. The best information about what you love is in your last contact with what you love. Put simply, your work is your guide: a complete, comprehensive, limitless reference book on your work. There is no other such book, and it is yours alone. It functions this way for no one else. Your fingerprints are all over your work, and you alone know how they got there. Your work tells you about your working methods, your discipline, your strengths and weaknesses, your habitual gestures, your willingness to embrace.

你应该在工作中学到的教训。要看到它们，你只需要清楚地观察作品——没有判断，没有需要或恐惧，没有愿望或希望。没有情感上的期待。询问你的工作需要什么，而不是你需要什么。然后抛开恐惧，倾听，就像好父母倾听孩子的方式一样。

The lessons you are meant to learn are in your work. To see them, you need only look at the work clearly — without judgement, without need or fear, without wishes or hopes. Without emotional expectations. Ask your work what it needs, not what you need. Then set aside your fears and listen, the way a good parent listens to a child.

对他人的恐惧

FEARS ABOUT OTHERS

“不要回头——有些东西可能正在逼近你。”
“Don’t look back — something might be gaining on you.”

— 萨切尔·佩奇

— Satchel Paige

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

RT常常是在被遗弃的情况下制作的，在与我们关心的材料和想法无私融洽的时刻不请自来地出现。在这样的时刻，我们不给别人留下空间。这大概就是应该的样子。毕竟，艺术很少是从委员会中产生的。

ART IS OFTEN MADE IN ABANDONMENT, emerging unbidden in moments of selfless rapport with the materials and ideas we care about. In such moments we leave no space for others. That’s probably as it should be. Art, after all, rarely emerges from committees.

尽管其他人的反应不一定会给艺术家带来问题，但他们通常会造成问题。当我们将别人的优先事项与我们自己的优先事项混淆时，就会出现问题。我们不断地带着真实的和想象的批评者——真正的喋喋不休的声音，有些是记忆中的，有些是预言的，每个人都渴望对我们所做的一切发表评论。除此之外，甚至社会对艺术创作的普遍观念也让艺术家面临着令人瘫痪的矛盾。作为一名艺术家，您需要让每一件连续的作品都变得独特、新颖和不同，但与您早期的作品放在一起时又令人放心地熟悉。你被期望创作出亲密的（也许甚至是痛苦的）个人化的艺术——但又有吸引力，并且很容易被那些可能从未认识你个人的观众所理解。

But while others’ reactions need not cause problems for the artist, they usually do. The problems arise when we confuse others’ priorities with our own. We carry real and imagined critics with us constantly — a veritable babble of voices, some remembered, some prophesied, and each eager to comment on all we do. Beyond that, even society’s general notions about artmaking confront the artist with paralyzing contradictions. As an artist you’re expected to make each successive piece uniquely new and different — yet reassuringly familiar when set alongside your earlier work. You’re expected to make art that’s intimately (perhaps even painfully) personal - yet alluring and easily grasped by an audience that has likely never known you personally.

当工作进展顺利时，我们会避免这种内心的干扰，但在不确定或需要的时候，我们会开始倾听。当我们担心作品本身不会给我们带来我们所寻求的理解、接受和认可时，我们就会将艺术决策权让给他人。对于学术环境中的学生来说，这种麻烦几乎是必然的。你知道（而且你是对的），如果你沿着特定的路径引导你的工作，三个单位的“A”就可以是你的。在学术界之外，认可可能会被冠以更崇高的术语——批判性认可、表演、奖学金——但机制保持不变。

When the work goes well, we keep such inner distractions at bay, but in times of uncertainty or need, we begin listening. We abdicate artistic decision-making to others when we fear that the work itself will not bring us the understanding, acceptance and approval we seek. For students in academic settings, this trouble is a near certainty; you know (and you are correct) that if you steer your work along certain paths, three units of “A” can be yours. Outside academia, approval may be clothed in loftier terms — critical recognition, shows, fellowships — but the mechanism remains the same.

对于商业艺术来说，这个问题通常不那么麻烦，因为客户的批准是主要的，其他奖励是次要的。但对于大多数艺术来说，没有客户，在创作过程中，你暴露了一个你可能从未预料到的事实：通过与你所爱的事物的接触，你已经将自己暴露给了世界。你怎么能不把对那部作品的批评视为针对你个人的呢？

With commercial art this issue is often less troublesome since approval from the client is primary, and other rewards appropriately secondary. But for most art there is no client, and in making it you lay bare a truth you perhaps never anticipated: that by your very contact with what you love, you have exposed yourself to the world. How could you not take criticism of that work personally?

理解

UNDERSTANDING

我们都在年轻时就知道被视为与众不同的危险。我们了解到，其他人有能力挑选、嘲笑、拒绝或标记与众不同的人。选择你自己的记忆，但无论怎样，我们都感受到了那个想要写诗的小男孩，或者那个试图参加沙地球赛的小女孩的伤害。

We all learn at a young age the perils of being perceived as different. We learn that others have the power to single out, to ridicule, to turn away from and to mark the one who is different. Choose your own memories, but one way or another we’ve all felt the hurt of the little boy who wanted to write poems, or the little girl who tried to join the sandlot ball game.

作为一名艺术家，你会重新学习这些课程——而且要狠狠地学习。在追随你内心的道路时，你的工作很可能不会被其他人理解。至少不是立即，也不是向广大观众。当作者向计算机输入“什么有效？”这个问题时，一种奇怪的模式出现了：从制作任何给定的底片到该底片的印刷品开始销售，之间持续延迟大约五年。事实上，一部现在流行的作品首先是在评论中被转载的，以说明当时的新作品已经变得多么弱。表演艺术家面临着额外的、实时的恐惧，即当面收到对他们作品的即时裁决——就像《春之祭》巴黎首演进行到一半时，指挥家被大量腐烂的水果猛击，或者鲍勃·迪伦被轰下舞台他第一次用电吉他出现在现场。难怪艺术家常常怀有一种令人沮丧的感觉，认为他们的作品正在走下坡路：在任何特定时刻，旧的作品总是更有吸引力，总是更容易理解。

As an artist, you learn these lessons all over again — with a vengeance. In following the path of your heart, the chances are that your work will not be understandable to others. At least not immediately, and not to a wide audience. When the author fed his computer the question, “What works?”, a curious pattern emerged: a consistent delay of about five years between the making of any given negative, and the time when prints from that negative began selling. In fact, one now-popular work was first reproduced in a critical review to illustrate how much weaker the then-new work had become. Performing artists face the added, real-time terror of receiving an instant verdict on their work in person — like the conductor being pummeled with a barrage of rotten fruit halfway through the Paris premier of Rite of Spring, or Bob Dylan being hooted off the stage the first time he appeared live with an electric guitar. No wonder artists so often harbor a depressing sense that their work is going downhill: at any give moment the older work is always more attractive, always better understood.

不是很好。毕竟，想要被理解是一种基本需求——这是对你与周围每个人分享的人性的肯定。风险是可怕的：在创作真正的作品时，你赋予了观众否认你所寻求的理解的权力；你赋予他们权力说：“你和我们不一样；你不喜欢我们。”你很奇怪;你疯了。”

This is not good. After all, wanting to be understood is a basic need — an affirmation of the humanity you share with everyone around you. The risk is fearsome: in making your real work you hand the audience the power to deny the understanding you seek; you hand them the power to say, “you’re not like us; you’re weird; you’re crazy.”

诚然，他们总有可能是对的——你的工作可能提供明确的证据，证明你是与众不同的，你是孤独的。毕竟，艺术家本身很少成为常态的榜样。正如本·沙恩 (Ben Shahn) 颇为讽刺地评论道：“在客厅的墙上挂上梵高的肖像可能是一件令人非常自豪的事情，但如果客厅里有梵高本人，就会让许多忠实的艺术爱好者崩溃。 ”这样看来，关于个性美德的陈词滥调听起来显然是空洞的。你的艺术——或者你——在别人看来是多么难以理解，这可能是你不想面对的事情，至少不想那么快面对。

And admittedly, there’s always a chance they may be right — your work may provide clear evidence that you are different, that you are alone. After all, artists themselves rarely serve as role models of normalcy. As Ben Shahn rather wryly commented, “It may be a point of great pride to have a Van Gogh on the living room wall, but the prospect of having Van Gogh himself in the living room would put a great many devoted art lovers to rout.” Put that way, platitudes about the virtues of individuality sound distinctly hollow. Just how unintelligible your art — or you — appear to others may be something you don’t really want to confront, at least not all that quickly.

有时所需要的只是一段隔离期，即在你的艺术创作与你与外界分享艺术之间的一段纯粹的时间间隔。安德鲁·怀斯 (Andrew Wyeth) 多年来一直私下追求他的《赫尔加》系列，按照自己的节奏工作，远离批评和建议的聚光灯，否则该系列中每部新作品的发布都会伴随着批评和建议。这样的休息或许也能让完成的作品在艺术家的内心和思想中找到其应有的位置——简而言之，这是一个被创作者更好地理解的机会。然后，当其他人评判这项工作时，他们的反应（无论是什么）就不会那么具有威胁性。

What is sometimes needed is simply an insulating period, a gap of pure time between the making of your art, and the time when you share it with outsiders. Andrew Wyeth pursued his Helga series privately for years, working at his own pace, away from the spotlight of criticism and suggestion that would otherwise have accompanied the release of each new piece in the series. Such respites also, perhaps, allow the finished work time to find its rightful place in the artist’s heart and mind — in short, a chance to be understood better by the maker. Then when the time comes for others to judge the work, their reaction (whatever it may be) is less threatening.

相反，迎合对被误解的恐惧会让你依赖于你的听众。在最简单但最致命的情况下，想法被淡化为你想象的观众所能想象的，导致作品居高临下、傲慢，或两者兼而有之。更糟糕的是，你在这个过程中放弃了自己的最高愿景。

Conversely, catering to fears of being misunderstood leaves you dependent upon your audience. In the simplest yet most deadly scenario, ideas are diluted to what you imagine your audience can imagine, leading to work that is condescending, arrogant, or both. Worse yet, you discard your own highest vision in the process.

面对这样的压力，令人振奋的是，即使在那些以向大众观众演讲为目标的人中，也能找到当代的榜样。查尔斯·伊姆斯和雅各布·布罗诺夫斯基始终相信观众有潜力从新想法中成长并受益。埃姆斯曾经设计过一个博物馆展览，其中有一张十五英尺长的挂图（采用教科书大小的字体和同样小的图片）描绘了整个数学史。当被问到到底谁可能读完整面墙时，他平静地回答说，每个人可能会尽其所能地吸收，然后抛弃其余的内容。他补充说，其中包括一些能够在数据之间建立超出埃姆斯本人感知能力的联系的人。

In the face of such pressures, it’s heartening to find contemporary role models even among those who made it their goal to address the mass audience. Charles Eames and Jacob Bronowski consistently placed trust in the potential of their audience to grow and benefit from new ideas. Eames once designed a museum exhibit that featured a fifteen foot long wall chart (set in textbook-sized type and equally small pictures) delineating the entire history of mathematics. When asked who on earth would possibly read the whole wall, he calmly replied that each person would probably absorb about as much as he/she were able to, and just slough off the rest. And, he added, that would include some who would make connections between the data beyond what Eames himself could perceive.

验收

ACCEPTANCE

对于艺术家来说，接受的问题始于一个简单而令人难以忘怀的问题：当你的作品被计算时，它会被算作艺术吗？这是一个基本问题，其根源可以追溯到童年时期。 （还记得那些可怕的操场仪式吗？如果你不是第一个被选入垒球队的人，你会感觉很糟糕，但宁愿死也不愿根本不被选中？）

For the artist, the issue of acceptance begins as one simple, haunting question: When your work is counted, will it be counted as art? It’s a basic question, with antecedents stretching back to childhood. (Remember those dreaded playground rituals, when you’d feel badly enough if you weren’t the first one chosen for the softball team, but would rather die than not be chosen at all?)

如果接受的需要是让你的作品被接受为艺术，那么随之而来的恐惧就是发现它被视为工艺品、爱好、装饰——或者根本什么都不是。 1937 年，当博蒙特·纽霍尔 (Beaumont Newhall) 撰写第一篇关于摄影史的实质性叙述时（标题很合乎逻辑，《摄影史》），他挑选了一些艺术家来赞扬或批评。事实证明，被纽霍尔的书伤害的摄影师并不是那些被他诅咒的摄影师，而是那些被他完全排除在外的摄影师。在公众心目中，前者至少成为“摄影史”的一部分，而后者则彻底不复存在！事实上，几十年过去了，一些才华横溢的“外来者”才开始因其早期的工作而受到认可。这个例子很极端，但一般性的警告仍然适用：接受和批准是其他人所拥有的权力，无论他们是朋友、同学、策展人……还是你所选择的媒介的权威历史的作者。

If the need for acceptance is the need to have your work accepted as art, then the accompanying fear is finding it dismissed as craft, hobby, decoration — or as nothing at all. In 1937, when Beaumont Newhall wrote the first substantive account of the history of photography (titled, logically enough, The History of Photography), he picked a select number of artists to praise or criticize. As it turned out, the photographers hurt by Newhall’s book were not those he damned, but those he left out entirely. In the public’s mind, the former at least became part of “the history of photography”, while the latter ceased to exist entirely! Literally decades passed before some talented “outsies” began receiving recognition for the work they produced in those early days. That example is extreme, but the general caveat still applies: acceptance and approval are powers held by others, whether they be friends, classmates, curators...or author of the definitive history of your chosen medium.

在某些时候，接受的需要很可能与完成自己工作的需要发生正面冲突。这太糟糕了，因为这个请求本身看起来很合理：你想做你自己的工作，并且你希望得到接受。这是牛仔和山人的歌谣，是艺术完整性的神话，也是《芝麻街》：唱出你内心的歌，世界迟早会接受并奖励真实的声音。精疲力尽的老练者嘲笑这种信念，但通常还是会和其他人一起接受它。

At some point the need for acceptance may well collide head-on with the need to do your own work. It’s too bad, since the request itself seems so reasonable: you want to do your own work, and you want acceptance for that. It’s the ballad of the cowboy and the mountain man, the myth of artistic integrity and Sesame Street: sing the song of your heart, and sooner or later the world will accept and reward the authentic voice. Jaded sophisticates laugh at this belief, but usually buy into it along with everyone else anyway.

在非艺术世界中，这种信仰体系是美国梦和中年危机背后的驱动机制。在艺术界，它是防止幻灭的主要缓冲剂。毕竟，世界（在很大程度上）确实奖励真正的工作。问题不是绝对的，而是暂时的：当你的奖励到达时，你可能不再能够领取它。问舒伯特。

In the non-art world, this belief system is a driving mechanism behind the American Dream - and the Mid-Life Crisis. In the art world, it’s a primary buffer against disillusionment. After all, the world does (in large measure) reward authentic work. The problem is not absolute, but temporal: by the time your reward arrives, you may no longer be around to collect it. Ask Schubert.

对此有一个相当简单的解释：在任何特定时刻，世界都会为其已经理解的作品（即已经存在了一代人或一个世纪的艺术）提供更多的支持。真正新思想的表达往往连糟糕的艺术都算不上——它们根本就被视为根本不是艺术。斯特拉文斯基的《火鸟》如今被认为是二十世纪交响乐作品中旋律最为丰富的作品之一，但在首次演出时却被认为纯粹是不和谐而被拒绝。罗伯特·弗兰克的《美国人》现在被认为是美国摄影的一个开创性的转折点，在其出版时，媒体和公众基本上忽视了它，因为他们无法解读其黑暗而坚韧的视觉。这是一个沉闷的传统：从阿杰特到维吉的艺术家在他们的大部分职业生涯中都被忽视，因为他们创作的作品不符合既定的艺术定义。

There’s a fairly straightforward explanation for this: at any given moment, the world offers vastly more support to work it already understands — namely, art that’s already been around for a generation or a century. Expressions of truly new ideas often fail to qualify as even bad art — they’re simply viewed as no art at all. Stravinski’s Firebird, today considered one of the more lushly melodic of twentieth century symphonic pieces, was rejected as sheer cacophony when first performed. Robert Frank’s The Americans, now considered a seminal turning point in American photography, was at the time of its publication largely ignored by a press and public that couldn’t decipher its dark and gritty vision. It’s a dreary tradition: artists from Atget to Weegee were ignored through most of their careers because the work they produced didn’t fit within the established definition of art.

对于艺术家来说，困境似乎是显而易见的：探索新世界可能会面临被拒绝的风险，而遵循已探索好的道路则可能会赢得别人的认可。不用说，后一种策略是压倒性的选择药物，其中接受是主要目标。做出看起来像艺术的作品，人们会自动接受。

For the artist, the dilemma seems obvious: risk rejection by exploring new worlds, or court acceptance by following well-explored paths. Needless to say, the latter strategy is the overwhelming drug of choice where acceptance is the primary goal. Make work that looks like art, and acceptance is automatic.

然而，令人惊讶的是，这并不总是坏事。至少对于见习者来说，一段时间的艺术重演是不可避免的，而且在大多数情况下是有益的。从智力和技术的角度来看，明智的做法是与你的艺术遗产保持良好的关系，以免你多次投入重复发明轮子的工作。但一旦考虑到这一点，更大的危险不是艺术家无法从过去学到任何东西，而是无法向未来传授任何新东西。

Surprisingly, however, this is not always a bad thing. At least for the novitiate, some period of artistic recapitulation is both inevitable and, by most accounts, beneficial. On both intellectual and technical grounds, it’s wise to remain on good terms with your artistic heritage, lest you devote several incarnations to re-inventing the wheel. But once having allowed for that, the far greater danger is not that the artist will fail to learn anything from the past, but will fail to teach anything new to the future.

最近的摄影史提供了一个教科书般的例子，说明成功本身可能会阻碍艺术持续发展的道路。在本世纪的前三分之一，爱德华·韦斯顿、安塞尔·亚当斯和一些同行彻底颠覆了当时盛行的柔焦摄影艺术世界。他们通过发展一种视觉哲学来实现这一目标，该哲学证明了清晰聚焦的图像的合理性，并将自然景观作为摄影艺术的主题。他们的观点花了几十年的时间才渗透到公众意识中，但现在确实已经渗透到了公众意识中：从香烟广告到塞拉俱乐部书籍中出现的图片，要归功于那些曾经引起争议的图片，它们现在被接受了。事实上，这种愿景已经如此普遍地成为我们的愿景，以至于今天人们拍摄度假风景时常常希望，如果一切都恰到好处，那么结果将不仅仅是看起来像风景，而是看起来像安塞尔·亚当斯拍摄的风景照片。 。

Recent photo history offers a textbook example of the perils that success itself can lay in the path of continued artistic growth. In the first third of this century, Edward Weston, Ansel Adams and a few fellow travellers turned the then-prevailing world of soft-focus photographic art upside down. They did so by developing a visual philosophy that justified sharply-focused images, and introduced the natural landscape as a subject for photographic art. It took decades for their viewpoint to filter into public consciousness, but it sure has now: pictures appearing in anything from cigarette ads to Sierra Club books owe their current acceptance to those once-controversial images. Indeed, that vision has so pervasively become ours that people photographing vacation scenery today often do so with the hope that if everything turns out just right, the result will not simply look like a landscape, it will look like an Ansel Adams photograph of the landscape.

当然，这也会过去。其实从艺术上来说，它已经过去了。随着时间的推移，一个伟大想法的展开就像分形晶体的生长一样，让细节和改进不断地增加——但规模却不断缩小。最终（也许到了 1960 年代初）那些挺身而出、扛起西海岸风景摄影大旗的人并不是在创作艺术，而是在再现艺术史。两三代人与产生他们所拥护的愿景的力量分离了，他们只能拍摄自己从未真正拥有过的经历。如果您发现自己陷入类似的情况，我们谦虚地提供一点牛仔智慧：当您的马死后，下车。

This too will pass, of course. In fact, artistically speaking, it has passed. The unfolding over time of a great idea is like the growth of a fractal crystal, allowing details and refinements to multiply endlessly — but only in ever-decreasing scale. Eventually (perhaps by the early 1960’s) those who stepped forward to carry the West Coast Landscape Photography banner were not producing art, so much as re-producing the history of art. Separated two or three generations from the forces that spawned the vision they championed, they were left making images of experiences they never quite had. If you find yourself caught in similar circumstances, we modestly offer this bit of cowboy wisdom: When your horse dies, get off.

尽管有牛仔智慧，韦斯顿/亚当斯的愿景即使在今天仍然继续支持着规模庞大的艺术家和教师家庭手工业。但这种安全感是有代价的：冒险行为受到阻碍，艺术发展受到阻碍，个人风格升华以适应预先存在的模式。只有那些致力于走自己的艺术道路的人才能回顾并清晰地看待这个问题：关于接受的真正问题不是你的作品是否会被视为艺术，而是它是否会被视为你的艺术。

Cowboy wisdom notwithstanding, the Weston/ Adams vision continues to support a sizable cottage industry of artists and teachers even today. But this security carries a price: risk-taking is discouraged, artistic development stunted, and personal style sublimated to fit a pre-existing mold. Only those who commit to following their own artistic path can look back and see this issue in clear perspective: the real question about acceptance is not whether your work will be viewed as art, but whether it will be viewed as your art.

赞同

APPROVAL

接受和批准之间的区别很微妙，但又很明显。接受意味着你的工作被视为真实的东西；认可意味着有人喜欢它。

The difference between acceptance and approval is subtle, but distinct. Acceptance means having your work counted as the real thing; approval means having people like it.

收到其中之一而没有收到另一个的情况并不罕见。诺曼·洛克威尔（Norman Rockwell）的作品在他活着的时候非常受欢迎，但很少受到评论家的尊重。一两代人之前，人们普遍认为约翰·辛格·萨金特很优秀，但由于各种原因，他的作品并没有真正发挥作用。另一方面，每一季都会有一小部分电影和戏剧获得好评如潮，同时也可能成为票房灾难。

It’s not unusual to receive one without the other. Norman Rockwell’s work was enormously well-liked during his lifetime, but received little critical respect. A generation or two earlier there was widespread agreement that John Singer Sargent was good, but that for various reasons his work didn’t really count. On the flip side, every season brings a small bundle of films and plays that garner rave critical reviews while on their way to becoming box office disasters.

这种二分法的存在是不可否认的。它是否需要具有破坏性是一个悬而未决的问题。很明显，接受和认可都是与受众相关的问题。在健康的环境中，好的工作会得到认可；如果你唯一的验证是内部的，那么社会就失败了。听起来很简单，但社会并不是一个整体——它包含着许多环境，有些对艺术家是压制性的，有些是支持性的。对于那些在对抗中茁壮成长的艺术家来说，拒绝不是问题，但对于许多其他人来说，持续的磨损会造成损失。对于这些艺术家来说，生存意味着找到一个重视艺术、鼓励艺术创作的环境。

That this dichotomy exists is undeniable; whether it need be destructive is an open question. Both acceptance and approval are, quite plainly, audience-related issues. In a healthy environment, good work would get recognition; if your only validation is internal, society has failed. Sounds straightforward enough, but society is hardly a monolith — it harbors many environments, some repressive to the artist, others supportive. For artists who thrive on confrontation, rejection is not a problem, but for many others the constant wear and tear takes a toll. For those artists, survival means finding an environment where art is valued and artmaking encouraged.

在一个支持性的环境中——人们常常发现，在艺术界本身——认可和接受往往是联系在一起的，甚至是难以区分的。埃德·拉斯查（Ed Ruscha）的评论“只有艺术家和黑客”或詹姆斯·瑟伯（James Thurber）的观察“没有好艺术或坏艺术之类的东西”代表了这个经过精心挑选的观众的操作标准。只有艺术——而且少得可怜！”

In a supportive environment — one found, more often than not, within the artistic community itself — approval and acceptance often become linked, even indistinguishable. The operative criteria for this rather select audience is typified by Ed Ruscha’s remark, “There are only artists and hacks,” or James Thurber’s observation, “There’s no such thing as good art or bad art. There’s only Art — and damn little of it!”

但请预先警告：这种方法可能很严厉。有一个故事（也许是杜撰的），大师被要求对 20 名年轻钢琴家的比赛进行评判，按照从 1 到 100 的等级对他们的表现进行评分。后来，他的计分表显示，他给两位钢琴家打了满分，而给其余的打了零分。当赞助商提出抗议时，他直言不讳地回答说：“要么你能打，要么你不能。”

But be forewarned: this approach can be harsh. There’s a story (perhaps apocryphal) of the Master who was asked to judge a competition for twenty young pianists by rating their performance on a scale of 1-to-100. Afterwards, his tally sheet revealed he had awarded two pianists a perfect hundred — and given the rest a zero. When the sponsors protested, he replied bluntly, “Either you can play or you can’t.”

电影制片人卢·斯托曼 (Lou Stoumen) 向著名教师兼电影理论家斯拉夫科·沃尔卡皮奇 (Slavko Vorkapitch) 讲述了他将自己的第一部电影（在他还是学生时制作的）交给著名教师和电影理论家斯拉夫科·沃尔卡皮奇 (Slavko Vorkapitch) 的痛苦而又真实的故事。老师默默地看完了整部影片，观看结束后，一言不发地起身离开了教室。斯托曼有点震惊，他跑出去问道：“但是你觉得我的电影怎么样？”

Filmmaker Lou Stoumen tells the painfully un-apocryphal story about hand-carrying his first film (produced while he was still a student) to the famed teacher and film theorist Slavko Vorkapitch. The teacher watched the entire film in silence, and as the viewing ended rose and left the room without uttering a word. Stoumen, more than a bit shaken, ran out after him and asked, “But what did you think of my film?”

沃卡皮奇回答道：“什么电影？”

Replied Vorkapitch, “What film?”

这里的教训很简单，争取认可，即使是同龄人的认可，也会将危险的权力置于观众手中。更糟糕的是，观众很少能够对一个真正重要的问题给予（或拒绝）批准，即你的工作是否取得进展。他们可以很好地评论成品如何感动（或挑战或娱乐），但对您的过程知之甚少或不感兴趣。观众稍后到来。唯一纯粹的沟通是在你和你的工作之间。

The lesson here is simply that courting approval, even that of peers, puts a dangerous amount of power in the hands of the audience. Worse yet, the audience is seldom in a position to grant (or withhold) approval on the one issue that really counts — namely, whether or not you’re making progress in your work. They’re in a good position to comment on how they’re moved (or challenged or entertained) by the finished product, but have little knowledge or interest in your process. Audience comes later. The only pure communication is between you and your work.

对于我们是否能实现内心愿望的外在表现，世界表现出完美的中立态度。但不是艺术。艺术具有敏锐的反应力。没有什么地方的反馈比艺术创作更绝对。我们所做的工作，即使没有被世界注意到和不受欢迎，也与我们投入或保留的一切完美和谐地振动。外面的世界也许对我们所做的事情没有任何反应；在我们的艺术作品中，只有反应。

THE WORLD DISPLAYS PERFECT NEUTRALITY on whether we achieve any outward manifestation of our inner desires. But not art. Art is exquisitely responsive. Nowhere is feedback so absolute as in the making of art. The work we make, even if unnoticed and undesired by the world, vibrates in perfect harmony to everything we put into it — or withhold from it. In the outside world there maybe no reaction to what we do; in our artwork there is nothing but reaction.

这种反应令人惊叹的奇妙之处在于它的真实性。看看你的作品，它会告诉你当你退缩或拥抱时会是什么样子。当你懒惰时，你的艺术也懒惰；当你懒惰时，你的艺术也懒惰。当你退缩时，它也会退缩；当你犹豫时，它会站在那里盯着你，双手插在口袋里。但当你做出承诺时，它就会像火焰一样燃烧起来。

The breathtakingly wonderful thing about this reaction is its truthfulness. Look at your work and it tells you how it is when you hold back or when you embrace. When you are lazy, your art is lazy; when you hold back, it holds back; when you hesitate, it stands there staring, hands in its pockets. But when you commit, it comes on like blazes.

最近，出于纯粹的乐趣，一位颇有成就的视觉艺术家开始跳舞。她从来没有体验过如此纯粹的身体艺术形式，全身心投入其中。她的参与变得更加激烈：更多的课程、更多的练习、更多的投入、更长的时间。她表现出色。几个月后的一天，她的导师让她考虑加入一个表演团。她愣住了。她的舞蹈崩溃了。她变得僵硬、难为情。她变得严肃起来，或者说以另一种方式严肃起来。她觉得自己不够好，她的舞蹈也不够好。她感到非常沮丧和沮丧，以至于她不得不辞职几周来解决问题。最近，回到新的但不稳定的领域工作，她必须教会自己享受在她以前热衷于自己的艺术领域为他人努力工作的乐趣。

Recently, out of pleasure alone, an accomplished visual artist took up dance. Never before experiencing an artform so purely physical, she threw herself into it. Her involvement became intense: more classes, more practice, more commitment, longer hours. She excelled. Then one day several months into it, her instructor asked her to consider joining a performing troupe. She froze. Her dancing fell apart. She became stiff and self-conscious. She got serious, or serious in a different way. She didn’t feel she was good enough, and her dancing promptly was not good enough. She got frustrated and depressed enough that she had to quit for a few weeks to sort things out. More recently, back to work on new but shaky ground, she’s having to teach herself to enjoy working hard for others at the art she previously enjoyed passionately for herself.

在理想的——也就是说，真实的——艺术家中，恐惧不仅继续存在，而且与补充恐惧的欲望并存，也许驱动恐惧，当然也滋养恐惧。天真的热情，促进在无视障碍的情况下完成工作，有了勇气，就变成了知情的热情，促进在完全接受这些障碍的情况下完成工作。

In the ideal — that is to say, real — artist, fears not only continue to exist, they exist side by side with the desires that complement them, perhaps drive them, certainly feed them. Naive passion, which promotes work done in ignorance of obstacles, becomes — with courage — informed passion, which promotes work done in full acceptance of those obstacles.

这些障碍中最重要的是不确定性。我们都知道艺术作品在其不确定性中产生的感觉。音乐以其密集的结构和内在的抽象性提供了最清晰的例子。在演奏真正美妙的音乐时，音乐线的位置和我们知道它需要走向的方向之间存在着持续的紧张关系。我们（暂时）不确定如何解决神游，即使（同时）我们知道它会如此。更难以描述的是艺术家创作作品时的心态。大多数艺术家都会在手边准备一份经过精心排练的演讲，以应对解释成品作品的常见要求。但如果被要求描述艺术创作过程中的感受——嗯，这常常有点像多萝西试图向艾姆阿姨描述奥兹国。在最初的想法和最终的作品之间存在着一条鸿沟，我们可以看到，但从未完全绘制出来。艺术创作中真正特殊的时刻在于概念转化为现实的时刻——跨越鸿沟的时刻。精确的描述是不可能的，但它与一种美妙的状态联系在一起，在这种状态下，作品似乎是在自我创造，而艺术家只是充当引导者或调解者，让一切成为可能。

Foremost among those obstacles is uncertainty. We all know the feeling of finished art that rides from within its uncertainties. Music, with its dense structure and built-in abstraction, offers the clearest examples. In performances of really wonderful music there’s an ongoing tension between where the musical line is, and where we know it needs to go. We’re uncertain (momentarily) just how the fugue can be resolved, even as (simultaneously) we know it will be. What is more difficult to describe is the state of mind held by the artist while working on the piece. Most artists keep a well-rehearsed speech close at hand for fielding the familiar request to explain a finished piece. But if asked to describe how it felt during the artmaking — well, that often comes out a bit like Dorothy trying to describe the Land of Oz to Auntie Em. Between the initial idea and the finished piece lies a gulf we can see across, but never fully chart. The truly special moments in artmaking lie in those moments when concept is converted to reality — those moments when the gulf is being crossed. Precise descriptions fail, but it connects to that wonderful condition in which the work seems to make itself and the artist serves only as guide or mediator, allowing all things to be possible.

总而言之，在大多数艺术事务中，作为一个创作者比作为一个观众更有营养。但并非在所有问题上。当谈到我们可以有效参与的艺术范围时，艺术观众可以自由获得的一些好处对艺术创作者来说却是遥不可及的。作为一个听众，你可以通过巴赫 B 小调弥撒的演奏而达到真正的狂喜和宣泄，但作为一个创作者，你甚至无法创作出最琐碎的正宗巴洛克音乐。作为一名观众，你可以感受到平原印第安药包的存在，但作为一名二十世纪的艺术家，你无法自己开始制作。

All things considered, in most matters of art it is more nourishing to be a maker than a viewer. But not in all matters. When it comes to the range of art we can usefully engage, some benefits that flow freely to art viewers remain tantalizingly inaccessible to art makers. As a listener you can be transported to authentic ecstasy and catharsis by a performance of the Bach B-minor Mass, but as a maker you cannot compose even the most trivial piece of authentic baroque music. As a viewer you can feel the charge in the presence of the Plains Indian medicine bundle, but as a twentieth century artist you could not begin to make one yourself.

作为观众，你的影响力远远大于作为创造者的影响力。你能体验到的艺术可能起源于一千英里之外或一千年前，但你能创作的艺术却与你生活的时代和地点有着不可逆转的联系。受到你所立足的基础的限制。如果没有对十字架的象征意义和天堂的承诺的广泛共同信仰，欧洲大教堂的十字形设计和高耸的尖塔就毫无意义。纵观浩瀚的历史，只有在短暂的时刻，特定的事件和信仰才具有迫使我们建造大教堂或写赋格曲的力量。同样，同样狭窄（尽管不同）的信念范围很可能驱动着我们此时此刻真正可用的一切。具有决定性意义的艺术作品直接参与其创作者周围的历史结构。简而言之，你必须在那里。

Your reach as a viewer is vastly greater than your reach as a maker. The art you can experience may have originated a thousand miles away or a thousand years ago, but the art you can make is irrevocably bound to the times and places of your life. Limited by the very ground on which you stand. Without a broadly shared belief in the symbolism of the Cross and the promise of Heaven above, the cruciform design and towering spires of the great European cathedrals would have made no sense whatsoever. Seen against the vast sweep of history, it is only for brief moments that particular events and beliefs carry the power to compel us to build cathedrals or write fugues. And it’s just as likely that a similarly narrow (albeit different) range of beliefs drives all that is authentically available to us in this moment. Decisive works of art participate directly in the fabric of history surrounding their maker. Simply put, you have to be there.

令人惊讶（也可能令人不安）的推论是，我们并没有从艺术的感动中学到太多关于艺术创作的知识。艺术创作受到我们所处位置的限制，而我们作为观众所拥有的艺术体验并不能可靠地指导我们所处的位置。作为观众，我们很容易体验到我们无法站立的地面的力量——但正是这种体验是如此引人注目，以至于我们可能会感到几乎光荣地必须创作出重新夺回这种力量的艺术。或者更危险的是，想要使用激起我们热情的作品中出现的相同技巧、相同主题、相同符号——实际上是借用来自另一个时间和地点的电荷。

The surprising (and probably disturbing) corollary to this is that we don’t learn much about making art from being moved by it. Making art is bound by where we are, and the experience of art we have as viewers is not a reliable guide to where we are. As viewers we readily experience the power of ground on which we cannot stand — yet that very experience can be so compelling that we may feel almost honor-bound to make art that recaptures that power. Or more dangerously, feel tempted to use the same techniques, the same subjects, the same symbols as appear in the work that aroused our passion — to borrow, in effect, a charge from another time and place.

追踪这种欲望的根源并不难：我们最个人的历史保存着专注于令人回味的工作的水晶般的记忆。有时，这样的时刻是我们成为艺术家的部分原因，而那些感动我们的作品也具有英雄般的重要性。当我第一次看到爱德华·韦斯顿的印刷品时，我只记得一瞬间的事。当我走在通往加州大学洛杉矶分校图书馆善本室的昏暗走廊上时，我抬起头，看到了这张照片。我停下脚步。困惑。这与我见过的任何其他东西都不一样。它比其他照片，特别是我的照片，……有更多……意义。它的种类不同。在那一瞬间，我的内心不由自主地形成了一种区别——世界上现在有两种照片：墙上挂在我面前的那张，以及其他所有的照片。

It’s not hard to track the source of such desire: our most personal histories hold crystalline memories of absorption into evocative work. Sometimes such moments are part of why we become artists, and the works that moved us take on heroic importance. I can remember to within one heartbeat the moment I first saw an Edward Weston print. As I was walking the dim hallway that leads to the Rare Book Room of the UCLA Library, I glanced up — and saw this photograph. I stopped walking. Confusion. It was unlike anything else I had seen. It was so much more....something...than other photographs, particularly my photographs. It was different in kind. In that instant an unbidden distinction formed in my gut — there were now two kinds of photographs in the world: the one before me on the wall, and all the rest.

那张照片是我体验的。但无论是它还是类似的东西都不是我制作的。然而，我花了十年的时间才消除了一种痛苦的感觉，即我的工作应该做那些工作已经完成的事情。又过了很多年，我才开始质疑这种艺术的力量在哪里：在创作者身上？在艺术品中？在查看器中？

That photograph was mine to experience. But neither it, nor anything like it, was mine to make. Yet it took a decade to dispel the gnawing feeling that my work should do what that work had done. And more years still before I thought to question where the power of such art resided: In the maker? In the artwork? In the viewer?

事实上，如果在任何给定的时间内只有某种类型的工作与生活产生共鸣，那么这就是你在那一刻需要做的工作。如果你尝试做其他工作，你就会错过你的时刻。事实上，我们自己的作品与时间和地点有着千丝万缕的联系，以至于我们甚至无法重新捕捉到我们自己过去时代的审美基础。如果可以的话，尝试重新占据几年前甚至几个月前的审美空间。不可能。你只能全力以赴，即使这会带来苦乐参半的认识，即你已经完成了最好的工作。

If, indeed, for any given time only a certain sort of work resonates with life, then that is the work you need to be doing in that moment. If you try to do some other work, you will miss your moment. Indeed, our own work is so inextricably tied to time and place that we cannot recapture even our own aesthetic ground of past times. Try, if you can, to reoccupy your own aesthetic space of a few years back, or even a few months. There is no way. You can only plunge ahead, even when that carries with it the bittersweet realization that you have already done your very best work.

在早期，这种高度的自我意识很少成为问题，因为艺术家（以及其他所有人）的文化根基深深地交织在一起，这似乎是不言而喻的。艺术品所体现的意义和区别是日常生活结构的一部分，艺术问题与所有其他问题之间的距离很小。当艺术家的作品涵盖从教堂的圣像到家庭用具的一切时，所有人口都算作观众。在两千二百年前的希腊圆形剧场，欧里庇得斯的戏剧作为当代戏剧在一万四千名观众面前上演。今天不是这样。

This heightened self-consciousness was rarely an issue in earlier times when it seemed self-evident that the artist (and everyone else, for that matter) had roots deeply intertwining their culture. Meanings and distinctions embodied within artworks were part of the fabric of everyday life, and the distance from art issues to all other issues was small. The whole population counted as audience when artists’ work encompassed everything from icons for the Church to utensils for the home. In the Greek amphitheater twenty-two hundred years ago, the plays of Euripides’ were performed as contemporary theatre before an audience of fourteen thousand. Not so today.

如今，艺术问题在很大程度上已经完全成为艺术家的关注点，脱离了更大的社区，并被更大的社区所忽视。如今，艺术家常常不再关注生活中的时代和地点，而是选择参与艺术的时代和地点，以应对智力上的挑战。但这是一个人造建筑，从画廊门口开始到结束。除了《艺术论坛》的读者之外，很少有人因为试图将性别中立的生物形态解构主义融入个人生活而失眠。正如亚当·戈普尼克在《纽约客》上所说，“后现代主义艺术首先是后观众艺术。”

Today art issues have for the most part become solely the concern of artists, divorced from — and ignored by — the larger community. Today artists often back away from engaging the times and places of their life, choosing instead the largely intellectual challenge of engaging the times and places of Art. But it’s an artificial construct that begins and ends at the gallery door. Apart from the readership of Artforum, remarkably few people lose sleep trying to incorporate gender-neutral biomorphic deconstructivism into their personal lives. As Adam Gopnik remarked in The New Yorker, “Post-modernist art is, above all, post-audience art.”

在这样的背景下，艺术家们努力寻找对人类有影响的材料。在迫在眉睫的无关紧要的压力下，他们可能开始用从其他地方和时间“挪用”的带电粒子填充他们的画布和监视器。就好像艺术本身赋予了它的主题普遍性，就好像在艺术中所有物体都自动保留了它们的力量——就好像你可以将平原印第安药包的力量融入到你的作品中。或者令人信服地完成舒伯特未完成交响曲的结束乐章。事实上，今天你可以找到城市白人艺术家——他们在一百英尺外无法可靠地区分土狼和德国牧羊犬——随意地将骗子土狼的形象融入到他们的作品中。所有这些努力的共同前提是可以跨越空间和时间借用权力。这不可以。所体现的意义和所引用的意义之间存在差异。正如有人曾经说过的，除了希腊渔夫之外，没有人应该戴希腊渔夫帽。

In such a setting artists strain to find material of any human consequence. Under pressure of impending irrelevance, they may begin to fill their canvasses and monitors with charged particles “appropriated” from other places and times. It is as though art itself confers universality upon its subject, as though in art all objects automatically retain their power — as though you could incorporate the power of the Plains Indian medicine bundle into your work. Or convincingly complete the closing movements to Schubert’s Unfinished Symphony. Today, indeed, you can find urban white artists — people who could not reliably tell a coyote from a german shepherd at a hundred feet — casually incorporating the figure of Coyote the Trickster into their work. A premise common to all such efforts is that power can be borrowed across space and time. It cannot. There’s a difference between meaning that is embodied and meaning that is referenced. As someone once said, no one should wear a Greek fisherman’s hat except a Greek fisherman.

如果你像我们认识的大多数艺术家一样，你可能习惯于在很长一段时间内看着你的作品顺利展开，直到有一天——没有明显的原因——事实并非如此。这种意想不到的裂痕是司空见惯的，甚至达到了陈词滥调的地步，但艺术家们通常将每一个重复出现的实例视为他们个人失败的阴暗证据。连续艺术家放克中的主角提名者是：（1）你已经完全没有新想法了，或者（2）你一直在走一条毫无价值的死胡同。获胜者是：（幸运的是）两者都不是。艺术创作最保守的秘密之一是，新想法发挥作用的频率远低于实用想法——这些想法可以重复使用成千种变体，为整个作品而不是单件作品提供框架。同样，担心自己一直遵循错误的想法只是对事情本来可能的方式的常见幻想的负面变体。未采取的道路的承诺是，我们的工作实际上比表面上看起来的要多，如果事情稍微有所不同，它就会表现得更好。面对一件令人失望的作品，人们不知何故想要否认它，并说：“那不是我想要做的；这不是我想要的。”我应该把它做得更大，或者更小；如果我有更多的时间或金钱，或者没有使用那种愚蠢的绿色油漆就好了……”我们都想摆脱这个，但不可否认的事实是，你的艺术并不是你创作时留下的残留物。减去所有你还没有做的事情——这是你已经做过的所有事情的全部回报。人们不妨放纵一下，回去为上周的彩票挑选更好的号码。

If you’re like most artists we know, you’re probably accustomed to watching your work unfold smoothly enough for long stretches of time, until one day — for no immediately apparent reason — it doesn’t. Hitting that unexpected rift is commonplace to the point of cliche, yet artists commonly treat each recurring instance as somber evidence of their own personal failure. Nominees for Leading Role in a Continuing Artists’ Funk are: (1) you’ve entirely run out of new ideas forever, or (2) you’ve been following a worthless deadend path the whole time. And the winner is: (fortunately) neither. One of the best kept secrets of artmaking is that new ideas come into play far less frequently than practical ideas — ideas that can be re-used for a thousand variations, supplying the framework for a whole body of work rather than a single piece. And likewise, fear that you’ve been following the wrong ideas is merely the downside variant of common fantasies about the way things could have been. The promise of paths not taken is that our work is really more than it appears, that it would shine through better if only things had been a little bit different. Confronting a disappointing piece, one somehow wants to disown it, to say, “That’s not what I meant to do; I should have made it larger, or maybe smaller; if only I’d had more time or money or hadn’t used that stupid green paint...” We’d all love to squirm out of this one, but the undeniable fact is that your art is not some residue left when you subtract all the things you haven’t done — it is the full payoff for all the things you have done. One might as well wish for indulgence to go back and pick better numbers for last week’s lottery.

除了时间旅行者和小报通灵者之外，我们其他人只在今天直接参与。当你看着自己的工作日复一日、一件一件地展开时，你会发现其中的因果关系是无法逃避的。简而言之，你所做的事情让你来到这里，如果你再次应用相同的方法，你可能会再次得到相同的结果。这不仅适用于陷入困境，也适用于所有其他艺术状态——包括高生产力的状态。实际上，有效的想法和方法通常会持续有效。如果你之前工作很顺利，现在却陷入困境，那么你很可能不必要地改变了一些已经运行良好的方法。 （多年来，我把白天用于艺术创作，晚上用于写作；在某个时候，我改变了这个时间表，几个月过去了，我才意识到我的写作已经枯竭了——不是因为缺乏想法，而是因为事实证明我能更好地处理文字。当事情变得混乱时，你最好的开场策略可能是——非常小心地、有意识地——回到上次你对工作感觉良好时的习惯和做法。回到你离开的空间，（至少有时）工作也会回来。

Time travellers and tabloid psychics aside, the rest of us directly engage only today. And when you watch your work unfold day by day, piece by piece, there’s no escaping cause and effect. Simply put, what you did got you here, and if you apply the same methods again you will likely get the same result again. This is true not just for being stuck, but for all other artistic states as well - including highly productive states. As a practical matter, ideas and methods that work usually continue to work. If you were working smoothly and now you are stuck, chances are you unnecessarily altered some approach that was already working perfectly well. (For years I set aside daytimes for artmaking and evenings for writing; at some point I reversed that schedule, and months passed before I realized my writing had dried up — not for lack of ideas, but because it turns out I process words better at midnight than at midday.) When things go haywire, your best opening strategy might be to return — very carefully and consciously — to the habits and practices in play the last time you felt good about the work. Return to the space you drifted away from and (sometimes at least) the work will return as well.

有时也不会。艺术家（像其他人一样）有一定的概念惯性，即使世界本身偏离了另一个方向，他们也倾向于保持自己的方向。当哥伦布从新大陆回来并宣布地球是圆的时，几乎其他人都相信地球是平的。然后他们死了——下一代人在成长过程中相信世界是圆的。人们就是这样改变想法的。

And sometimes it won’t. Artists (like everyone else) have a certain conceptual inertia, a tendency to keep to their own compass heading even as the world itself veers off another direction. When Columbus returned from the New World and proclaimed the earth was round, almost everyone else went right on believing the earth was flat. Then they died — and the next generation grew up believing the world was round. That’s how people change their minds.

这也意味着，通常（但并非总是）你明天生产的作品将纯粹而简单地由你今天手中握着的工具塑造。从这个意义上说，艺术史也是技术史。文艺复兴前意大利的壁画、佛兰德斯的蛋彩画、法国南部的户外油画、纽约的丙烯酸树脂——每一种连续的技术都赋予了艺术作品特有的色彩和饱和度、笔触和纹理、感性或形式感。简而言之，某些工具使某些结果成为可能。

That’s also to say that usually — but not always — the piece you produce tomorrow will be shaped, purely and simply, by the tools you hold in your hand today. In that sense the history of art is also the history of technology. The frescoes of pre-Renaissance Italy, the tempera paintings of Flanders, the plein aire oils of southern France, the acrylics of New York City — each successive technology imparted characteristic color and saturation, brushstroke and texture, sensuality or formality to the art piece. Simply put, certain tools make certain results possible.

你的工具不仅仅影响最终艺术作品的外观——它们基本上限制了你对艺术作品的表达能力。当特定的工具和材料消失时（因为如何制造或使用它们的知识消失了），艺术的可能性也随之消失。巴洛克乐器的声音、凸版印刷的印象、铂金版画的色调——这些都被视为濒临灭绝的艺术创作。同样，当新工具出现时，新的艺术可能性就会出现。例如，一幅根据生活描绘的场景所揭示的世界与根据记忆描绘的世界截然不同。这在 1870 年代变得很明显，当时制造商找到了一种将油画颜料密封在可折叠金属箔管中的方法，并且使用这种介质工作的艺术家第一次可以选择离开工作室并直接在现场用油画进行绘画。有些做到了，有些则没有。那些这样做的人被称为印象派。

Your tools do more than just influence the appearance of the resulting art — they basically set limits upon what you can say with an art piece. And when particular tools and materials disappear (because knowledge of how to make or use them is lost), artistic possibilities are lost as well. The sound of baroque instruments, the impression of the letterpress, the tonality of platinum prints — count these among the endangered species of artmaking. And likewise when new tools appear, new artistic possibilities arise. A scene painted from life, for instance, reveals a world far different from the one painted from memory. This became evident in the 1870’s when manufacturers found a way to seal oil colors in collapsible metal-foil tubes, and for the first time artists working in that medium had the option of leaving the studio and painting with oils directly in the field. Some did, and some didn’t. Those who did became known as the Impressionists.

每个艺术家一次又一次面临的困境是何时坚持使用熟悉的工具和材料，何时伸出援手并拥抱那些提供新可能性的工具和材料。平均而言，年轻的艺术家倾向于尝试大量多样的工具和材料，而经验丰富的艺术家则倾向于使用小而具体的工具和材料。随着时间的推移，随着艺术家的手势变得更加有把握，所选择的工具几乎成为艺术家自身精神的延伸。随着时间的推移，探索让位于表达。

The dilemma every artist confronts, again and again, is when to stick with familiar tools and materials, and when to reach out and embrace those that offer new possibilities. And on average, the younger artist tends to experiment with a large and varied range of tools and materials, while the veteran artist tends to employ a small and specific set. In time, as an artist’s gestures become more assured, the chosen tools become almost an extension of the artist’s own spirit. In time, exploration gives way to expression.

但无论怎样，对我们的工作方法进行中途修正总是存在一个很大的障碍：我们几乎不知道方法本身是什么。当工作进展顺利时，我们到底为什么想知道？制作一件作品（或一年的作品）的无数步骤中的大多数都在有意识的思考水平以下进行，涉及到关于什么是艺术创作的未明确的信念和假设。它们仍然像我们在决定用直线还是用圆形笔划抛光盘子时所采取的步骤一样未知和未被考虑。问问自己为什么（例如）在绘画时听乡村西部音乐？ （这是否鼓励您选择更明亮的颜色？）为什么您离开工作室时没有暖气，即使这意味着要穿着大衣工作？ （这会让你的笔触更清晰吗？）当湿纸想要画水彩时，你有什么感觉？ （通过触摸？气味？纸张的柔软度？）我们很少思考我们如何或为什么做这些事情——我们只是做它们。改变工作结果的模式意味着首先要确定你的方法中的一些事情，这些事情就像楔入粘土一样自动，就像从弓上放箭一样微妙。

Either way, however, there is always one large obstacle to making mid-course corrections in our working methods: we hardly know what the methods themselves are. And when the work is going well, why on earth would we want to know? Most of the myriad of steps that go into making a piece (or a year’s worth of pieces) go on below the level of conscious thought, engaging unarticulated beliefs and assumptions about what artmaking is. They remain as unknown and unconsidered as the steps we take in deciding whether to burnish the plate with straight or with circular strokes. Ask yourself why (for instance) you listen to country western music while you’re painting? (Does it encourage you to choose brighter colors?) Why do you leave your studio unheated even when it means working with your overcoat on? (Does it make your brushstrokes crisper?) How do you sense when the dampened paper wants to take the watercolors? (By touch? Smell? The limpness of the paper?) We rarely think about how or why we do such things — we just do them. Changing the pattern of outcome in your work means first identifying things about your approach that are as automatic as wedging the clay, as subtle as releasing the arrow from the bow.

我们确实认识到的艺术创作的细节往往是来之不易的实际工作习惯，以及我们可以反复坚持工作的反复出现的形式。 （有时在无聊的日子里，我会半声地对自己说，如果我只是走进工作室开始创作一件湿的作品，我至少必须在它干之前完成一些东西。）我们使用可预测的工作习惯来让我们进入工作室和我们的材料；我们使用重复出现的形式作为制作特定作品的起点。考虑到他写的玛祖卡舞曲的数量，我们不得不认为，一旦肖邦发现了这种音乐形式，他一定是一个更快乐的作曲家。很容易想象，他几乎任何时候都可以坐在钢琴前，开始在那个奇怪的切分音四分之三的时间里进行演奏，逐渐将其打造成一首小规模的作品。对于肖邦来说，这种形式非常有利于探索和变化，他能够多年来重复使用它。同样，当 JS 巴赫致力于用二十四个调中的每一个调写一首前奏曲和赋格曲时，这肯定很有帮助，因为每次他坐下来作曲时，他至少都有一个开始的地方。 （“让我们看看，我还没有开始练习升 F 小调……”）在特定形式的自我强加的纪律中工作可以减轻必须在每首新作品中重塑自己的前景。

The details of artmaking we do recognize tend to be hard-won practical working habits, and recurrent bits of form that we can repeatedly hang work on. (Sometimes on dull days I’ve said to myself half-aloud that if I just go into the studio and start a wet piece, I’ll at least have to finish something before it dries.) We use predictable work habits to get us into the studio and into our materials; we use recurrent bits of form as starting points for making specific pieces. Considering the number of Mazurkas he wrote, we have to think that once Chopin found that musical form he must have been a happier composer. It’s easy to imagine that he could sit at the piano most any time and begin to vamp in that oddly syncopated three-quarter time, gradually building it into a small-scale piece. For Chopin that form was so conducive to exploration and variation he was able to reuse it for years. Equally, it must have been just plain helpful when J.S. Bach committed to writing a prelude and fugue in each of the twenty-four keys, since each time he sat down to compose he at least had a place to start. (“Let’s see, I haven’t begun to work on the F-sharp minor yet... ”) Working within the self-imposed discipline of a particular form eases the prospect of having to reinvent yourself with each new piece.

发现有用的形式是宝贵的。一旦发现，决不应该因为微不足道的原因而抛弃它们。很容易想象今天的艺术导师警告肖邦，玛祖卡的事情有点重复，工作没有进展。嗯，确实，事情可能没有取得进展——但这不是问题所在。写玛祖卡舞曲可能只对肖邦有用——作为重新投入作品的工具，以及开始创作下一首作品的地方。对于大多数艺术家来说，创作出好的艺术作品取决于创作大量的艺术作品，任何将第一笔触带到下一张空白画布的装置都具有有形的实用价值。

The discovery of useful forms is precious. Once found, they should never be abandoned for trivial reasons. It’s easy to imagine today’s art instructor cautioning Chopin that the Mazurka thing is getting a little repetitive, that the work is not progressing. Well, true, it may not have been progressing — but that’s not the issue. Writing Mazurkas may have been useful only to Chopin — as a vehicle for getting back into the work, and as a place to begin making the next piece. For most artists, making good art depends upon making lots of art, and any device that carries the first brushstroke to the next blank canvas has tangible, practical value.

只有创造者（而且只有随着时间的推移）才有机会知道小惯例和仪式在坚持工作的实践中有多么重要。艺术创作的私人细节对观众（通常对老师来说也是如此）完全不感兴趣，也许是因为它们几乎从不通过检查完成的作品而可见，甚至不可知。例如，海明威将打字机安装在柜台高度，并站着完成所有写作。如果他没有站着，他就不会打字。当然，这种奇怪的习惯在他的故事中是看不到的——但如果他否认了这种习惯，可能就不会有任何故事了。

Only the maker (and then only with time) has a chance of knowing how important small conventions and rituals are in the practice of staying at work. The private details of artmaking are utterly uninteresting to audiences (and frequently to teachers), perhaps because they’re almost never visible — or even knowable — from examining the finished work. Hemingway, for instance, mounted his typewriter at counter-height and did all his writing while standing up. If he wasn’t standing, he wasn’t typing. Of course that odd habit isn’t visible in his stories — but were he denied that habit, there probably wouldn’t be any stories.

艺术创作中最困难的部分是以这样一种方式生活，让你的工作一遍又一遍地完成——这意味着，除其他外，找到一系列非常有用的实践。一件艺术品是生产模式中生活的表面表达。随着时间的推移，一位多产艺术家的生活变得充满了有用的惯例和实用的方法，因此一连串的成品不断出现在表面。在真正快乐的时刻，这些艺术姿态超越了简单的程序，并获得了自己固有的美感。它们是你的艺术壁炉和家，是连接形式和感觉的未来工作场所。它们就像玛祖卡舞曲的深色和不对称轻快的旋律一样，与创作者的生活密不可分。他们是经典。它们让人充满信心和集中注意力。他们允许不知道。它们让自动化和不明确的事物保持如此。一旦你找到了你想要做的工作，任何一件作品的细节就不再那么重要了。

The hardest part of artmaking is living your life in such a way that your work gets done, over and over — and that means, among other things, finding a host of practices that are just plain useful. A piece of art is the surface expression of a life lived within productive patterns. Over time, the life of a productive artist becomes filled with useful conventions and practical methods, so that a string of finished pieces continues to appear at the surface. And in truly happy moments those artistic gestures move beyond simple procedure, and acquire an inherent aesthetic all their own. They are your artistic hearth and home, the working-places-to-be that link form and feeling. They become — like the dark colors and asymmetrical lilt of the Mazurka — inseparable from the life of their maker. They are canons. They allow confidence and concentration. They allow not knowing. They allow the automatic and unarticulated to remain so. Once you have found the work you are meant to do, the particulars of any single piece don’t matter all that much.

对于艺术家来说，所有艺术问题都是独一无二的个人问题。嗯，这是可以理解的，因为没有多少其他活动经常质疑一个人的基本自我价值。但那些真正的个人问题都与艺术创作有关。一旦艺术创作完成，一系列全新的问题就会出现，这些问题需要艺术家与外部世界接触。称它们为普通问题。

TO THE ARTIST, all problems of art appear uniquely personal. Well, that’s understandable enough, given that not many other activities routinely call one’s basic self-worth into question. But those really personal problems all relate to the making of the art. Once the art has been made, an entirely new set of problems arise, problems that require the artist to engage the outside world. Call them ordinary problems.

一般问题

ORDINARY PROBLEMS

然而，普通问题并不是微不足道的问题。除其他事项外，它们消耗了几乎每个艺术家的大部分时间。一位著名画家，经过几个月的仔细记录，得出了一个令人沮丧的结论，即使最好的情况下，他每个月最多也只能腾出六七天时间来实际绘画，剩下的二十多天不可避免地都花在了画廊生意上，工作室清理、UPS 运行等。寓意：艺术不仅仅只是创造它。在许多情况下，你今天创作的艺术明天才能到达观众手中，只是因为有一个面向艺术教育、资助、批评、出版、展览和表演的庞大社会网络。

Ordinary problems are not, however, trivial problems. Among other things, they consume the larger part of almost every artist’s time. One well-known painter, after several months of careful record keeping, reached the discouraging conclusion that even at best he could free up only six or seven days a month for actually painting, while the remaining twenty-odd days inevitably went to gallery business, studio cleanup, UPS runs and the like. Moral: There’s one hell of a lot more to art than just making it. In many cases, the art you make today will reach its audience tomorrow only because of a vast societal network geared to arts education, funding, criticism, publication, exhibition and performance.

不幸的是，在许多其他情况下，尽管有这个网络，你的艺术也只能到达世界。许多将艺术引入更广阔世界的尝试只是证明了我们社会中经济与信仰之间的不稳定契合。在许多方面，艺术被认为是危险的、不必要的、精英主义的、昂贵的——并且依赖于衰弱的东海岸自由派的赞助才能生存。艺术家本身的情况也好不到哪里去，他们被广泛描绘为颠覆性的怪人，他们不仅喜欢生活在罪恶中，而且可能还用你的税款来做这件事！

In many other cases, unfortunately, your art will only reach the world in spite of this network. Many attempts to introduce art to the larger world simply give evidence of the uneasy fit in our society between economics and beliefs. In many quarters art is viewed as dangerous, unnecessary, elitist, expensive — and dependent on the patronage of effete East Coast liberals for its survival. Artists themselves fare little better, being widely portrayed as subversive weirdos who not only enjoy Living In Sin, but are probably doing it off Your Tax Dollars as well!

话虽如此，作者想用这句话来宣布在他们未来的讨论中自我强加的愤世嫉俗的暂停——无论这些混蛋有多么应得。谢谢。
Having said that, the authors would like to employ this sentence to proclaim a self-imposed moratorium on cynicism in their future discussions — regardless of how much the bastards deserve it. Thank You.

无论如何，这些态度的原因和结果都是显而易见的。有些艺术，就其本质而言，是具有颠覆性的。通过引导观众通过艺术家截然不同的感受来体验世界，一件好的艺术作品不可避免地会质疑观众自己的信仰体系。这是威胁吗？教皇是天主教徒吗？艺术越有效，观众的第一反应就越有可能是愤怒和否认，然后立即寻找罪魁祸首。在这个部门中，艺术家总是最有可能的候选人——毕竟，我们有杀死传递坏消息的使者的悠久传统。

In any case, there’s nothing obscure about either the cause or the effect of these attitudes. Some art, by its very nature, is subversive. By leading the viewer to experience the world through the very different sensibilities of the artist, a good work of art inevitably calls the viewer’s own belief system into question. Is this threatening? Is the Pope Catholic? The more effective the art, the more likely the viewer’s first reaction will be anger and denial — followed immediately by a search for someone to blame. And in that department the artist is always the most likely candidate — we have, after all, a time-honored tradition of killing the messenger who delivers the bad news.

罗伯特·梅普尔索普 (Robert Mapplethorpe) 是一名摄影师，他拍摄了一组公然将同性恋浪漫化的照片，其中一个比较著名的砍倒信使和其他所有人的例子包括在内。事实证明，威胁对梅普尔索普来说并没有多大意义，尽管他正在准备展出这批作品，但他已经病入膏肓了。相反，压力点出现在支持艺术的网络中，尤其是国家艺术基金会。这里的微妙之处并不多：国家艺术局只是受到威胁，如果它向创作或展出违反“社区标准”的作品的艺术家或博物馆提供支持，就会被切断资金。

One of the more celebrated examples of mowing down the messenger — and everyone else in sight — involved Robert Mapplethorpe, a photographer who made a set of images overtly romanticizing homosexuality. As it turned out, threats didn’t mean a lot to Mapplethorpe, who was already terminally ill even as he readied this body of work for exhibit. Instead, pressure points were found in the supporting arts network, especially the National Endowment for the Arts. Subtlety was not in great abundance here: the NEA was simply threatened with a cutoff of funding if it lent support to artists or museums that made or exhibited work that offended “community standards”.

当然，也有反对的抗议，最终梅普尔索普的作品得以展出，但向艺术界传达的信息很明确：偏离无害的东西太远，斧头就会掉下来。称之为选择性审查制度：言论自由得到保障，除非它以艺术作品的形式表达。这部美国道德剧最令人惊奇的方面并不是政府在感到受到威胁时将自身利益置于原则之上，而是没有人在数英里之外预见到这一点。历史提醒我们：美国革命并没有得到来自王室的相应拨款的资助。

There were counter-protests, of course, and in the end Mapplethorpe’s work was exhibited, but the message to the arts community was clear: stray too far from the innocuous, and the axe would fall. Call it selective censorship: freedom of expression was guaranteed unless it was expressed in a work of art. The most amazing aspect of this American morality play was not that the government would place self-interest above principle when it felt threatened, but that no one foresaw this coming from miles down the road. A reminder from history: the American Revolution was not financed with matching Grants from the Crown.

共同点

COMMON GROUND

不言而喻，审查制度正在削弱艺术家的力量。审查制度是一种完全自然的事态，这一点不太明显（至少对艺术家来说）。大自然对那些离开羊群而走自己的路的人施加了一个简单的限制：他们会被吃掉。在社会上，情况要复杂一些。尽管如此，还是有一个警告：避免未知事物具有相当大的生存价值。社会、自然和艺术创作往往会产生受保护的生物。

It goes without saying that censorship is debilitating to the artist. It’s a little less obvious (at least to artists) that censorship is an entirely natural state of affairs. Nature places a simple constraint on those who leave the flock to go their own way: they get eaten. In society it’s a bit more complicated. Nonetheless the admonition stands: avoiding the unknown has considerable survival value. Society, nature and artmaking tend to produce guarded creatures.

这里的困境是，对于艺术家来说，与主题和材料的接触必须始终保持不加防范。在创作艺术时，你会追求未知，并随之而来的是那些担心变化可能带来的人们的偏执。但是，虽然担心引起某些南方参议员的愤怒可能会给你的言论自由蒙上阴影，但通常更令人烦恼的问题是首先引起任何人的注意。毕竟，大多数人认为没有理由质疑自己的信仰，更不用说征求你的信仰了。

The dilemma here is that for the artist, contact with subject and materials must always remain unguarded. In making art you court the unknown, and with it the paranoia of those who fear what change might bring. But while fear of attracting the wrath of some southern Senator may cast a shadow on your freedom of expression, often the more vexing problem is catching anyone’s attention in the first place. After all, most people see no reason to question their own beliefs, much less solicit yours.

为什么他们应该这样做？无论是在艺术上还是在其他方面，我们进入的世界已经被其他人观察和定义过——彻底、冗余、全面，而且通常非常恰当。人类花了几千年的时间形成了一套庞大而有力的对世界的观察，其形式多种多样，包括语言、艺术和宗教。这些观察结果又经受住了许多——非常多的——测试。我们继承了一大堆无法确定的意义。

And why should they? Artistically and otherwise, the world we come into has already been observed and defined by others — thoroughly, redundantly, comprehensively, and usually quite appropriately. The human race has spent several millennia developing a huge and robust set of observations about the world, in forms as varied as language, art and religion. Those observations in turn have withstood many — enormously many — tests. We stand heir to an unstatably large set of meanings.

我们继承的大部分内容都是如此明显正确，以至于我们看不见。它与世界无缝衔接。这是世界。然而，尽管我们所继承的这个定义明确的世界具有丰富性和可变性，但它并不完全适合我们每个人。我们大多数人的大部分时间都花在别人的世界里——做预定的工作，放松享受预先包装好的娱乐——无论这个现成的世界多么美好，总会有一些东西缺失或不存在的时候。不太真实。因此，你通过成为世界的一部分——通过为场景贡献一些新的部分——来在世界上占据一席之地。当然，当人们花时间参观你创造的世界时，艺术创作更令人惊讶的回报之一就会到来。事实上，有些人甚至可能会购买你世界的一部分，带回去并据为己有。你的每一件新艺术作品都扩大了我们的现实。世界还没有完成。

Most of what we inherit is so clearly correct it goes unseen. It fits the world seamlessly. It is the world. But despite its richness and variability, the well-defined world we inherit doesn’t quite fit each one of us, individually. Most of us spend most of our time in other peoples’ worlds — working at predetermined jobs, relaxing to pre-packaged entertainment — and no matter how benign this ready-made world may be, there will always be times when something is missing or doesn’t quite ring true. And so you make your place in the world by making part of it — by contributing some new part to the set. And surely one of the more astonishing rewards of artmaking comes when people make time to visit the world you have created. Some, indeed, may even purchase a piece of your world to carry back and adopt as their own. Each new piece of your art enlarges our reality. The world is not yet done.

艺术问题

ART ISSUES

在这里观察到拥有艺术硕士学位（甚至现代艺术知识）几乎不应该成为创作艺术的先决条件，这似乎是无害的。毕竟，艺术早在艺术部门出现之前就出现了，早在任何人开始分类或收集艺术家作品之前就已经出现了。尽管如此，今天的大多数艺术家确实接受过正规的艺术培训，熟悉当前的艺术世界趋势，并且至少在一定程度上依赖画廊或学术界维持生计。

It seems harmless enough to observe here that having an MFA (or even a knowledge of modern art) should hardly be a prerequisite to making art. After all, art appeared long before Art Departments, long before anyone began classifying or collecting artists’ works. Nonetheless, most artists today do have formal training in art, a familiarity with the current art world trends, and at least some dependence on galleries or academia for their livelihood.

这是可以理解的（如果不是完全健康的话），因为艺术网络中的每个环节都将自己的角色定义为基本和必要的既得利益。艺术家面临的常见问题之一是找到一种与艺术网络及其所珍视的问题和平相处的方法。请注意，不一定要加入它——只是与它和平相处。如果您想保证您的作品能够在任何合理的时间内展示、出版或表演，至少您需要这样做。

This is understandable (if not exactly healthy) given that each link in the arts network has a vested interest in defining its own role as fundamental and necessary. One of the ordinary problems artists face is finding a way to make peace with the arts network and the issues it holds dear. Not necessarily joining it, mind you — just making peace with it. At least you need to if you want assurance your work will likely be shown, published or performed in any reasonable length of time.

如果展示的需求足够强烈，这不是问题。但今天许多艺术家感到的不安暴露了他们内心的作品与策展人、出版商和发起人在情感上的遥远关注之间缺乏契合。很难夸大这个问题的严重性。在艺术世界中找到自己的位置并不是一件容易的事，如果确实有适合你的地方的话。事实上，当代艺术界为数不多的确定的事情之一是，除了你之外，还有人在决定哪些艺术——以及哪些艺术家——属于其中。对于谦逊、工艺和温柔来说，这是一个艰难的世纪。

If the need to get shown is strong enough, this is not a problem. But the unease many artists feel today betrays a lack of fit between the work of their heart and the emotionally remote concerns of curators, publishers and promoters. It’s hard to overstate the magnitude of this problem. Finding your place in the art world is no easy matter, if indeed there is a place for you at all. In fact one of the few sure things about the contemporary art scene is that someone besides you is deciding which art — and which artists — belong in it. It’s been a tough century for modesty, craftsmanship and tenderness.

竞赛

COMPETITION

不可否认竞争。它是我们根深蒂固的。这是化学的。优秀的运动员会依靠在知道自己可以超越前方跑步者的瞬间产生的能量激增。优秀的艺术家在展览和出版的截止日期前茁壮成长，在连续二十个小时的工作中看到罐子被上釉并烧成这样，让他们的下一件作品比上一件更好。竞争的冲动提供了原始能量的来源，仅出于此目的，它就非常有用。在健康的艺术环境中，这种能量会向内引导，以发挥自己的潜力。在一个健康的艺术环境中，艺术家之间不存在竞争。

There’s no denying competition. It’s hard-wired into us. It’s chemical. Good athletes bank on that surge of energy that arises in the instant of knowing they can overtake the runner just ahead. Good artists thrive on exhibit and publication deadlines, on working twenty hours straight to see the pots are glazed and fired just so, on making their next work better than their last. The urge to compete provides a source of raw energy, and for that purpose alone it can be exceptionally useful. In a healthy artistic environment, that energy is directed inward to fulfill one’s own potential. In a healthy artistic environment, artists are not in competition with each another.

不幸的是，健康的艺术环境就像独角兽一样常见。我们生活在一个鼓励明显恶性竞争的社会，并为判断谁获胜制定了严格而负责任的标准。根据艺术家所获得的认可（很容易比较）来评价艺术家，比根据他们创作的作品（这可能就像苹果和华尔兹一样不同）来评价艺术家更容易。当这种情况发生时，竞争中心就不再存在了。创作作品，但收集该作品的接受和认可的象征——NEA Grants、Gallerie d'jour 的一场展览、 《纽约客》的名人简介等等。

Unfortunately, healthy artistic environments are about as common as unicorns. We live in a society that encourages competition at demonstrably vicious levels, and sets a hard and accountable yardstick for judging who wins. It’s easier to rate artists in terms of the recognition they’ve received (which is easily compared) than in terms of the pieces they’ve made (which may be as different as apples and waltzes.) And when that happens, competition centers not on making work, but on collecting the symbols of acceptance and approval of that work — N.E.A. Grants, a Show at Gallerie d’jour, a celebrity profile in The New Yorker and the like.

如果走向极端，这种竞争就会陷入与他人命运的不必要的（而且往往是自我毁灭性的）比较。 WC·菲尔兹只要一提到查理·卓别林的名字就会勃然大怒。弥尔顿因不断与莎士比亚进行自我比较而终生抑郁。每次将自己的音乐与莫扎特的音乐进行比较时，索列里都会变得更加疯狂。 （我们当中谁会欢迎这种比较！？）担心自己没有得到应有的认可会导致愤怒和痛苦。担心自己不如其他艺术家会导致抑郁。

Taken to extremes, such competition slides into needless (and often self-destructive) comparison with the fortunes of others. W.C. Fields became enraged at the mere mention of Charlie Chaplin’s name; Milton suffered lifelong depression from ongoing self-comparison with Shakespeare; Solieri went a bit more insane each time he compared his music to Mozart’s. (And who among us would welcome that comparison!?) Fear that you’re not getting your fair share of recognition leads to anger and bitterness. Fear that you’re not as good as a fellow artist leads to depression.

诚然，当别人赢得我们所寻求的奖学金时，我们中很少有人会感到短暂的痛苦，或者当我们获得同样的奖项时，我们会感到一种秘密的胜利。 （金斯利·艾米斯承认，当他开始写一本新小说时，他的部分动机是，“这一次我要向他们展示！”）但偶尔的竞争性抱怨是健康的一步，远离将成功等同于站在顶峰你同龄人的身体。如果不出意外的话，当你想象中的竞争对手可能完全不知道你的存在时，就很难声称自己是胜利了——毕竟，有些人可能已经去世一个世纪了。很有可能他们不会赢，而你——迟早——会输。在某些形式的比较中，失败几乎是不可避免的。

Admittedly, few of us are above feeling a momentary stab of pain when someone else wins the fellowship we sought, or a secret rush of triumph when we scoop up the same prize. (Kingsley Amis allowed that when he’d start writing a new novel, part of his motive was, “I’m going to show them, this time!”) But occasional competitive grousing is a healthy step removed from equating success with standing atop the bodies of your peers. If nothing else it’s hard to claim victory when your imagined competitors may be entirely unaware of your existence — after all, some may have already been dead for a century. Quite plausibly they don’t win, while you — sooner or later — will lose. In some forms of comparison, defeat is all but inevitable.

但无论使用何种标准，所有竞争对手都有一个明显的特征：他们知道自己在群体中的排名。狂热的竞争对手会不断检查他们的排名。痴迷的竞争者只是将排名与自我等同起来——这是一种冒险的策略，但通过挖掘使他们在艺术上更加努力的能量来源而发挥作用（当它确实起作用时），并且几乎总是使他们成为优秀的野心家。当自我意识如此直接地取决于外界给予的排名时，创作高收视率作品的动力就会变得极端。如果不知道如何告诉自己你的工作还不错，你可能会被迫试图让世界其他地方告诉你。

But regardless of the yardstick used, all competitors share one telling characteristic: they know where they rank in the pack. Avid competitors check their ranking constantly. Obsessive competitors simply equate rank with self — a chancy gambit, but one that works (when it does work) by tapping a source of energy that makes them work harder at their art, and almost always makes them good careerists. When sense of self depends so directly upon the ranking bestowed by the outside world, motivation to produce work that brings high ratings is extreme. In not knowing how to tell yourself that your work is OK, you may be driven to the top of the heap in trying to get the rest of the world to tell you.

从理论上讲，这是一种完全有效的方法——棘手的部分是找到衡量你的成就的正确标准。艺术竞争之所以成为一个棘手的问题，原因很简单，那就是对于你最好的作品是什么，很少有任何共识。此外，每件新作品重要的不是它比你之前的作品好还是差，而是它的相似或不同之处。两首巴赫赋格曲之间有意义的比较不在于它们的排名，而在于它们的演奏方式。

In theory this is a perfectly valid approach — the tricky part is finding the right yardstick for measuring your accomplishments. What makes competition in the arts a slippery issue is simply that there’s rarely any consensus about what your best work is. Moreover, what’s important about each new piece is not whether it is better or worse than your previous efforts, but the ways in which it is similar or different. The meaningful comparison between two Bach fugues is not how they rank, but how they work.

当你的艺术创作进展顺利时，你制作的所有作品都会有生命力，无论它们如何成为个人最爱。毕竟，他们都是你的宝贝。甚至可以说，你有义务探索可能的变化，因为一个艺术问题可以产生许多正确的答案。富有成效的时间鼓励你构建一个扩展的作品体系，其中所有作品（甚至是永远不会出现在画廊墙上的有缺陷的草图）都有机会发挥。在健康的时期，你很少停下来区分内在动力、工艺感、截止日期的压力或新想法的魅力——它们都是你制作作品的能量来源。

When things go really well in your artmaking, all the pieces you make have a life to them, regardless of how they stack up as personal favorites. After all, they’re all your babies. It can even be argued that you have an obligation to explore the possible variations, given that a single artistic question can yield many right answers. Productive times encourage you to build an extended body of work, one where all the pieces (even the flawed sketches that will never see the gallery wall) have a chance to play. In healthy times you rarely pause to distinguish between internal drive, sense of craft, the pressure of a deadline or the charm of a new idea — they all serve as sources of energy in the pieces you make.

系统导航

NAVIGATING THE SYSTEM

事实证明，艺术家是一群狡猾的人，而且出人意料地擅长让系统买单，让他们做他们想做的事。米开朗基罗受教会委托绘制了西斯廷教堂的天花板；安塞尔·亚当斯 (Ansel Adams)为内政部拍摄了埃尔南德斯的月出照片。伊姆斯家具和埃夫登时尚传播证明，即使在商业和绒毛的极端情况下，艺术也能盛行。事实上，对于许多艺术品——尤其是像帕台农神庙或越南战争纪念馆这样的大型作品——在艺术品开始创作之前就已经有了买家的主张，可以提出一个令人不安的有力论据。

Artists, it turns out, are a crafty lot, and surprisingly adept at getting the system to foot the bill for letting them do exactly what they wanted to do anyway. Michelangelo painted the ceiling of the Sistine Chapel on commission from the Church; Ansel Adams photographed Moonrise, Hernandez on assignment for the Department of the Interior. Eames furniture and Avedon fashion spreads prove that art can prevail even at the extremes of commerce and fluff. Indeed, a disconcertingly strong argument can be made for the proposition that many artworks — especially large-scale efforts like the Parthenon or the Vietnam War Memorial — have had a buyer in place before the artwork was begun.

问题是要防止这种指挥表演玷污接下来的作品，因为委托艺术有一种缓慢而不知不觉地滑入商业贸易的方式。对于具有广泛（且报酬较高）商业应用的艺术形式来说，这尤其麻烦。在这种情况下的挑战是让顾客相信只有你知道制作这件作品的正确方法。

The problem is to keep such command performances from tainting the work that follows, since commissioned art has a way of sliding slowly and imperceptibly into commercial trade. This is especially troublesome for art forms that have widespread (and higher paying) commercial applications. The challenge in such circumstances is to convince the patron that you alone know the right way to make the piece.

对于一些艺术家来说，这是一种权衡（或者可能是一种僵局）。在圣诞节期间，芭蕾舞团（甚至是主要演员）会提供大量的《胡桃夹子》演出，这是唯一一部能产生足够门票收入来支撑他们渡过难关的芭蕾舞剧。本赛季剩下的时间。同样，版画制作者在不改变其作品内容的情况下，很快就能了解到哪些图像可能值得运行大版本的成本。

For some artists it’s a trade-off (or perhaps a standoff.) At Christmastime, ballet companies (even the major players) offer an inordinate number of performances of the Nutcracker, that being the only ballet that generates enough ticket sales to pull them through the rest of the season. Likewise printmakers, without altering the content of their work, learn soon enough which images will likely justify the cost of running a large edition.

然而，对于许多其他艺术家来说，艺术网络是一场彻头彻尾的灾难。有时，只是导致艺术创作的随心所欲的思维模式并不能优雅地导致整洁的记录保存。然而，更常见的是，那些勤奋地遵循自我强加的纪律（比如用抑扬格五音步写作，或为钢琴独奏作曲）的艺术家，事实证明自己没有能力应对他人施加的限制。爱德华·韦斯顿的好心朋友曾经说服一家咖啡公司向这位艺术家提供委托，制作静物照片，供他们在杂志广告中使用。唯一的要求是该公司的产品必须出现在安排中的某个位置；尽管如此，韦斯顿将小物体作为艺术进行拍摄的能力是传奇的，但他却因为必须将其中一个小物体制作成咖啡罐而感到完全分心。

For many other artists, however, the arts network proves an unmitigated disaster. Sometimes it’s just that the freewheeling thought patterns that lead to artmaking don’t lead as gracefully to tidy record keeping. More often, though, the same artists who diligently follow a self-imposed discipline (like writing in iambic pentameter, or composing for solo piano) prove singularly ill-equipped to handle constraints imposed by others. Edward Weston’s well-meaning friends once convinced a coffee company to offer that artist a commission to make still-life photographs they could use in their magazine ads. About the only requirement was that the company’s product appear somewhere in the arrangement; nonetheless Weston, whose facility with photographing small objects as art is legendary, was driven to complete distraction by the pressure of having to make one of those small objects a coffee can.

理想情况下（至少从艺术家的角度来看），艺术网络可以处理所有那些与艺术创作过程无关的细节。这是一种健康的培育态度，因为如果没有外部的大量投资，某些艺术形式（如电影和文学）永远无法实现从想法到现实的飞跃。作者通常会邮寄手稿，然后几乎将所有后续工作——校对、设计、印刷、发行和推广——都交给出版商。一些艺术家甚至将界面作为他们作品的重要组成部分。克里斯托的各种“包装”是一种表演艺术形式，只有相对较少的人能够直接体验到——但表演的记录已经成为自己的艺术作品，在博物馆中展出，其中包括地图、工作图、与分区板的通信、后勤计划和很快。如果当今艺术网络影响力的所有这些证据仍然无法给您留下深刻的印象，请考虑一个发人深省的推论：一旦您死了，您所有的艺术都将由这个网络处理。

Ideally (at least from the artist’s viewpoint), the arts network is there to handle all those details not central to the artmaking process. This is a healthy attitude to nurture, since some art forms (like cinema and literature) could never make the leap from idea to reality without a sizeable investment from the outside anyway. Writers routinely mail out manuscripts and leave virtually all that follows — proofreading, design, printing, distribution and promotion — in the publisher’s hands. Some artists even make the interface a prominent part of their work. Christo’s various “wrappings” are a form of performance art experienced directly by relatively few people — but the record of the performance has become its own art piece, exhibited in museums complete with maps, working drawings, correspondence with zoning boards, logistical plans, and so on. If all this evidence of the reach of today’s arts network still fails to impress you, consider the sobering corollary: once you’re dead, all your art is handled by this network.

但如果艺术家在当代经济和营销面前处于濒危物种的地位，我们就面临着一个令人困惑的问题：为什么个体艺术家的神话——追随自己内心的孤独者——会随着每一个新的艺术家的诞生而如此可预见地出现。一代？

But if the artist stands as an endangered species in the face of contemporary economics and marketing, we are faced with a perplexing question: why does the myth of the individual artist — the loner following his/her own heart — arise so predictably with each new generation?

一个可能的答案是通过观察过去那些让艺术变得值得做的事情来提出的。由艺术家与作品、艺术家与材料、或艺术家与主题之间的关系所产生的问题驱动的作品听起来是真实的。这样的工作，无论是否符合当时的态度，随着时间的推移似乎仍然有意义。

One possible answer is suggested by looking at the things that have made art worth doing in the past. Work that was driven by issues arising from the relationship between the artist and the work, or the artist and the materials, or the artist and the subject matter, rings true. Such work, regardless of whether it fits with then-contemporary attitudes, seems to continue to make sense over time.

第二个答案更具尝试性，它挖掘了艺术的深层源泉：实用性和仪式性。在很早的时候，这些基本需求为艺术提供了文化定位，而自我表达（即使未被认识到）有助于将个人经验和技能与这些更大的目标结合起来。但是，仪式的形式是在洞壁上绘制的野牛，并在伟大的宗教时代得到了鼎盛的发展，现在已经退居到世俗的时尚和装饰中。早期艺术家为从黑曜石箭头到烧制粘土陶器的每件物品赋予了实用性，但实用性已经让位于复杂性和大规模生产。在我们这个时代，艺术的文化利基仍未被填补，而自我表达本身已成为目的。这可能不是最健康的情况——但话又说回来，也没有人说我们生活在最健康的时代。

A second answer, more tentative, taps into the deep wellsprings of art: utility and ritual. In very early times, these basic needs provided the cultural niche for art, while self-expression (even if unrecognized as such) served to integrate personal experience and skill with those larger goals. But ritual, which took form as painted bison on the cave wall and found its high flowering in the time of the great religions, has receded into secular fad and decoration. And utility, in whose service the early artist gave form to every object from obsidian arrowheads to fired clay pottery, has yielded to complexity and mass production. In our time, the cultural niche for art remains unfilled, while self-expression has become an end in itself. This may not be the healthiest of situations — but then again no one said we’re living in the healthiest of times either.

VII.

学术界

THE ACADEMIC WORLD

当我女儿大约七岁的时候，有一天她问我工作是做什么的。我告诉她我在大学工作——我的工作是教人们如何画画。
When my daughter was about seven years old, she asked me one day what I did at work. I told her I worked at the college — that my job was to teach people how to draw.
她难以置信地看着我，说道：“你的意思是他们忘记了？”
She stared back at me, incredulous, and said, “You mean they forget?”

— 霍华德·池本

— Howard Ikemoto

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

作者想以一个激进的主张来开始这次讨论——即大学艺术课程确实有一些有用的目的。诚然不是一个大目的。通常不是他们声明的目的。但有些目的。现在，这可能不完全是一个响亮的认可，但请记住，我们在这里谈论的是一个领域，其最杰出的毕业生将自己描述为正规教育的幸存者。

THE AUTHORS would like to open this discussion with a radical proposition — namely, that University art programs do serve some useful purpose. Admittedly not a large purpose. And generally not their stated purpose. But some purpose. Now that may not be exactly a ringing endorsement, but remember, we’re talking here about a field whose most prominent graduates describe themselves as survivors of their formal education.

事实上，在艺术教育系统中工作的想法——无论是作为学生还是教师——听起来就像站在死猫的细雨下一样有吸引力。从表面上看，大多数学校教育不仅对个人具有破坏性，而且与伟大的历史进程无关。恐怖故事比比皆是。我们都被三年级老师的同行所感动，他告诉某些孩子他们唱得很糟糕，他们应该默默地念出圣诞盛会的歌词。或者一些艺术史老师用讽刺的俏皮话对摇滚乐或电影制作不屑一顾，“这不是艺术。”

Indeed, the thought of working in the art education system — either as student or faculty — may sound about as attractive as standing beneath a steady drizzle of dead cats. Viewed from the outside, most schooling gives every appearance of being not only destructive to the individual, but irrelevant to the great sweep of history as well. Horror stories abound. We’ve all been emotionally singed by some counterpart to the third grade teacher who told certain kids they sang so badly they should just silently mouth the words of the Christmas Pageant. Or some art history teacher who dismissed Rock’n Roll or filmmaking with the backhanded one-liner, “It isn’t art.”

然而，从那些每天处理教育问题的内部人士看来，事情自然会变得更加复杂。和个人的。学术界面临的困境是，它不仅要容纳努力成为艺术家的学生，还要容纳努力保持艺术家身份的老师。

Viewed from the inside, however — by those who grapple with educational issues on a day-to-day basis — things naturally get more complicated. And personal. The dilemma facing academia is that it must accommodate not only students who are striving to become artists, but also teachers who are struggling to remain artists.

师资问题

FACULTY ISSUES

讽刺的是，想要教书的艺术家往往在踏入课堂之前就注定要失败。即使在最初的工作选择过程中，对教学能力的评估也会出现偏差。典型的申请表几乎不允许对教学质量做出判断，但通常会要求任意数量的教学质量。这使得新晋的艺术硕士毕业生成为大学就业市场上常年的炮灰，他们在获得第一个职位之前经常会被解雇。而且，同样忽视新人潜力的制度往往会低估老手的成就。作者回忆起曾经在一个大学遴选委员会任职，当时该委员会比较了两份申请，其中一份列出了当地公园与休闲暑期项目的三年教学时间，另一份则证明了哈佛大学艺术系的同等任期。根据国家招聘指南，我们必须对这两项记录给予同等的排名——一旦满足“三年教学经验”的要求，就明确禁止基于质量的歧视。

Ironically, the artist who would teach is often doomed before ever setting foot in the classroom. Appraisals of teaching ability get skewed during even the initial job selection process. Typical application forms allow few judgements about the quality of one’s teaching, but routinely demand some arbitrary amount of same. This makes newly-minted MFA graduates the perennial cannon fodder of the college job market, where they’re routinely axed before ever landing their first position. Moreover, the same system that ignores the potential of the newcomer often discounts the achievements of the veteran. The author recalls once serving on a university search committee while it compared two applications, one listing three years teaching for the local Parks & Recreation summer program, the other attesting to an equivalent tenure on the art faculty at Harvard. Under state hiring guidelines, we were required to accord the two records equal ranking — once the requirement for “three years teaching experience” had been met, discriminating on the basis of quality was specifically forbidden.

当教学能力被降级为统计数据时，艺术能力就变成了（有点令人惊讶）一种资产。 （顺便说一句，大学在吸引优秀艺术家方面很少遇到困难——艺术有一个令人怀疑的区别，那就是作为一种职业，你通常通过教学赚到的钱比实践赚的多。）最终的选择往往取决于一个人在艺术中的地位的强弱世界：令人印象深刻的展览和出版记录、强烈的批评性评论、同行的认可、荣誉资助或奖学金、长期参与艺术界——所有这些都有帮助。在最好的情况下，这将是一个很好的标准；在学术界，这是一场灾难。高等教育可能擅长吸引一流艺术家，但很少有能力支持一流艺术家。

When teaching ability is relegated to a statistic, artistic ability becomes (somewhat surprisingly) an asset. (As an aside, universities rarely have trouble attracting good artists — art has the dubious distinction of being one profession in which you routinely earn more by teaching it than by doing it.) Final selection often turns on the strength of one’s standing in the art world: an impressive record of exhibition and publication, strong critical reviews, recognition from peers, honorary grants or fellowships, long-term involvement in the arts community — all these things help. In the best of all worlds, this would be a fine criterion ; in the academic world, it’s a setup for disaster. Higher education may excel in attracting a first-class artist, but it’s rarely capable of supporting one.

中立地看待场景——也就是说，在纯粹的结构层面上——第一个断点只是与设置优先级有关。毕竟，很难想象将全职教学生涯置于全职艺术创作生涯之上，而在此过程中不会出现任何问题。正如古老的谚语所告诫的那样：如果你追两只兔子，你什么也抓不到。

Viewing the scene neutrally — that is, at the purely structural level — the first breakpoint has simply to do with setting priorities. It is, after all, hard to imagine placing a full-time teaching career atop a full-time artmaking career without something going awry in the process. As the old proverb cautions: if you chase two rabbits, you catch neither.

通常，艺术兔子首先消失。如果你教书，你就已经知道这个模式了。到了学校一周结束时，你已经没有多少精力去进行任何比楔入粘土或清洁刷更重要的艺术创作活动了。到学期结束时，培养未完成的工作（以及紧张的关系）很可能比创作任何新艺术更重要。危险是真实存在的（例子很多），一位教授的艺术家最终会沦落为一个更小的东西：一位以前创作过艺术的老师。一个人的表演变成了回忆，旧的工作穿梭于一系列敷衍的团体表演中，最后一切都完全消失了。就像科幻小说中某些反常的回收过程一样，产生新艺术家的系统也会产生前艺术家。

Typically, the artmaking rabbit disappears first. If you teach, you know the pattern already. By the end of the school week, you’ve little energy left for any artmaking activity of more consequence than wedging clay or cleaning brushes. By the end of the term, nurturing unfinished work (and frayed relationships) may well take precedence over making any new art at all. The danger is real (and the examples many) that an artist who teaches will eventually dwindle away to something much less: a teacher who formerly made art. One-person shows become memories, older work shuttles around a circuit of perfunctory group shows, and finally things just trail off entirely. Like some perverse recycling process from a sci-fi novel, the same system that produces new artists, produces ex-artists.

不用说，这种情况相当令人沮丧。然而，它既不是绝对的，也不是不可避免的。就此而言，你可能首先会问自己：减少艺术创作有什么问题吗？毕竟，你的孩子很重要，你的工作确实有一个有用的目的——他们也值得你投入时间和精力。除此之外，确实存在策略——艺术策略，如果你愿意的话——它允许甚至增强你在学术环境中创作新艺术的能力。不管怎样，所有这些策略大多数都建立在艺术家之间的广泛共识之上，即教学最可取的特征就是教学。

Needless to say, this scenario is fairly depressing. It is, however, neither absolute nor inevitable. For that matter you might first ask yourself: What’s wrong with producing less art? After all, your kids are important, your job does serve a useful purpose — they deserve your time and energy too. And beyond that, strategies do exist — artistic strategies, if you will — that allow for and even enhance your ability to make new art while working in an academic setting. One way or another, most all these strategies build upon the widespread consensus among artists that the single most redeeming feature of teaching is teaching.

如果你教书，你就会知道你从交流中得到的收获和你的学生一样多。毕竟，课堂工作室为您提供了一个论坛，在这里，想法就是这个领域的硬币。它可以让你从充满潜力的年轻人身上汲取能量。它让您在塑造下一代艺术中发挥作用。它让你活下去。教学是成为艺术家过程的一部分。

If you teach, you know that you gain as much from the interchange as do your students. The classroom studio, after all, gives you a forum where ideas are the coin of the realm. It allows you to draw energy from young minds filled with potential. It gives you a role in shaping the next generation of art. It keeps you alive. Teaching is part of the process of being an artist.

这里的推论是，你必须给你的学生的最好的礼物就是你自己作为一名职业艺术家的生活的例子。有一个关于哲学家乔治·桑塔亚纳（George Santayana）的故事——在哈佛大学任教时，一名学生找到他，询问他下学期将教授什么课程。桑塔亚那回答：“桑塔亚那一号、桑塔亚那二号，以及桑塔亚那三号的研讨会”。

The corollary here is that the greatest gift you have to offer your students is the example of your own life as a working artist. There’s a story told about philosopher George Santayana — that while teaching at Harvard he was approached by a student who asked what courses he would be teaching the following term. Replied Santayana: “Santayana I, Santayana II, and a seminar in Santayana III”.

就是这么基本。你的生活是成为一名艺术家过程的典范，是艺术创作过程中时间和环境、事件和情感、勇气和恐惧方式的见证和记录。你们的经历为年轻艺术家提供了一种肯定，即他们所选择的道路确实通向某个地方，而且你们都是真正的旅伴，只是在你们已经沿着这条道路旅行的时间上分开了。优秀的老师为学生提供的东西类似于人际关系中的脆弱性——一种艺术和智力上的亲密关系，让其他人看到他们是如何达到某个特定点的，而不仅仅是他们确实达到了这一点。正是这种愿意在生活和艺术之间划清界限的意愿，赋予了技术意义，并赋予了艺术目标权力，而对于学生来说，这些目标可能还需要很多年才能实现。学习是与杰出思想和杰出人士会面的自然回报。

It’s that basic. Your life is a paradigm of the process of being an artist, a witness and record to the way time and circumstance, event and emotion, courage and fear surround the making of art. Your experiences provide an affirmation to younger artists that the path they have chosen does lead somewhere, and that you are all really fellow travellers, separated only by the time you’ve already travelled down that path. What good teachers offer their students is something akin to the vulnerability found in a personal relationship — a kind of artistic and intellectual intimacy that lets others see how they reached a specific point, not simply that they did reach it. It is that willingness to lay open the line that runs between their life and their art that gives meaning to technique, and empowerment to artistic goals that for the student may still lie many years distant. Learning is the natural reward of meetings with remarkable ideas, and remarkable people.

作为一名教师，要分享这一点，你最重要的工作就是保持你的自主权——无论是作为一名艺术家还是作为一名教师。然而，保持这种自主权并非易事。持续艺术创作的障碍有时与学术政策密不可分。例如，加利福尼亚州的法律规定，州立大学的全职讲师每周每天都在校园里——即使他们没有课。由于每一天都无可救药地支离破碎，许多艺术创作过程所必需的大量时间都无可挽回地丢失了。除此之外，教学和艺术创作的时间必须经常得到支撑，以防止行政繁忙工作的侵蚀。问题的严重程度差异很大。我记得在俄勒冈大学，艺术系的会议和备忘录每周都会浪费掉二十个小时，而这些时间本来是有用的。我还（更亲切地）记得，在斯坦福大学的整个学年中，艺术系只安排了一次会议，然后因缺乏法定人数而取消了！

To share this as a teacher, your job above all is to maintain your autonomy — both as an artist and as a teacher. Maintaining that autonomy, however, is no easy matter. Obstacles to continued artmaking are sometimes hard-wired into academic policy. It is, for instance, the law in California that full-time instructors at state colleges be on campus every day of the week - even when they have no classes. With each day hopelessly fragmented, the large blocks of time essential to many artmaking processes are irretrievably lost. And beyond that, time for both teaching and artmaking must often be shored up against erosion from a steady river of administrative busywork. The magnitude of the problem varies widely. I recall that at the University of Oregon, Art Department meetings and memos routinely bled away twenty hours a week from otherwise useful time. I also recall (more fondly) that during an entire academic year at Stanford University, the Art Department scheduled exactly one meeting — and then cancelled it for lack of a quorum!

两线作战并不有趣，但无论如何你都必须保留时间来创作艺术并与你的学生分享艺术创作过程。通常，培养优质时间的最佳策略就是像瘟疫一样避免所有不那么重要的活动。多年来在旧金山州立大学负责摄影项目的艺术家/教师杰克·韦尔波特（Jack Welpott）为这种方法提供了经典模型。当被问及他如何在面对全职教员职责的情况下有效地教学并多产地创作艺术时，韦尔波特说：“从我受聘的那天起，我就开始在艺术系内树立一种怪人的声誉。我发现，在一年左右的时间里，我忘记了委员会的任务，忘记回复备忘录，错过了部门会议——好吧，过了一段时间，他们就不再要求我做所有这些事情了。”

It’s no fun fighting a two-front war, but one way or another you have to preserve time both for making art and for sharing that artmaking process with your students. Often the best strategy for cultivating quality time is to simply avoid like the plague all activities that don’t. Artist/ teacher Jack Welpott, who for many years ran the photography program at San Francisco State University, provided the classic model for this approach. When asked how he managed to teach effectively and make art prolifically in the face of full-time faculty duties, Welpott said, “From the day I was hired I began cultivating a reputation within the Art Department of being sort of a flake. I found that after a year or so of losing track of my committee assignments, forgetting to answer memos and missing departmental meetings — well, after while they just stopped asking me to do all those things.”

学生问题

STUDENT ISSUES

理想主义的伤亡率很高。 （从统计学上来说）如果你是一名艺术家，你很可能也是一名学生。这说明了人们学习艺术的愿望非常令人鼓舞，但同时也说明了那些尝试学习的人的流失率是非常不祥的。毕竟，有一个致命的推论：大多数人在不再是学生时就停止创作。

Idealism has a high casualty rate. The chances are (statistically speaking) that if you’re an artist, you’re also a student. That says something very encouraging about the desire to learn art — and something very ominous about the attrition rate of those who try. There is, after all, a deadly corollary: most people stop making art when they stop being students.

鉴于这一相当发人深省的现实检验，我们最初的主张——艺术教育确实有一些有用的目的——引发了一系列与学生相关的问题。比如这个目的到底是什么？为什么要在学术环境中学习艺术呢？或者说，“学习艺术”到底意味着什么？您是来思考普遍真理、探索新的艺术前沿还是培养名誉和财富吗？

Given that rather sobering reality check, our initial proposition — that art education does serve some useful purpose — triggers a flurry of student-related questions. Like what exactly is that purpose? Why study art in an academic setting anyway? Or for that matter, what does it even mean to “study art”? Are you there to contemplate universal truths, explore new artistic frontiers, or breed fame and fortune?

这场旨在定义帮助艺术家学习的最佳框架的竞赛已经持续了至少几个世纪，而且机会是——令人惊讶！ ——我们不会在接下来的几句话中突然解决这个问题。你可以汇集好的论据、成功的例子、杰出的毕业生——以及令人难以忍受的皈依者——来支持所有可能的道路中的任何一条。理想情况下，您的选择范围包括大学、艺术学校、工作室、学徒、游学、自学等。根据经验，它们会内爆成两个领域：大学和其他一切。

This contest to define the best framework for helping artists learn has been going on for at least a couple of centuries now, and chances are — surprise! — that we won’t suddenly resolve the issue in the next few sentences. You can corral good arguments, successful examples, prominent graduates — and insufferable converts — to champion any of a whole flock of possible pathways. Ideally your options range across colleges, art schools, workshops, apprenticeships, study tours, self-teaching and more. Empirically, they implode to a field of two: the University, and everything else.

这很大程度上是一个结构问题。大学的优势在于你可以同时学习艺术、物理、人类学、心理学和文学。 “其他一切”（例如学徒期）的基本优势在于，您可以始终将精力完全投入到艺术上。

It’s largely a question of structure. The strength of the university lies in the fact that you can study art, physics, anthropology, psychology and literature all at once. The basic strength of the “everything else” — an apprenticeship, for instance — is that you can devote your energies solely to art all the time.

毫不奇怪，每种方法也都有其内在的局限性。事实证明，这所大学可能过于庞大和缺乏人情味，无法在手艺和愿景扎根之前培养出经过长期自我怀疑的年轻艺术家。此外，许多大学艺术课程都是选修课，它们的重点和强度被非专业人士稀释，他们没有为该学科带来个人投资。 （如果微积分被定制为艺术专业的一门“有趣”的选修课，那么数学专业的学生无疑也会觉得他们的学习被耽误了！）相反，工作室或小型音乐学院可能会过于专注于艺术，以至于你与你需要的更大的世界失去了联系。探索。无论如何，使大多数艺术教育发挥作用的结构——为艺术创作提供庇护和支持性的环境，以及（暂时）脱离日常收入生产跑步机的邀请——一旦你开始工作，就会立即消失。放学了。令人沮丧的事实是，世界其他地方既不关心你是否创作艺术品，也没有兴趣购买你是否创作的艺术品。对大多数人来说，艺术作为一种职业也许可以接受，但作为一种职业肯定不行。 （或者正如作者的一位学生悲伤地指出的那样，“大多数职业都是有薪水的。”）简单地说，创作艺术并不被认为是一项真正的工作。

Not surprisingly, each approach also carries built-in limitations. The university may prove too large and impersonal to nurture a young artist through long periods of self-doubt before craft and vision take hold. In addition, many university art courses are electives, their focus and intensity diluted by non-majors who bring no personal investment to the subject. (If calculus were tailored as a “fun” elective for art majors, math majors would doubtless feel their studies were being retarded too!) Conversely, a workshop or small conservatory may focus so tightly on art that you lose touch with larger worlds you need to explore. And in any case the very structure that makes most art education work — a sheltered and supportive environment for artmaking, and an invitation to disengage (for a time) from the day-to-day treadmill of income production-vanishes instantly once you’re out of school. The discouraging truth is that the rest of the world neither cares whether you make art, nor has much interest in buying it if you do. As far as most people are concerned, art may be acceptable as a profession, but certainly not as an occupation. (Or as one of the authors’ students dolefully pointed out, “Most professions come with a salary.”) Simply put, making art is not considered a real job.

但是，大学的作用始终是提供教育，这与提供培训相比只是一小步，但却是重要的一步。培训让您为工作做好准备；教育为你的生活做好准备。但如果说大学从长远来看为丰富的跨学科成就奠定了基础，那么它在短期内却因提供很少的就业技能而臭名昭著。艺术评论家 AD Coleman 讲述了一位大学艺术老师的故事，焦虑的家长经常询问他的孩子毕业后是否有工作。教授总是会回答：“这不是他们从我身上学到的任何东西的直接结果！”这种方法无论多么真实，都很难让人放心：许多学生认为毕业就等于在毫无准备的情况下被永远推入某个张开的深渊。

But then, the role of the university has always been to provide an education, which is a small but significant step removed from providing training. Training prepares you for a job; an education prepares you for life. But if the university lays the foundation for rich and interdisciplinary achievement over the long run, it’s notorious for providing few employable skills in the short run. Art critic A.D. Coleman tells the story of a university art teacher who was frequently asked by anxious parents whether there would be jobs awaiting their children upon graduation. Invariably the professor would reply, “Not as a direct result of anything they’ll learn from me!” This approach, however truthful, is rarely reassuring: many students view graduating as tantamount to being pushed, unprepared, into some yawning abyss — forever.

这种前景令人畏惧，以至于许多艺术家在完成学业之前就辍学了。其他人确实毕业了，但后来迫于经济压力，找不到继续艺术创作的方法。还有一些人通过进入研究生课程来延长死亡观察时间。后一种方法置于已经完成的十五年教育之上，充其量是多余的，而且实际上往往对学生的艺术创作能力有害。 （杰里·尤尔斯曼（Jerry Uelsmann）将哄骗研究生艺术视为“教育过度者康复”的过程！）

That prospect is daunting enough that many artists drop out before ever completing their studies; others do graduate, but then — pressed by economics — find no way to continue artmaking afterwards. And yet others prolong the death-watch by entering graduate programs. The latter approach, placed atop fifteen-odd years of already-completed education, is superfluous at best and often actually harmful to the student’s artmaking capacity. (Jerry Uelsmann refers to coaxing art from graduate students as a process of “rehabilitating the over-educated”!)

这整个场景是一场学术界很少讨论的悲剧，即使如此，也很少被认为是系统的失败。从安全的终身制角度来看，系统反而对学生的失败表示遗憾。有人告诉我，糟糕的治疗师总是责怪他们的客户。

This whole scenario is a tragedy seldom addressed by academics, and even then is rarely acknowledged as a failure of the system. Watching from a safely tenured vantage point, the system instead laments the failure of the student. Poor therapists, I’m told, always blame their clients.

面对如此渺茫的艺术生存机会（更不用说成功），高年级学生成群结队地转向社会认可的艺术家工作：教学。这是一个危险的过程。想要教书的理由有很多，但回避未知并不是其中之一。每月薪水的保障与艺术探究的冒险性很难混合在一起。

Faced with such poor odds for artistic survival (much less success), upper division students migrate in droves toward the one job for artists that society does validate : Teaching. This is a perilous course. There are many good reasons for wanting to teach, but avoiding the unknown is not one of them. The security of a monthly paycheck mixes poorly with the risk-taking of artistic inquiry.

令人沮丧的事实是，硕士学位的设立主要是为了提供——然后满足——获得教学工作的先决条件。这实际上使整个系统成为一个金字塔计划：只有当有十几个新生与每个即将毕业的艺术硕士相匹配时，它才有效。无论好坏，这座金字塔几年前就开始崩溃。今天的艺术教育是一个稳定的世界，几乎没有创造任何新的就业机会。从统计学上来说，如果你学习艺术的目的是教授艺术，那么你最终可能会从事销售工作。你学习艺术创作是为了了解艺术创作。

The discouraging truth is that MFA degrees were created largely to provide — and then satisfy — a prerequisite for obtaining teaching jobs. This in effect rendered the entire system a pyramid scheme: it worked only so long as there were a dozen entering freshmen to match with each graduating MFA. For better or worse, this pyramid began crumbling years ago. Today art education is a steady-state universe, creating virtually no new jobs at all. Chances are — statistically speaking — that if you study art with a goal of teaching it, you’ll end up with a career in sales. You study artmaking in order to learn about artmaking.

关于艺术的书籍

BOOKS ABOUT ART

关于艺术的书籍，甚至是关于艺术家的书籍，通常都很少谈论实际的艺术创作。他们可能会提供一些关于“艺术家的奋斗”的浪漫寓言，但普遍的前提仍然是艺术显然是天才（或​​者有时是疯狂）的领域。接受这个前提不可避免地会得出这样的结论：虽然艺术应该被读者理解、享受或钦佩，但它绝对不应该由读者来完成。一旦读者和艺术家之间的亲缘关系被否认，艺术本身就变成了一种奇怪的异物——一种需要从安全的分析距离来指出和戳戳的东西。对于批评家来说，艺术是一个名词。

Books on art, even books on artists, characteristically have little to say about actually making art. They may offer a sprinkling of romantic parables about “the artist’s struggle”, but the prevailing premise remains that art is clearly the province of genius (or, on occasion, madness). Accepting this premise leads inescapably to the conclusion that while art should be understood or enjoyed or admired by the reader, it most certainly should not be done by the reader. And once that kinship between reader and artist has been denied, art itself becomes a strange foreign object — something to be pointed to and poked at from a safe analytical distance. To the critic, art is a noun.

显然，这里的翻译遗漏了一些东西。具体来说，艺术家们失去的正是他们一生中大部分时间所做的事情：即学习创作对他们来说重要的作品。艺术家从其他艺术家那里学到的并不是历史或技术（尽管我们也学到了很多）；而是更多。我们从别人的艺术创作中真正获得的是联想的勇气。随着恐惧的分享，接触的深度也会加深，从而解除恐惧，而这来自于将艺术视为过程，将艺术家视为志趣相投的人。对于艺术家来说，艺术是一个动词。

Clearly, something’s getting lost in the translation here. What gets lost, quite specifically, is the very thing artists spend the better part of their lives doing: namely, learning to make work that matters to them. What artists learn from other artists is not so much history or technique (although we learn tons of that too); what we really gain from the artmaking of others is courage-by-association. Depth of contact grows as fears are shared — and thereby disarmed — and this comes from embracing art as process, and artists as kindred spirits. To the artist, art is a verb.

这种区别在现实世界中具有重要的基础。至少足以支持这一具有争议性的——即使不是完全无懈可击的——主张，即从观看成品艺术中无法学到任何真正有用的东西。至少其他艺术家在创作自己的艺术时没有什么可以有用的。每个艺术家面临的真正关键的决定——比如，知道何时停止——无法从最终结果中得知。就这一点而言，一件成品几乎没有提供关于艺术家在制作该物品时权衡的任何问题的宝贵线索。

This distinction has substantial footing in the real world. Substantial enough at least to support the provocative — if not entirely airtight — proposition that nothing really useful can be learned from viewing finished art. At least nothing other artists can usefully apply in making their own art. The really critical decisions facing every artist — like, say, knowing when to stop — cannot be learned from viewing end results. For that matter, a finished piece gives precious few clues as to any questions the artist weighed while making the object.

你知道这是怎么回事：在工作的热度中，你的头脑中的想法在个人、共同和普遍关注的混乱中跳来跳去。 （但是哦，是的：对于每个艺术家来说，都是一个非常具体的混乱！）从身体上来说，当你在阳光下出汗、回应现场观众时，你可能处于最佳状态，或者——就像作者写下这句话时——独自喝一杯酒放松一下。很容易想象出一百种不同的心态可能导致爱德华·韦斯顿拍摄他花园里的蔬菜，但我们没有丝毫可能知道我们对这一百种心态的最佳猜测是否与他的实际状态相符。同样前景不佳的是，最终的印刷品将提供任何指导来理解将第 30 号辣椒转变为第 30 号辣椒的心理状态。

You know how it is: in the heat of working, the thoughts in your head ricochet among a bewildering jumble of personal, shared and universal concerns. (But oh yes: for each artist, a very specific jumble!) And physically, you may be at your best when you’re sweating in the sun, responding to a live audience, or — like the author as he writes this sentence — relaxing alone with a glass of wine. It’s easy to imagine a hundred different states of mind that might have led Edward Weston to photograph his garden vegetables, but we have not the faintest possibility of knowing whether our best guess from that hundred matched his actual state. And equally poor prospects that the resulting print will provide any guide to understanding the state of mind that transformed pepper number thirty into Pepper #30.

这种僵局可能导致埃兹拉·庞德（Ezra Pound）说，他从观看一件好的艺术作品中学到的一件事是，另一位艺术家很好地完成了他的工作，因此他[庞德]可以自由地探索另一个方向。艺术评论家面临着一个更令人烦恼的困境：简而言之，他无法通过观看艺术家来解释完成的艺术作品，也无法通过观看完成的艺术作品来解释艺术家。因此，艺术被当作某种外来物体来对待，从远处分析它与政治、文化、历史以及（乱伦的）与其他艺术运动的关系。或者更辛苦地按连续的风格、时期和“杰作”分类。教科书将艺术史简化为可复制的艺术史，从而使问题变得更加复杂。维米尔 (VerMeer) 微型画和比尔施塔特 (Bierstadt) 壁画被分配了相同的四分之一页壁龛，不适合半色调的艺术作品完全消失。

This impasse may be what led Ezra Pound to remark that the one thing he learned from viewing a good piece of art was that the other artist had done his job well, and thus he [Pound] was freed to explore another direction. The art critic faces a more vexing dilemma: in a nutshell, he cannot explain the finished art piece from looking at the artist, and he cannot explain the artist by viewing the finished art piece. And so art is treated like some foreign object, analyzed from afar for its relationship to politics and culture and history and (incestuously) to other art movements. Or more drudgerously catalogued into successive styles, periods and “Masterworks.” Textbooks compound the problem by reducing the history of art to the history of art that can be reproduced. VerMeer miniatures and Bierstadt murals are allotted identical quarter-page niches, and art that doesn’t lend itself to halftoning disappears entirely.

我们并不是想在这里设置稻草人，当然，偶尔退后一步以了解历史（并幻想一下您在其中的位置）也没有什么坏处。要点很简单，这些都不能帮助你让颜料按照你需要的方式落到画布上。这些都无法告诉您第一次将锤子放在大理石上是什么感觉。所有这些都无法传达走上舞台面对一千人的恐惧。对于职业艺术家来说，最好的艺术著作不是分析性的或按时间顺序排列的，而是按时间顺序排列的。它们是自传体的。毕竟，艺术家就在那里。

We’re not trying to set up straw men here, and certainly there’s no harm in standing back occasionally to gain an overview of history (and fantasize about your place in it). The point is simply that none of this will help you to get the paint to fall to the canvas the way you need it to. None of this will tell you what it’s like to set the hammer to the marble for the first time. None of this will convey the terror of walking onto the stage to face a thousand people. For the working artist, the very best writings on art are not analytical or chronological; they are autobiographical. The artist, after all, was there.

中国画的一个古老信条认为，大师画的不是被创造的事物，而是创造它的力量。同样，关于艺术的最佳著作描绘的不是成品，而是创造它的过程。爱德华·韦斯顿在他的日记中对暴露那一刻所产生的无数影响进行了亲密的描述（有些人会说太亲密了）。在《双螺旋》中，沃森和克里克（以更为克制的风格）记录了导致他们发现 DNA 分子结构的猜想和实验。在 Daybook 中，艺术家 Anne Truitt 开始了为期一年的日记（后来延长到七期），充满了智慧和洞察力。韦斯顿的激情、沃森的逻辑、特鲁伊特的内省：这些都是过程的驱动机制。每个艺术家都有同样深切关注的问题。每个艺术家都可以写这样一本书。你可以写这样一本书。

An ancient tenet in Chinese painting holds that the Master paints not the created thing, but the forces that created it. Likewise, the best writing about art depicts not the finished piece, but the processes that created it. In his Daybooks, Edward Weston offered an intimate account (too intimate, some would say) of the myriad of influences bracketing the moment of exposure. In The Double Helix, Watson & Crick recorded (in more restrained style) the conjecture and experiments that led to their discovery of the molecular structure of DNA. In Daybook, artist Anne Truitt began a one-year journal (which in due time stretched to seven) filled with wisdom and insight. Weston’s passion, Watson’s logic, Truitt’s introspection: these are all driving mechanisms of process. Every artist has issues that lie similarly close to the heart. Every artist could write such a book. You could write such a book.

VIII.

概念世界

CONCEPTUAL WORLDS

您得到的答案取决于您提出的问题。
The answers you get depend upon the questions you ask.

— 托马斯·库恩

— Thomas Kuhn

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

作家亨利·詹姆斯曾经提出了三个可以有效地应用于艺术家作品的问题。前两个问题非常简单：艺术家想要实现什么目标？他/她成功了吗？第三个很有趣：这值得做吗？

WRITER HENRY JAMES once proposed three questions you could productively put to an artist’s work. The first two were disarmingly straightforward: What was the artist trying to achieve? Did he/she succeed? The third’s a zinger: Was it worth doing?

仅前两个问题就值回票价了。他们所探讨的艺术水平可以直接根据现实世界的价值观和经验进行测试；他们承诺让你接受创作者的观点并融入你对作品的理解中。简而言之，他们要求你对作品本身做出回应，而不是首先将其通过一些标有行为主义、女权主义、后现代主义或随便主义的美学过滤器。1

Those first two questions alone are worth the price of admission. They address art at a level that can be tested directly against real-world values and experience; they commit you to accepting the perspective of the maker into your own understanding of the work. In short, they ask you to respond to the work itself, without first pushing it through some aesthetic filter labelled Behaviorism, Feminism, Postmodernism or Whateverism. 1

但这是第三个问题——值得这样做吗？ ——这真正打开了宇宙。什么是值得做的？有些艺术问题本质上比其他问题更有趣吗？更相关吗？更有意义吗？更加困难？更具挑衅性？每一位当代艺术家都会带着这样的问题跳舞。

But it’s that third question — Was it worth doing? — that truly opens the universe. What is worth doing? Are some artistic problems inherently more interesting than others? More relevant? More meaningful? More difficult? More provocative? Every contemporary artist dances with such questions as these.

想法与技术

IDEAS & TECHNIQUE

挑衅性的艺术不仅挑战观众，也挑战其创作者。艺术的失败往往不是因为艺术家未能应对挑战，而是因为从一开始就不存在挑战。可以把它想象成奥运会跳水：即使是在低台上进行完美的天鹅跳水，你也不会赢得高分。许多人迅速达到的简单完美并没有什么回报。

Provocative art challenges not only the viewer, but also its maker. Art that falls short often does so not because the artist failed to meet the challenge, but because there was never a challenge there in the first place. Think of it like Olympic diving: you don’t win high points for making even the perfect swan dive off the low board. There’s little reward in an easy perfection quickly reached by many.

抵制艺术中的完美模式似乎很奇怪，因为它们在生活的许多其他方面都被接受。尽管天鹅跳水，奥运会本身还是建立在严格框架内取得伟大成就的理念之上的。毕竟，百米短跑比赛中的荣誉并不属于那些表现出令人着迷的个人跳跃的跑步者，而是属于第一个达到设定目标的人。正如安妮·特鲁伊特（Anne Truitt）在她的日记中所指出的那样，艺术家的负担是“律师和医生实践他们的使命。水管工和木匠知道他们将被要求做什么。他们不必把作品从自己身上剥离出来，发现它的规律，然后把自己翻个底朝天地呈现在公众面前。”

To resist models of perfection in art may seem strange, given their acceptance in so many other facets of living. Swan dives notwithstanding, the Olympic Games themselves are founded on the concept of great achievement within a strict framework. Honors in the hundred meter dash, after all, go not to the runner who displays some intriguing personal skip, but to the one who reaches the set goal first. The burden for the artist, as Anne Truitt observes in her Daybook, is that “The lawyer and the doctor practice their callings. The plumber and the carpenter know what they will be called upon to do. They do not have to spin the work out of themselves, discover its laws, and then present themselves turned inside out to the public gaze.”

显然，这不是一个容易让自己置身其中的空间。事实上，许多艺术家也没有这样做。需要持续保证自己走在正确轨道上的艺术家通常会寻找能够提供明确目标和可衡量反馈的挑战——也就是说，技术挑战。其根本问题并不在于追求技术卓越是错误的，而只是将其作为首要目标本末倒置。我们不会长久记住那些比其他人更勤奋地遵守规则的艺术家。我们记得那些创造了不可避免地遵循“规则”的艺术的人。

Clearly that is not an easy space to put yourself in. And indeed many artists don’t. Artists who need ongoing reassurance that they’re on the right track routinely seek out challenges that offer the clear goals and measurable feedback — which is to say, technical challenges. The underlying problem with this is not that the pursuit of technical excellence is wrong, exactly, but simply that making it the primary goal puts the cart before the horse. We do not long remember those artists who followed the rules more diligently than anyone else. We remember those who made the art from which the “rules” inevitably follow.

更阴险的是，技术标准总能披上审美标准的所有外衣。例如，人们普遍认为，为照片冲印赋予丰富的黑色和微妙的高价值是一个真正的挑战。然而，在某种程度上，这种看似中立的观察引起了（尤其是在西海岸风景摄影师中）一种道德要求，即照片应该表现出如此完美的色调。随着这一流派的确立，判断印刷品的标准越来越集中于产生所需色调所需的精湛技术性能。微妙的语气通常确实成为主要内容。同样的命运也降临到了二十世纪的交响乐身上，它们受到神秘的和声理论的诱惑，以至于其挑剔的听众逐渐转向其他仍然扎根于现实世界节奏的习语（如爵士乐）。

More insidiously, technical standards have a way of taking on all the trappings of aesthetic standards. There is widespread agreement, for instance, that it’s a genuine challenge to impart rich blacks and subtle high values to a photographic print. At some point, however, this seemingly neutral observation gave rise (especially among West Coast landscape photographers) to a moral imperative that photographs should display such tonal perfection. As this genre established itself, criteria for judging a print increasingly concentrated on the virtuoso technical performance needed to produce the desired tones. Subtlety of tone became, often quite literally, the primary content. An equivalent fate befell much twentieth century symphonic music, which was seduced by arcane harmonic theory to the degree that its critical audience drifted progressively to other idioms (like jazz) that remained grounded in the rhythms of the real world.

对于观众来说，他们对作品的完成方式几乎没有情感投入，主要为了展示技术精湛而创作的艺术往往是美丽的、引人注目的、优雅的……而且是空洞的。对于对一切事物都有情感投入的艺术家来说，更多的是走向哪个方向的问题。与其他挑战相比，技术问题的最终缺点不是难，而是容易。

To the viewer, who has little emotional investment in how the work gets done, art made primarily to display technical virtuosity is often beautiful, striking, elegant...and vacant. To the artist, who has an emotional investment in everything, it’s more a question of which direction to reach. Compared to other challenges, the ultimate shortcoming of technical problems is not that they’re hard, but that they’re easy.

当然，艺术家们是最后一个承认这一点的人，哪怕只是因为对花费大量时间建造模具或铸造铁水的英雄描述仍然是艺术对话中的礼仪。但是，虽然掌握技术既困难又耗时，但本质上来说，实现已经定义的目标（“正确答案”）仍然比将新想法付诸实践更容易。把天使的脚画在别人的杰作上比发现天使住在你自己内心的地方更容易。如果技术是艺术的核心问题，那么我们著名艺术家蜡像馆的提名人将是圣昆汀的终身教授，他花了二十年时间用牙签建造了埃菲尔铁塔的完美复制品。 （嗯，是的，它以自己的方式令人印象深刻！）但这不是它的工作方式。简而言之，涉及思想的艺术比涉及技术的艺术更有趣。

Artists, naturally, would be the last to admit that, if only because heroic accounts of grueling hours spent building the mold or casting the hot metal remain de rigueur of artistic conversation. But while mastering technique is difficult and time-consuming, it’s still inherently easier to reach an already defined goal — a “right answer” — than to give form to a new idea. It’s easier to paint in the angel’s feet to another’s master-work than to discover where the angels live within yourself. If technique were the core issue in art, our nominee for the Famous Artists Wax Museum would be the lifer at San Quentin who spent twenty years constructing a perfect replica of the Eiffel Tower from toothpicks. (And well, yes, in its own way it was pretty impressive!) But that’s not the way it works. Simply put, art that deals with ideas is more interesting than art that deals with technique.

工艺

CRAFT

是的，艺术和手工艺之间是有区别的——只是这两个术语都充满了模糊的定义，以至于在它们之间进行清晰的区分几乎是不可能的。我们将在这里进行模糊区分。

Yes, there is a difference between art and craft — it’s just that both terms are so overgrown with fuzzy definitions that drawing a clear distinction between them is close to impossible. We’ll settle here for a fuzzy distinction.

想到手工艺，你就会想到萨姆·马洛夫 (Sam Maloof) 制作的家具、文艺复兴博览会上炫耀的手工服装、工业革命之前制造的一切。想到艺术，你就会想到《战争与和平》、贝多芬协奏曲、《蒙娜丽莎》。显然，这两个学科都产生了好的东西、有价值的东西，有时甚至是切实有用的东西，乍一看它们之间的区别似乎非常清楚。

Think of craft and you think of furniture shaped by Sam Maloof, of handmade clothing flaunted at Renaissance Faires, of everything made before the Industrial Revolution. Think of art and you think of War and Peace, a Beethoven concerto, the Mona Lisa. Both disciplines obviously yield good things, valuable things, sometimes tangibly useful things, and at first pass the distinction between them seems perfectly clear.

但《蒙娜丽莎》真的是艺术吗？那么，如果有一份完美得无法察觉的《蒙娜丽莎》复制品呢？这种比较（无论多么狡猾）指出了这样一个事实：孤立地看待任何一件作品并明确地规定“这是艺术”或“这是工艺”是非常困难的，甚至可能是不可能的。找出这种差异意味着比较同一个人连续制作的作品。

But is the Mona Lisa really art? Well then, what about an undetectably perfect copy of the Mona Lisa? That comparison (however sneaky) points up the fact that it’s surprisingly difficult, maybe even impossible, to view any single work in isolation and rule definitively, “This is art” or “This is craft.” Striking that difference means comparing successive pieces made by the same person.

从本质上讲，艺术植根于作品之间的概念飞跃，而不是作品本身。简而言之，从一件艺术品到下一件艺术品的概念跳跃比从一件工艺品到下一件工艺品的概念跳跃更大。最终的结果是，艺术不如手工艺那么精致，但更具创新性。五架施坦威三角钢琴（显然是精湛工艺的作品）之间的差异与您在这些乐器上演奏的五首贝多芬钢琴协奏曲之间的差异相比很小。

In essence, art lies embedded in the conceptual leap between pieces, not in the pieces themselves. And simply put, there’s a greater conceptual jump from one work of art to the next than from one work of craft to the next. The net result is that art is less polished — but more innovative — than craft. The differences between five Steinway grand pianos — demonstrably works of consummate craftsmanship — are small compared to the differences between the five Beethoven Piano concerti you might perform on those instruments.

一件工艺品通常是根据特定的模板制作的，有时是一个非常困难的模板，需要多年的实践学徒才能掌握。令人震惊的是，几乎所有真正伟大的小提琴都是由居住在几个街区内的几个工匠在几年内制作而成的。这一切都发生在三个世纪前的一个偏远的意大利村庄。安东尼奥·斯特拉迪瓦里 (Antonio Stradivari) 和他的工匠同行的成就指出了艺术与工艺之间的一个真正区别：有了工艺，完美就成为可能。从这个意义上说，西方对工艺的定义与东方对艺术的定义非常吻合。在东方文化中，忠实继承老一代大师传统的艺术受到尊重；在西方，它被视为衍生品。

A work of craft is typically made to fit a specific template, sometimes a painstakingly difficult template requiring years of hands-on apprenticeship to master. It’s staggering to realize that nearly all the truly great violins ever produced were made in the course of a few years by a few artisans living within a few blocks of each other. All this in a remote Italian village, three centuries ago. The accomplishments of Antonio Stradivari and his fellow craftsmen point up one real difference between art and craft: with craft, perfection is possible. In that sense the Western definition of craft closely matches the Eastern definition of art. In Eastern cultures, art that faithfully carries forward the tradition of an elder master is honored; in the West it is put down as derivative.

但奇怪的是，随着时间的推移，大多数艺术家的作品的进展都是从艺术到工艺的进展。就像任何单件作品走向完成时想象力让位于执行一样，艺术家的重大发现通常很早就出现，然后用一生的时间来充实和完善这些发现。正如禅宗谚语所说，对于初学者来说，道路很多，对于高级者来说，道路很少。

Yet curiously, the progression of most artists’ work over time is a progression from art toward craft. In the same manner that imagination gives way to execution as any single work builds toward completion, an artist’s major discoveries usually come early on, and a lifetime is then allotted to fill out and refine those discoveries. As the Zen proverb suggests, for the beginner there are many paths, for the advanced, few.

在这条道路上的任何时刻，作为一名艺术家，你的工作就是将工艺推向极限——而不是被它困住。陷阱就是完美：除非你的工作不断产生新的、未解决的问题，否则你的下一个工作没有理由与上一个有任何不同。艺术与手工艺的区别不在于你手中持有的工具，而在于指导它们的思维定势。对于工匠来说，工艺本身就是目的。对于艺术家来说，工艺是表达您的愿景的工具。工艺是艺术的可见边缘。

At any point along that path, your job as an artist is to push craft to its limits — without being trapped by it. The trap is perfection: unless your work continually generates new and unresolved issues, there’s no reason for your next work to be any different from the last. The difference between art and craft lies not in the tools you hold in your hands, but in the mental set that guides them. For the artisan, craft is an end in itself. For you, the artist, craft is the vehicle for expressing your vision. Craft is the visible edge of art.

新工作

NEW WORK

在常规的艺术发展中，新作品并不会让旧作品变得虚假——它会让旧作品变得更加虚假，更像是一种技巧行为。较旧的作品常常让艺术家感到尴尬，因为它感觉像是由一个更年轻、更天真的人创作的——一个对作品中的自负和努力一无所知的人。奇怪的是，早期的工作常常给人一种既太费力又太简单的感觉。这个是正常的。新的工作应该取代旧的工作。如果它是通过让旧作品变得不充分、不充分和不完整来实现的——那么，这就是生活。 （弗兰克·劳埃德·赖特（Frank Lloyd Wright）建议年轻建筑师在他们早期的建筑周围种植常春藤，暗示随着时间的推移，常春藤会生长以掩盖他们“年轻时的轻率行为”。）旧作品告诉你当时你在关注什么；旧作品会告诉你你当时在关注什么；而旧作品会告诉你，你当时在关注什么；而旧作品会告诉你，你当时在关注什么。新作品通过指出你之前没有注意到的内容来对旧作品进行评论。现在这一切都将是顺利和可爱的，除了新的工作可以立即变成旧的工作——有时，确实，在工作完成后的那一刻。欣赏完成的作品可能只需要眨眼的时间。这当然令人不快——但这是一个好兆头。

In routine artistic growth, new work doesn’t make the old work false — it makes it more artificial, more an act of artifice. Older work is ofttimes an embarrassment to the artist because it feels like it was made by a younger, more naive person — one who was ignorant of the pretension and striving in the work. Earlier work often feels, curiously, both too labored and too simple. This is normal. New work is supposed to replace old work. If it does so by making the old work inadequate, insufficient and incomplete — well, that’s life. (Frank Lloyd Wright advised young architects to plant ivy all around their early buildings, suggesting that in time it would grow to cover their “youthful indiscretions.”) Old work tells you what you were paying attention to then; new work comments on the old by pointing out what you were not previously paying attention to. Now this would all be smooth and lovely except that new work can turn to old work in an instant — sometimes, indeed, in the instant immediately following the work’s completion. Savoring finished work may last only an eye-blink. This is certainly unpleasant — but it’s a good sign.

习惯

HABITS

习惯是心灵的余光。他们在有意识的决策水平以下不断地进行思考，用概念性的眼光审视情况，而忽略了其中的大部分内容。这个理论很简单：自动对熟悉的做出反应，然后你就可以自由地有选择地对不熟悉的做出反应。然而，应用这一理论有点冒险。养成太多的习惯，生活就会陷入令人麻木的例行公事。太少了，应对源源不断的细节会让你不知所措（就像某些精神药物的使用者一旦注意到每一片草叶都在生长时就会着迷一样。）

Habits are the peripheral vision of the mind. Churning away just below the level of conscious decision-making, they scan a situation with a conceptual eye to disregarding most of it. The theory is simple enough: respond automatically to the familiar, and you’re then free to respond selectively to the unfamiliar. Applying that theory, however, is a bit dicier. Indulge too many habits, and life sinks into mind-dulling routine. Too few, and coping with a relentless stream of incoming detail overwhelms you (much as users of certain psychotropic drugs become mesmerized once they notice that every blade of grass is growing.)

这都是一个平衡问题，而艺术创作有助于实现这种平衡。对于艺术家来说，画板或笔记本是探索的许可——连续几分钟站在那里，盯着树桩是完全可以接受的。有时你需要审视森林，有时你需要触摸一棵树——如果你不能理解两者，你就永远无法完全理解其中任何一个。观察事物可以增强你对自己经历的独特模式以及塑造所有经历的元模式的好奇心。所有这些都表明了一种有用的艺术创作方法：注意你注意到的物体。 （例如，再读一遍这句话。）或者换句话说：制作会说话的物体，然后听它们说话。

It’s all a matter of balance, and making art helps achieve that balance. For the artist, a sketchpad or a notebook is a license to explore — it becomes entirely acceptable to stand there, for minutes on end, staring at a tree stump. Sometimes you need to scan the forest, sometimes you need to touch a single tree — if you can’t apprehend both, you’ll never entirely comprehend either. To see things is to enhance your sense of wonder both for the singular pattern of your own experience, and for the meta-patterns that shape all experience. All this suggests a useful working approach to making art: notice the objects you notice. (e.g. Read that sentence again.) Or put another way: make objects that talk — and then listen to them.

在艺术界，习惯受到很多负面报道。好吧，这并不奇怪——在一个反传统者蓬勃发展、探索新想法已成为当务之急的领域，谁愿意呆在家里和熟悉的东西在一起呢？确实，你为什么要这么做？毕竟，如果您对自己正在做的事情感到满意，那么您以前可能已经经历过这种情况。然而，除非大量的细节可以信赖于习惯，否则更大的问题永远不会被解决。如果艺术要滋养意识，就必须鼓励和质疑习惯性反应。需要的是在你自己对世界的重复反应中寻找，揭露那些不真实或无用的，并改变它们。剩下的就是你的了：培养它们。无论如何，你没有太多选择。正如数学家 GK 切斯特顿 (GK Chesterton) 讽刺地指出的那样：“你可以让事物摆脱外来或偶然的法则，但无法摆脱它们本身的本质法则。不要到处鼓励三角形冲破三边的牢笼；如果一个三角形的三条边断裂，它的生命就会以可悲的方式结束。”

Habits get a lot of bad press in the art world. Well, no surprises there — in a field where iconoclasts flourish and exploring new ideas is the order of the day, who wants to stay home with the familiar? Indeed, why should you? After all, if you’re comfortable with what you’re doing, you’ve probably been there before. Yet larger questions will never get engaged unless huge amounts of detail can be trusted to habit. If art is to nourish consciousness, habitual reactions must be encouraged as well as questioned. The need is to search among your own repeated reactions to the world, expose those that are not true or useful, and change them. The remainder are yours: cultivate them. In any case, you haven’t much choice. As mathematician G.K. Chesterton wryly noted, “You can free things from alien or accidental laws, but not from the laws of their own nature. Do not go about encouraging triangles to break out of the prison of their three sides; if a triangle breaks out of its three sides, its life comes to a lamentable end.”

当然，诀窍是培养你的习惯性手势。不幸的是，外界对艺术家的这一努力并没有过于宽容。受基因、父母、教会、工作和人际关系影响的习惯被称为性格特征。从其他艺术家那里习得的习惯被称为——取决于它们所采取的形式——矫揉造作、衍生、抄袭或伪造。你的作者发现这个判断有点严厉，特别是因为它使艺术家在早期艺术创作中最常借鉴的来源无效。

The trick, of course, is cultivating habitual gestures that are yours. Unfortunately the outside world is not overly charitable to the artist in this effort. Habits imprinted by genes, parents, church, jobs and relationships are called character traits. Habits acquired from other artists are called — depending on the form they take — affectation, derivation, plagiarism or forgery. Your authors find this judgement a trifle harsh, especially since it invalidates the very source artists most often draw from in their early artmaking.

然而，这对艺术家的影响并不像评论家想象的那么可怕。许多人在发现似乎能直接与他们对话的艺术作品后，首先对艺术做出了深刻的反应——事实上，对世界做出了深刻的反应。那么，如果他们在开始自己创作艺术时，开始模仿带来这一启示的艺术或艺术家，那就不足为奇了。例如，贝多芬的早期作品就显示出他的老师弗兰茨·约瑟夫·海顿的明显影响。事实上，大多数早期作品仅暗示了主题和姿态，如果潜力没有被浪费的话，这些主题和姿态将在后来的成熟作品中成为艺术家的特征签名。然而，一开始，无论什么主题和技术吸引你，很可能已经有人在同一方向进行过实验。这是不可避免的：制作任何艺术作品都不可避免地涉及艺术家几个世纪以来使用的大主题和基本技术。寻找自己的作品是一个从每一个痕迹中提炼出符合你自己精神的痕迹的过程。

The effect on the artist, however, isn’t nearly so dire as critics would have it appear. Many people first respond deeply to art — indeed, respond deeply to the world — upon finding works of art that seem to speak directly to them. Small surprise, then, if upon setting out to make art themselves, they begin by emulating the art or artist that brought this revelation. Beethoven’s early compositions, for instance, show the unmistakable influence of his teacher, Franz Joseph Haydn. Most early work, in fact, only hints at the themes and gestures that will — if the potential isn’t squandered — emerge as the artist’s characteristic signature in later, mature work. At the outset, however, chances are that whatever theme and technique attract you, someone has already experimented in the same direction. This is unavoidable : making any art piece inevitably engages the large themes and basic techniques that artists have used for centuries. Finding your own work is a process of distilling from each those traces that ring true to your own spirit.

一旦养成，艺术习惯就会根深蒂固、可靠、有益且方便。此外，习惯在风格上也很重要。从某种意义上说，习惯就是风格。未经考虑的姿态、重复的措辞、自动选择、对主题和材料的特有反应——这些正是我们所说的风格。很多人，包括艺术家，都认为这是一种美德。然而，仔细观察，风格并不是一种美德，而是一种必然性——任何事情重复几次之后不可避免的结果。艺术家习惯性的姿势出现在任何发展到足以被称为作品主体的作品中。风格不是优秀作品的一个方面，而是所有作品的一个方面。风格是习惯的自然结果。

Once developed, art habits are deep-seated, reliable, helpful, and convenient. Moreover, habits are stylistically important. In a sense, habits are style. The unconsidered gesture, the repeated phrasing, the automatic selection, the characteristic reaction to subject matter and materials — these are the very things we refer to as style. Lots of people, artists included, consider this a virtue. Viewed closely, however, style is not a virtue, it is an inevitability — the inescapable result of doing anything more than a few times. The habitual gestures of the artist appear throughout any body of work developed enough to be called a body of work. Style is not an aspect of good work, it is an aspect of all work. Style is the natural consequence of habit.

艺术与科学

ART & SCIENCE

对于艺术家和科学家来说，这是一条信念，即他们的学科在某些深层次上有共同点。科学通过实验证明了这一点，而艺术始终凭直觉知道——自然的反复出现的形式具有与生俱来的正确性。科学并不是要证明抛物线、正弦曲线或圆周率的存在，但只要观察到现象，它们就在那里。艺术并不从数学上衡量笔触的结果，但每当创作艺术品时，就会出现原型形式。当查尔斯·伊姆斯 (Charles Eames) 被问及他是如何设计出他著名的模压胶合板椅子中使用的曲线时，他显然很困惑居然有人会问这样的问题；最后他只是耸耸肩回答道：“这是事情的本质。”有些事情，无论是被发现的还是发明的，都简单而确定地感觉是正确的。自然与美丽在最纯粹的状态下是无法区分的。你能改进 Circle 吗？

It is an article of faith, among artists and scientists alike, that at some deep level their disciplines share a common ground. What science bears witness to experimentally, art has always known intuitively — that there is an innate rightness to the recurring forms of nature. Science does not set out to prove the existence of parabolas or sine curves or pi, yet wherever phenomena are observed, there they are. Art does not weigh mathematically the outcome of the brushstroke, yet whenever artworks are made, archetypal forms appear. Charles Eames, when asked just how he arrived at the curves used in his famous molded plywood chair, was clearly baffled that anyone would ask such a question; finally he just shrugged and replied, “It’s in the nature of the thing.” Some things, regardless of whether they are discovered or invented, simply and assuredly feel right. What is natural and what is beautiful are, in their purest state, indistinguishable. Could you improve upon the Circle?

然而，在日常生活中，改进圆圈与改进轮子不同。科学的进步速度是技术提供了更高精度的工具，而艺术的进步速度是进化为思想提供了更大的洞察力——无论好坏，这一速度都极其缓慢。因此，虽然冰河时代以前的洞穴居民制作的石器以当前的技术标准来看是极其原始的，但他们的壁画仍然像任何现代艺术一样优雅和富有表现力。虽然有一百个文明在没有计算机、风车甚至轮子的情况下繁荣起来（有时长达几个世纪），但没有一个文明能够在没有艺术的情况下生存几代人。

In the day-to-day world, however, improving the circle is different from, say, improving the wheel. Science advances at the rate that technology provides tools of greater precision, while art advances at the pace that evolution provides minds with greater insight — a pace that is, for better or worse, glacially slow. Thus while the stone tools fashioned by cave dwellers an Ice Age ago are hopelessly primitive by current technological standards, their wall paintings remain as elegant and expressive as any modern art. And while a hundred civilizations have prospered (sometimes for centuries) without computers or windmills or even the wheel, none have survived even a few generations without art.

所有这一切并不是为了让艺术和科学陷入某种道德赛跑，而只是为了指出——在艺术和科学中——你得到的答案取决于你提出的问题。当科学家问什么方程最能描述空中岩石的轨迹时，艺术家则问扔一块石头会是什么感觉。

All that is not meant to cast art and science into some sort of moral footrace, but simply to point out that — in art as well as in science — the answers you get depend upon the questions you ask. Where the scientist asks what equation would best describe the trajectory of an airborne rock, the artist asks what it would feel like to throw one.

正如道格拉斯·霍夫施塔特（Douglas Hofstadter）指出的那样，“要记住的主要一点是，科学是关于事件的类别，而不是特定的实例。”艺术恰恰相反。艺术涉及任何一种特定的岩石，包括其受欢迎的变幻莫测、形状的特殊性、不均匀性和噪音。我们所经历的生活真相——以及艺术所表达的生活真相——包括随机和分散注意力的影响，这是其本质的重要组成部分。理论岩石属于科学范畴。特殊的岩石属于艺术范畴。

“The main thing to keep in mind,” as Douglas Hofstadter noted, “is that science is about classes of events, not particular instances.” Art is just the opposite. Art deals in any one particular rock, with its welcome vagaries, its peculiarities of shape, its unevenness, its noise. The truths of life as we experience them — and as art expresses them — include random and distracting influences as essential parts of their nature. Theoretical rocks are the province of science; particular rocks are the province of art.

科学的丰富性来自于真正聪明的人对精心控制的事件提出精确框架的问题——控制是指这种随机或分散注意力的影响不算数。如果科学家被问到是否可以重复某个实验并得到相同的结果，他必须说“可以”——否则就不是科学了。假设是，在科学实验结束时，研究人员和世界都没有改变，因此重复实验必然会产生相同的结果。事实上，任何正确进行实验的人都会得到相同的结果——这种情况有时会导致同一发现出现多种说法。

The richness of science comes from really smart people asking precisely framed questions about carefully controlled events — controlled in the sense that such random or distracting influences don’t count. The scientist, if asked whether a given experiment could be repeated with identical results, would have to say yes — or it wouldn’t be science. The presumption is that at the end of a scientific experiment neither the researcher nor the world have changed, and so repeating the experiment would necessarily re-produce the same result. Indeed, anyone performing the experiment correctly would get the same results — a circumstance that on occasion leads to multiple claims for the same discovery.

但是，如果艺术家被问到一件艺术作品是否可以重新制作并获得相同的结果，他将不得不回答“否”——否则它就不是艺术了。在创作一件艺术品时，艺术家和艺术家的世界都发生了变化，面对下一张空白的画布重新提出问题，总会得到不同的答案。这就产生了一定的悖论，因为虽然优秀的艺术带有真理之环——一种关于世界的永久重要的东西已经被阐明的感觉——但赋予这一真理以形式的行为可以说是一个人、一次的独特行为。 。对于每个艺术家来说，都有一个时刻可以找到一个特定的真理，如果当时没有找到，那就永远不会了。没有人能够写出《哈姆雷特》。这是一个很好的证据，证明世界的意义是创造出来的，而不是被发现的。当这些话被写下时，我们对世界的理解发生了变化，我们无法回去……就像莎士比亚一样。

But the artist, if asked whether an art piece could be remade with identical results, would have to answer no — or it wouldn’t be art. In making a piece of art, both the artist and the artist’s world are changed, and re-asking the question — facing the next blank canvas — will always yield a different answer. This creates a certain paradox, for while good art carries a ring of truth to it — a sense that something permanently important about the world has been made clear — the act of giving form to that truth is arguably unique to one person, and one time. There is a moment for each artist in which a particular truth can be found, and if it is not found then, it will not ever be. No one else will ever be in a position to write Hamlet. This is pretty good evidence that the meaning of the world is made, not found. Our understanding of the world changed when those words were written, and we can’t go back...any more than Shakespeare could.

这样改变的世界就变成了一个不同的世界，我们的改变也是其中的一部分。我们今天看到的世界是人们关注世界并以保留下来的形式评论世界的遗产。当然，很难想象在有人把马的形状画在洞壁上之前，马是没有形状的，但不难看出世界因此变得更大、更丰富、更复杂和更有意义。

The world thus altered becomes a different world, with our alterations being part of it. The world we see today is the legacy of people noticing the world and commenting on it in forms that have been preserved. Of course it’s difficult to imagine that horses had no shape before someone painted their shape on the cave walls, but it is not difficult to see the world became a subtly larger, richer, more complex and meaningful place as a result.

自参考

SELF-REFERENCE

自我参照、重复、戏仿、讽刺——艺术如果不是乱伦就什么都不是。见证埃舍尔的手绘图。二十世纪的艺术在很大程度上以自我参照为己任——关于绘画的绘画，关于写作的著作。此外，几乎每件艺术品都引用自己，通过节奏和重复呼唤自己的名字。音乐提供了最明显的例子——比如贝多芬仅围绕四个音符构建了他的第五交响曲的第一乐章——但所有媒体都有其对应的例子。

Self-reference, repetition, parody, satire — art is nothing if not incestuous. Witness Escher’s drawing of hands drawing hands. Twentieth century art has made self-reference pretty much its stock in trade — paintings about painting, writings about writing. Moreover, most every piece of art quotes itself, calling out its own name through rhythm and repetition. Music offers the clearest examples — like Beethoven building the first movement of his Fifth Symphony around just four notes — but all media have their equivalents.

当不引用自身时，艺术品常常向先于它们的艺术致敬：肖斯塔科维奇精湛的中提琴奏鸣曲（作品147）引用了贝多芬的月光奏鸣曲，将曲调包裹在自己周围，将注意力吸引到自身，将注意力吸引到其他事物。在不那么虔诚的层面上，这变成了讽刺和戏仿，就像伍迪·艾伦的《再玩一次，山姆》中那样。

When not quoting itself, artworks often pay homage to art that preceded them: Shostakovitch’s masterful viola sonata (Opus 147) quotes Beethoven’s Moonlight Sonata, wrapping the tune around itself, drawing attention to itself drawing attention to something else. At the less reverent level, this becomes satire and parody, as in Woody Allen’s Play It Again, Sam.

像（例如）应用油漆这样的操作不仅说明了其本身，还说明了所有其他应用的油漆。在你看过杰克逊·波洛克的作品之后，伦勃朗的作品看起来有所不同——颜料的使用更加刻意。当你自己涂上油漆后，它看起来更加不同。我们对过去的理解会因现在的经历而改变。

An operation like (for instance) applying paint says something not only about itself, but about all the other applied paint as well. Rembrandt’s work looks different — the paint more deliberately applied — after you’ve seen Jackson Pollock’s. It looks even more different after you’ve applied paint yourself. Our understanding of the past is altered by our experiences in the present.

将参考点转向内部，很明显，在某种程度上，所有艺术都是自传体的。毕竟，你的画笔只会根据你的手势绘制一个笔画，你的文字处理器只会根据你的击键敲出一个句子。正如田纳西·威廉姆斯所观察到的那样，即使是可论证的小说或幻想作品在情感上仍然是自传性的。约翰·萨科夫斯基（John Szarkowski）曾在现代艺术博物馆策划了一场题为“镜子与窗户”的展览。他的前提是，一些艺术家看待世界就像透过窗户看“外面”发生的事情，而另一些艺术家看待世界就像在镜子里看自己内心的世界。无论哪种方式，自传的优势都是隐含的。

Turning the reference point inward, it’s apparent that at some level, all art is autobiographical. After all, your brush only paints a stroke in response to your gesture, your word processor only taps out a sentence in response to your keystrokes. As Tennessee Williams observed, even works of demonstrable fiction or fantasy remain emotionally autobiographical. John Szarkowski once curated a show at the Museum of Modern Art titled Mirrors and Windows. His premise was that some artists view the world as if looking through a window at things happening “out there”, while others view the world as if looking in a mirror at a world inside themselves. Either way, the autobiographical vantage point is implicit.

如果艺术是关于自我的，那么广泛接受的推论就是艺术创作就是关于自我表达。确实如此——但这并不一定是全部。验证“你是谁”的感觉被认为是艺术创作的主要需求，这可能只是我们这个时代的一个短暂特征。这种解释中迷失的是一种古老的观念，即艺术是你在世界上所做的事情，或者你对世界所做的事情，甚至是你为世界所做的事情。创作艺术的需要可能不仅仅源于表达自己的需要，还源于与自己之外的事物建立联系的需要。作为一名艺术创造者，你是比自我更重要的问题的守护者。

If art is about self, the widely accepted corollary is that making art is about self-expression. And it is — but that is not necessarily all it is. It may only be a passing feature of our times that validating the sense of who-you-are is held up as the major source of the need to make art. What gets lost in that interpretation is an older sense that art is something you do out in the world, or something you do about the world, or even something you do for the world. The need to make art may not stem solely from the need to express who you are, but from a need to complete a relationship with something outside yourself. As a maker of art you are custodian of issues larger than self.

有些人创作艺术是出于灵感，有些人是出于挑衅，还有一些人是出于绝望。艺术创作可以让人们进入可能是危险的、神圣的、禁忌的、诱人的或以上所有的世界。它可以让你进入那些你可能永远无法完全参与的世界。事实上，您寻求的可能是参与感，而不是艺术。不同之处在于，创作艺术可以让你、甚至保证你表达自己。艺术是接触，你的作品必然揭示这种接触的本质。在创作艺术时，你要声明什么是重要的。

Some people who make art are driven by inspiration, others by provocation, still others by desperation. Artmaking grants access to worlds that may be dangerous, sacred, forbidden, seductive, or all of the above. It grants access to worlds you may otherwise never fully engage. It may in fact be the engagement — not the art — that you seek. The difference is that making art allows, indeed guarantees, that you declare yourself. Art is contact, and your work necessarily reveals the nature of that contact. In making art you declare what is important.

隐喻
METAPHOR
当你开始远行时，树是树，水是水，山是山。走了一段距离，树不再是树，水不再是水，山不再是山。但走了很远之后，树又是树，水又是水，山又是山。
When you start on a long journey, trees are trees, water is water, and mountains are mountains. After you have gone some distance, trees are no longer trees, water no longer water, mountains no longer mountains. But after you have travelled a great distance, trees are once again trees, water is once again water, mountains are once again mountains.
——禅宗教学
— Zen teaching

艺术创作取决于对事物的关注——关于你自己、你的方法、你的主题的事情。例如，每个视觉艺术家迟早都会注意到线条与图片边缘的关系。在那一刻之前，这种关系并不存在；之后就无法想象它不存在。从那一刻起，每一条新的线条都与画面的边缘来回对话。那些还没有实现这一小飞跃的人所看到的景象与那些已经实现这一小飞跃的人所看到的景象并不一样——事实上，从概念上来说，他们甚至并不生活在同一个世界。

Making art depends upon noticing things — things about yourself, your methods, your subject matter. Sooner or later, for instance, every visual artist notices the relationship of the line to the picture’s edge. Before that moment the relationship does not exist; afterwards it’s impossible to imagine it not existing. And from that moment on every new line talks back and forth with the picture’s edge. People who have not yet made this small leap do not see the same picture as those who have — in fact, conceptually speaking, they do not even live in the same world.

你的工作是无数此类关系的源泉。反过来，这些关系是艺术丰富性和复杂性的主要来源。随着你的艺术发展，概念关系越来越多地定义你所看到的世界的形状和结构。随着时间的推移，他们就是整个世界。你、你的工作和世界之间的区别逐渐缩小，变得透明，最后消失。随着时间的推移，树木再次成为树木。

Your work is the source for an uncountably large number of such relationships. And these relationships, in turn, are a primary source of the richness and complexity in your art. As your art develops, conceptual relationships increasingly define the shape and structure of the world you see. In time, they are the world. Distinctions between you, your work and the world lessen, grow transparent, and finally disappear. In time, trees are once again trees.

从多年的时间来看，一个人的艺术变化常常揭示出一种奇怪的模式，在长时间的安静改进和偶尔的失控变化之间不规则地摇摆。 （虽然这超出了我们的目的，但我们忍不住注意到这种模式与数学中混沌理论的表现之间的诱人相似性。）有时，我们对世界的感知会平稳而连续地从一种状态流向另一种状态，并且有时它会意外地（且不可逆转地）翻转为完全不同的配置。作为学生，我们会记住那些著名的例子——比如牛顿的苹果给他带来了万有引力定律——但总是要警告说，这样的事件很少见，甚至可能过于罕见。毕竟，有多少人有机会重写基本的物理定律？

Viewed over a span of years, changes in one’s art often reveal a curious pattern, swinging irregularly between long periods of quiet refinement, and occasional leaps of runaway change. (And though it’s beyond our purposes here, we can’t help but note the tantalizing similarity between this pattern and the manifestations of chaos theory in mathematics.) Sometimes our perception of the world flows smoothly and continuously from one state to the next, and sometimes it flips over unexpectedly (and irrevocably) into a different configuration entirely. As schoolkids we memorize the famous examples — like Newton’s apple delivering him the Law of Gravity — but always with the caveat that such events are rare, probably excessively rare. After all, how often does anyone get the chance to rewrite the underlying laws of physics?

然而，事实证明，我们所有人（时不时）都会经历这种概念跳跃，虽然我们的跳跃可能不会影响行星的轨道，但它们会显着影响我们与周围世界互动的方式。例如，学习法语，您可能会花第一个月的时间费力地将其逐字翻译成英语，以使其易于理解。然后有一天——瞧！ ——你发现自己在阅读法语时无需翻译，而以前神秘的过程已经变得自动化。或者和真正了解蘑菇的人一起去采蘑菇，你首先会忍受一些彻头彻尾的羞辱，专家找到了所有蘑菇，而你却什么也没找到。但在某个时刻，世界发生了变化，树林神奇地充满了——到处都是蘑菇！ ——以前不透明的视图变得透明了。

Yet it’s demonstrably true that all of us do (from time to time) experience such conceptual jumps, and while ours may not affect the orbit of planets, they markedly affect the way we engage the world around us. Study French, for instance, and you’ll likely spend the first month painstakingly translating it word by word into English to make it understandable. Then one day — voilá! — you find yourself reading French without translating it, and a process that was previously enigmatic has become automatic. Or go mushroom hunting with someone who really knows mushrooms, and you’ll first endure some downright humiliating outings in which the expert finds all the mushrooms and you find none. But then at some point the world shifts, the woods magically fill — mushrooms everywhere! — and a view that was previously opaque has become transparent.

对于艺术家来说，这种闪电般的转变是变化的核心机制。它们产生了最纯粹的隐喻形式：不同的事物之间建立了联系，一种事物的含义丰富了另一种事物的含义，并且不同的事物变得不可分割。跳跃之前有光和影。之后，物体漂浮在光影与它们定义的物体无法区分的空间中。

For the artist, such lightning shifts are a central mechanism of change. They generate the purest form of metaphor: connections are made between unlike things, meanings from one enrich the meanings of the other, and the unlike things become inseparable. Before the leap there was light and shadow. Afterwards, objects float in a space where light and shadow are indistinguishable from the object they define.

最近，一位颇有成就的画家（但和我们其他人一样缺乏安全感）正在与一位朋友喝咖啡时讨论他前一天晚上的梦。这是那些生动的彩色梦境之一，即使在醒来之后，细节仍然挥之不去。在梦中，他发现自己身处一家美术馆，当他走进去环顾四周时，他发现墙上挂满了画作——令人惊叹的画作，充满激情的强度和令人难以忘怀的美丽的画作。在讲述他的梦想时，这位艺术家热情地结束道：“只要能够创作出这样的画作，我愿意付出一切！”

Recently a painter of some accomplishment (but as insecure as the rest of us) was discussing his previous night’s dream with a friend over coffee. It was one of those vivid technicolor dreams, the kind that linger on in exact detail even after waking. In his dream he found himself at an art gallery, and when he walked inside and looked around he found the walls hung with paintings — amazing paintings, paintings of passionate intensity and haunting beauty. Recounting his dream, the artist ended fervently with, “I’d give anything to be able to make paintings like that!”

“等一下！”他的朋友惊呼道。 “你没看到吗？那是你的画！它们来自你自己的想法。还有谁可以画它们？”

“Wait a minute!” his friend exclaimed. “Don’t you see? Those were your paintings! They came from your own mind. Who else could have painted them?”

确实还有谁？

Who else indeed?

当然你可以否认你的梦想，但结果将是一致的沉闷。坚持认为世界必须始终保持x，而x确实正是您将得到的。但这就是世界的全部。你所有的艺术都将永远存在。当你唯一的工具是锤子时，俗话说，一切看起来都像钉子。想象力和执行力在可能的行为中找到了合理的共同点：可绘画的图画、可跳舞的步骤、可演奏的音符。作为艺术家，你的成长是朝着完全可实现的作品的成长——在充分阐明你所知道的一切的情况下，作品变得真实。包括你所知道的关于你自己的一切。

Of course you can deny your dreams, but the result will be uniformly dreary. Insist that the world must always remain x, and x is indeed exactly what you’ll get. But that’s all the world will ever be. And all your art will ever be. When your only tool is a hammer, so the saying goes, everything looks like a nail. Imagination and execution take their rightful common ground in possible acts: paintable pictures, danceable steps, playable notes. Your growth as the artist is a growth toward fully realizable works — works that become real in full illumination of all that you know. Including all you know about yourself.

人的声音

THE HUMAN VOICE

计算机毫无用处——它们只能给你答案。
Computers are useless — all they can give you are answers.

- 巴勃罗毕加索

— Pablo Picasso

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

在本书的大部分内容中，我们都试图通过研究这些困难在工作室中实际发生的方式来应对艺术创作的困难。这是一个简单的前提：遵循与工作本身接触所产生的线索，你的技术、情感和智力路径就会变得清晰。到目前为止，我们很容易尝试通过将所有这些线索解析为一个清晰、简洁、基本、精心打磨的答案来结束这个想法。很诱人，但无济于事。答案令人放心，但当您遇到真正有用的事情时，它可能会采取问题的形式。

THROUGHOUT MUCH OF THIS BOOK we’ve tried to confront the difficulties of making art by examining the way those difficulties really happen in the studio. It’s a simple premise: follow the leads that arise from contact with the work itself, and your technical, emotional and intellectual pathway becomes clear. Having come this far, it’s tempting to try to bring this idea to closure by resolving all those leads into a single clear, concise, fundamental, finely honed answer. Tempting, but futile. Answers are reassuring, but when you’re onto something really useful, it will probably take the form of a question.

问题

QUESTIONS

从长远来看，提出有趣问题的人才能得到有趣的答案。有时（而且可能比我们意识到的要频繁得多），真正重要的问题在我们采取行动之前会在我们的脑海中盘旋很长时间。事实上，有时它们会在那里待很长时间，直到我们意识到它们的重要性。这个问题可能是本书的雏形，是近二十年前向作者提出的。这次活动是围绕组建一个小型艺术家团体进行的友好辩论。问题是：艺术家之间有什么共同点吗？

Over the long run, the people with the interesting answers are those who ask the interesting questions. Sometimes (and probably far more often than we realize), the really important questions roll around in our minds for a long time before we act upon them. Sometimes, in fact, they sit there for a long time before we even realize they’re important. The question that probably served as the seed crystal for this book was posed to the authors nearly twenty years earlier. The occasion was a friendly debate surrounding the formation of a small artists’ collective. The question was: Do artists have anything in common with each other?

就像任何好问题一样，这个问题很快引起了一大群亲戚的关注：艺术家如何成为艺术家？艺术家如何学习创作他们的作品？怎样才能做出让自己满意的工作呢？对于充满活力和理想主义的年轻艺术家来说，答案似乎指日可待。只是随着时间的流逝，我们才开始越来越频繁地遇到一个更加黑暗的问题：为什么这么多开始的人又退出了？

Like any good question, that one quickly generated a flurry of relatives: How do artists become artists? How do artists learn to work on their work? How can I make work that will satisfy me? For young artists filled with energy and idealism, the answers seemed just around the corner. Only as the years passed did we begin to encounter, with increasing frequency, a much darker issue: Why do so many who start, quit?

总的来说，这组问题标志着《艺术与恐惧》的中心支点。这是一个奇怪的群体——也许不够神秘，不足以引起学者的兴趣，但又太难以捉摸，无法吸引流行心理学家。也许这样也好。在我们生活的世界中，对艺术创作的现成观察通常毫无用处，而且常常是宿命论。

Taken together, this cluster of questions marks the central pivot of Art & Fear. It’s an odd cluster — not arcane enough, perhaps, to interest scholars, but too elusive to attract pop psychologists. Perhaps that’s just as well. We live in a world where the ready-made observations about artmaking are typically useless, frequently fatalistic.

问：有人能与莫扎特的天才相媲美吗？
Q: Will anyone ever match the genius of Mozart?
答：没有。
A: No.
谢谢——现在我们可以继续我们的工作了吗？
Thank you — now can we get on with our work?

同样，没有现成的词汇来描述艺术家如何成为艺术家，也没有认识到艺术家必须学会成为他们自己（即使他们无法控制自己成为他们自己）。我们有一种语言，反映了我们如何学习绘画，但不是我们如何学习绘画。你如何描述当工艺膨胀为艺术时[读者在此放置文字]所发生的变化？

Equally, there is no ready vocabulary to describe the ways in which artists become artists, no recognition that artists must learn to be who they are (even as they cannot help being who they are.) We have a language that reflects how we learn to paint, but not how we learn to paint our paintings. How do you describe the [reader to place words here] that changes when craft swells into art?

艺术家们聚集在一起，清楚地知道，当一切尘埃落定后，他们将回到自己的工作室，独自实践他们的艺术。时期。这个简单的真理可能是我们最深厚的纽带。彩绘野牛和象牙雕刻印章所传达的跨越时间的信息并不是在说明艺术创作者与我们自己之间的差异，而是在说明它们的相似之处。如今，这些相似之处隐藏在城市复杂性之下——观众、评论家、经济、琐事——在一个自我意识的世界里。只有当我们真正致力于自己的作品时，我们才能恢复与所有艺术创作者之间的基本联系。其余的可能是必要的，但这不是艺术。你的工作就是在你的生活和你的艺术之间划出一条笔直而清晰的界限。

Artists come together in the clear knowledge that when all is said and done, they will return to their studio and practice their art alone. Period. That simple truth may be the deepest bond we share. The message across time from the painted bison and the carved ivory seal speaks not of the differences between the makers of that art and ourselves, but the similarities. Today those similarities lay hidden beneath urban complexity — audience, critics, economics, trivia — in a self-conscious world. Only in those moments when we are truly working on our own work do we recover the fundamental connection we share with all makers of art. The rest may be necessary, but it’s not art. Your job is to draw a line from your life to your art that is straight and clear.

常数

CONSTANTS

在很大程度上，外部世界由变量组成，而内部世界由常数组成。常数是不变的：除非精神崩溃或罕见的热带热病，否则明天和明年你将承受与今天相同的负担。作为艺术家，我们体验生活的方式与我们以任何其他角色体验生活的方式没有什么不同——我们只是存在，也许从我们眼睛后面的一个假想点观看，而我们从那个稳定的有利位置观察到的场景不断变化。

To a remarkable degree the outside world consists of variables and the interior world consists of constants. The constants are, well, constant: barring mental breakdown or a rare tropical fever, you’ll carry the same burdens tomorrow and next year as you do today. We experience life as artists no differently from the way we experience life in any other role — we simply exist, perhaps watching from an imaginary point a little behind our eyes, while the scene we observe from that steady vantage point changes constantly.

这种内心稳定的感觉与一个广泛观察到的事实是一致的：任何个体生命的弧线在很长一段时间内都是一致的。吸引我们的主题将继续吸引我们。我们回应的模式将继续回应。我们被一些力量所驱使，这些力量就像洋流一样，如此微妙和普遍，我们认为它们完全是理所当然的。当我们注意到我们游泳的大海时，那些奇怪的时刻让我们感到惊讶，就像莫里哀的角色发现他在说散文一样，事实上他一直在说散文。

This sense of interior stability is consistent with one widely observable truth: the arc to any individual life is uniform over long periods of time. Subjects that draw us in will continue to draw us in. Patterns we respond to we will continue to respond to. We are compelled by forces that, like the ocean current, are so subtle and pervasive we take them utterly for granted. Those odd moments when we notice the sea we swim in leave us as surprised as the discovery by Moliere’s character that he was speaking prose, that indeed he had always spoken prose.

室内问题持续存在的艺术证据随处可见。它展示了大多数艺术家一次又一次地回到相同的两三个故事的方式。它展示了梵高的调色板、海明威的人物、您最喜欢的作曲家的编曲。我们讲述我们必须讲述的故事，那些吸引我们的事物的故事——为什么我们每个人都应该拥有不止这些故事呢？唯一真正值得做的工作——唯一你能令人信服地做的工作——就是专注于你关心的事情的工作。不关注这些问题就等于否认生活中的常态。

The artistic evidence for the constancy of interior issues is everywhere. It shows in the way most artists return to the same two or three stories again and again. It shows in the palette of Van Gogh, the characters of Hemingway, the orchestration of your favorite composer. We tell the stories we have to tell, stories of the things that draw us in — and why should any of us have more than a handful of those? The only work really worth doing — the only work you can do convincingly — is the work that focuses on the things you care about. To not focus on those issues is to deny the constants in your life.

人性之声

VOX HUMANA

创作艺术就是用人声歌唱。为此，您必须首先了解您唯一需要的声音就是您已经拥有的声音。艺术工作是普通的工作，但拥抱这项工作需要勇气，需要智慧来调解艺术与恐惧的相互作用。有时，为了看到你的作品应有的地位，你必须走到悬崖边，寻找深深的裂缝。你必须看到宇宙并不是无形的和黑暗的，而只是等待着你自己心灵的启示之光。你的艺术并不是从黑暗中奇迹般地诞生，而是在光明中平安地创作出来。

To make art is to sing with the human voice. To do this you must first learn that the only voice you need is the voice you already have. Art work is ordinary work, but it takes courage to embrace that work, and wisdom to mediate the interplay of art & fear. Sometimes to see your work’s rightful place you have to walk to the edge of the precipice and search the deep chasms. You have to see that the universe is not formless and dark throughout, but awaits simply the revealing light of your own mind. Your art does not arrive miraculously from the darkness, but is made uneventfully in the light.

资深艺术家对彼此的了解是，他们都致力于解决对他们来说重要的问题。资深艺术家的共同点是他们都学会了如何继续他们的工作。简而言之，艺术家要么学习如何继续，要么不学习。任何艺术家找到的个人秘诀都只属于该艺术家——它是不可转让的，对其他人也没什么用处。它不会帮助你确切地知道梵高为了继续他的工作需要获得或失去什么。值得承认的是，梵高需要得到或失去，他的作品与你的作品一样不可避免，而且他——像你一样——只能依靠自己。

What veteran artists know about each other is that they have engaged the issues that matter to them. What veteran artists share in common is that they have learned how to get on with their work. Simply put, artists learn how to proceed, or they don’t. The individual recipe any artist finds for proceeding belongs to that artist alone — it’s non-transferable and of little use to others. It won’t help you to know exactly what Van Gogh needed to gain or lose in order to get on with his work. What is worth recognizing is that Van Gogh needed to gain or lose at all, that his work was no more or less inevitable than yours, and that he — like you — had only himself to fall back on.

今天，与许多年前相比，艺术更加困难，因为你必须始终如一地追求它。在很多不同的方面。对于如此少的外部奖励。艺术家只有通过与自己和平相处，而且与一系列广泛的问题和平相处，才能成为资深艺术家。你必须一直重新找到你的工作，要做到这一点，你必须在许多方面给自己留出回旋余地——精神上、身体上、时间上。体验包括能够轻松、即时地重新占用有用空间。

Today, more than it was however many years ago, art is hard because you have to keep after it so consistently. On so many different fronts. For so little external reward. Artists become veteran artists only by making peace not just with themselves, but with a huge range of issues. You have to find your work all over again all the time, and to do that you have to give yourself maneuvering room on many fronts — mental, physical, temporal. Experience consists of being able to reoccupy useful space easily, instantly.

最后，一切都归结为这一点：你可以选择（或者更准确地说，是一个滚动的选择），要么全力以赴地工作，要么冒着工作不让你开心的风险，要么不全力以赴——并且从而保证它不会让你快乐。它变成了确定性和不确定性之间的选择。奇怪的是，不确定性是令人欣慰的选择。

In the end it all comes down to this: you have a choice (or more accurately a rolling tangle of choices) between giving your work your best shot and risking that it will not make you happy, or not giving it your best shot — and thereby guaranteeing that it will not make you happy. It becomes a choice between certainty and uncertainty. And curiously, uncertainty is the comforting choice.

关于本书

ABOUT THIS BOOK

显然（因为你正在阅读它）这本书确实完成了，尽管描述它是如何完成的是一个困难的命题。字面上的答案可能是缓慢的，因为这些话标志着对这份手稿的七年或多或少的连续工作的终点。然而，从我们的角度来看，这似乎是一个完全自然的步伐。我们已经是多年的朋友了，我们进行了一次真正愉快的合作，其中写作成为了澄清我们在友好对话中经常遇到的问题的工具。

Evidently (since you’re reading it) this book did get done, though describing just how it got done is a difficult proposition. The literal answer would probably be slowly, given that these words mark the end-point to seven years of more-or-less continuous work on this manuscript. Viewed from our perspective, however, this seems an entirely natural pace. Having already been friends for a whole bunch of years allowed for a genuinely enjoyable collaboration, one in which writing became a tool for clarifying issues we had often grappled with in friendly conversation.

有时（当事情进展非常缓慢时），我们会尝试以人们想象的合作者的工作方式来推动手稿的进展：同意时间表、选择要处理的主题，甚至在录音机面前聚在一起以保存转瞬即逝的内容。长时间谈话的想法。就像许多其他完美的理论一样，这个理论并没有起作用。最终，工作按照此类事情通常完成的方式完成了——为该项目留出单独的时间，一次一个句子、一次一个想法。

Occasionally (when things were really slow) we tried to nudge the manuscript along by working in ways that one imagines collaborators working: agreeing to schedules, selecting topics to work on, or even meeting together in the presence of a tape recorder to preserve the fleeting ideas of long conversations. Like many other perfectly good theories, that one didn’t work. In the end the work got done the way such things always get done — by carving out solo time for the project and nibbling away at it one sentence at a time, one idea at a time.

与大多数项目一样，这个项目也成功地（大量）阐明了艺术创作中常见的危险。尽管我们有着长期的友谊，尽管我们就这里讨论的问题进行了持续的对话，但事实证明，我们的优势比相似的更互补，导致我们的角色永远无法逆转（事实上，我们甚至从未进行过谈判）。经过一番摸索之后，我们通过简单地让自己足够好而确定了正确的合作模式——并行而不是串联工作，我们每个人都参与我们感兴趣的问题。然而，由于艺术家很少讨论这个话题，我们真的不知道我们的视觉、盲目性和愿意以另一种方式看待的大量（但不完全匹配）组合与其他合作努力有多相似。

Like most projects, this one also managed to illuminate (in abundance) the familiar perils of artmaking. Despite our long friendship and despite ongoing conversations about the issues addressed here, our strengths proved to be more complementary than similar, resulting in roles that could never be reversed (and in fact were never even negotiated). We settled into the right pattern of collaboration, after a little fumbling, by simply letting well enough alone — working in parallel rather than in tandem, with each of us engaging the issues we were drawn to. Since artists rarely discuss this topic, however, we really don’t know how closely our large (but not entirely matching) mix of vision, blindness, and willingness to look the other way resembles other collaborative efforts.

在整个项目中，我们得到了许多朋友和旅伴的帮助——他们中的大多数人可能没有意识到他们的贡献。斯宾塞·贝勒斯 (Spencer Bayles)、弗朗西斯·奥兰 (Frances Orland)、史蒂夫·斯特吉斯 (Steve Sturgis)、琳达·琼斯 (Linda Jones) 和基思·米尔曼 (Keith Milman) 也在早期提供了知情帮助，我们对此深表感谢。我们要特别感谢戴夫·博恩（Dave Bohn），他以充沛的力量和精确度提出重大问题（和基本细节），不断挑战我们的思维。

We have been helped throughout this project by many friends and fellow travellers — most of whom are probably unaware of their contribution. There was also knowing help early on from Spencer Bayles, Frances Orland, Steve Sturgis, Linda Jones and Keith Milman, which we greatly appreciated. We would especially like to thank Dave Bohn, who consistently challenged our thinking by raising large questions (and essential details) with abundant force and precision.

最后，我们非常感谢卡普拉出版社的诺埃尔·杨（Noel Young）慷慨地接受这本书出版，尽管我们谁也搞不清这本书属于书店的哪个书架，还要感谢他的助手大卫·达尔（David Dahl）在本书中表现出的无尽的耐心和善意。在接下来的几年里回答我们的许多问题和要求。直到 2001 年卡普拉出版社永久关门之后，我们才开始以自己的品牌 Image Continuum Press出版《艺术与恐惧》 。
